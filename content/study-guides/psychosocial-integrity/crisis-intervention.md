---

### Types of Crises

#### 1. Maturational (Developmental) Crisis

**Definition**: Normal life transitions that cause stress

**Examples**:
- Starting school
- Puberty, adolescence
- Leaving home
- Marriage
- Becoming a parent
- Midlife transitions
- Retirement
- Aging, physical decline

**Nursing Focus**: These are expected; help patient develop coping skills for life transition

---

#### 2. Situational Crisis

**Definition**: Unexpected, external events

**Examples**:
- Death of loved one
- Divorce, separation
- Job loss, financial crisis
- Serious illness/injury
- Natural disaster
- Assault, violence
- Accident
- Unwanted pregnancy

**Nursing Focus**: Unpredictable; requires immediate support and mobilization of resources

---

#### 3. Adventitious Crisis

**Definition**: Major disasters affecting many people simultaneously

**Examples**:
- Terrorist attacks
- Natural disasters (earthquake, hurricane, tsunami)
- Pandemic
- War
- Mass shootings
- Major accidents (plane crash, building collapse)

**Nursing Focus**: Community-wide response needed; may cause PTSD; long-term recovery

## ğŸ¯ Crisis Intervention

### Goals of Crisis Intervention

```
PRIMARY GOALS:
  1. Ensure safety (prevent harm to self/others)
  2. Provide support
  3. Return to pre-crisis level of functioning
  4. Develop new coping skills

SECONDARY GOALS:
  5. Prevent deterioration
  6. Facilitate growth through crisis
  7. Connect with ongoing support/resources
```

---

### Principles of Crisis Intervention

**Time-Limited**: Brief, focused (usually 1-6 weeks)

**Here and Now**: Focus on immediate problem, not past

**Goal-Directed**: Specific, achievable goals

**Active Therapist Role**: Directive, supportive approach

**Support System**: Involve family, friends, community resources

### Suicide Risk Factors

**High-Risk Factors** - Mnemonic: **SAD PERSONS**

```
S = SEX (Male - complete suicide more often)
    (Female - attempt more often)

A = AGE (<19, >45; highest in elderly white males)

D = DEPRESSION (or other mental illness)

P = PREVIOUS ATTEMPT (Strongest predictor!)

E = ETHANOL/SUBSTANCE ABUSE

R = RATIONAL THINKING LOSS (psychosis, cognitive impairment)

S = SOCIAL SUPPORT LACKING (isolated, alone)

O = ORGANIZED PLAN (specific + lethal = higher risk)

N = NO SPOUSE (single, divorced, widowed)

S = SICKNESS (chronic illness, pain, terminal)
```

---

### Additional Risk Factors

**Psychiatric**:
- Depression, bipolar disorder
- Schizophrenia
- PTSD, anxiety disorders
- Personality disorders (especially borderline)
- Eating disorders
- Recent psychiatric hospitalization

**Psychological**:
- Hopelessness (stronger predictor than depression)
- Impulsivity
- Aggression, hostility
- Recent significant loss
- Trauma, abuse history

**Social**:
- Recent stressful life event
- Access to lethal means
- Family history of suicide
- Exposure to suicide (contagion)
- LGBTQ+ (higher risk due to discrimination, bullying)
- Bullying victim
- Financial problems, unemployment

**Medical**:
- Chronic pain
- Terminal illness
- Recent diagnosis of serious illness
- Traumatic brain injury

## ğŸ”ª Self-Harm vs. Suicide

### Non-Suicidal Self-Injury (NSSI)

**Definition**: Deliberate self-inflicted harm WITHOUT intent to die

**Common Methods**:
- Cutting (most common)
- Burning
- Hitting/punching self or objects
- Head-banging
- Picking at wounds

**Reasons** (NOT suicide attempt):
- Emotional regulation (release emotional pain)
- Self-punishment
- Feel something (combat numbness)
- Distraction from worse pain
- Control over something
- Communicate distress

**Key Difference from Suicide**:
- **NSSI**: Intent is to relieve emotional pain, not die
- **Suicide attempt**: Intent is to end life

### Scenario 3: Crisis Intervention

**Patient**: College student overwhelmed after failing exam, broke up with partner, and parent diagnosed with cancer - all in one week. States "I can't handle this. Everything is falling apart."

**Crisis Type**: Situational (multiple stressors)

- Assess suicide risk: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" (No)
- Assess functioning: "How are you coping day-to-day?"

- **Emergency**: Crisis hotline number if feelings worsen

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A depressed patient who has been withdrawn suddenly appears happy, gives away belongings, and says "Everything will be better soon." What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Assess for suicidal ideation and implement suicide precautions.

**Rationale**: Sudden mood improvement in a depressed patient is a RED FLAG for suicide risk. The patient may have decided on a suicide plan, which provides relief (explains improved mood). Giving away belongings and "everything will be better" are warning signs. This is a psychiatric emergency requiring immediate assessment and safety measures.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient states "I'm thinking about suicide but I would never do it because of my religious beliefs." What risk level is this?</summary>

**Answer**: Low to moderate risk. Continue assessment and monitoring.

**Rationale**: Patient has suicidal ideation (a concern) but strong protective factor (religious beliefs). Not imminent danger, but needs ongoing assessment, mental health referral, safety planning, and monitoring. Don't dismiss ideation, but protective factor reduces immediate risk.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Which question is MOST important when assessing suicide risk?</summary>

**Answer**: "Have you ever attempted suicide before?"

**Rationale**: Previous suicide attempt is the STRONGEST predictor of future attempt. This is the most critical piece of history. While plan, means, and current ideation are important, past attempt is the highest risk factor.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient says "I'm going to kill myself." What is the nurse's FIRST priority?</summary>

**Answer**: Ensure patient safety by staying with patient and implementing suicide precautions.

**Rationale**: SAFETY is always the first priority. Never leave a suicidal patient alone. Immediate actions: stay with patient, call for help, remove harmful objects, implement observation protocol. Assessment and planning continue, but safety interventions start immediately.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: What is the primary goal of crisis intervention?</summary>

**Answer**: Return the patient to pre-crisis level of functioning.

**Rationale**: The main goal is to help the person return to their baseline functioning as quickly as possible. Crisis intervention is short-term and focused on immediate problem resolution, not long-term therapy or personality change.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: A patient admitted for suicidal ideation says "I'm fine now. I want to go home." What should the nurse consider?</summary>

**Answer**: Patient may be planning to attempt suicide at home. Continue safety precautions and notify physician.

**Rationale**: Suicidal patients may claim to be "fine" to get discharged so they can attempt suicide. This requires careful assessment, not immediate discharge. Physician must evaluate. Patient safety is priority over patient's stated wishes to leave.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: Which patient is at HIGHEST risk for suicide?</summary>

**Answer**: 75-year-old white male, widowed, with terminal cancer and history of depression.

**Rationale**: Multiple high-risk factors: elderly, male, white, recent loss (spouse), chronic/terminal illness, mental illness history. Elderly white males have the highest suicide completion rate. This combination of factors creates very high risk.
</details>
