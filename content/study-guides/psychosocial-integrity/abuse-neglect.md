### Reporting Process

**Steps**:
1. **Recognize** indicators of abuse
2. **Document** objective findings (no opinions)
3. **Report** to supervisor and appropriate agency
4. **Provide** information to investigating agency
5. **Support** victim with resources and safety planning

**Protection for Reporter**:
- Good faith reports protected by law
- Cannot be sued for reporting
- Anonymous reporting available in some cases
- Employer cannot retaliate

**Consequences of NOT Reporting**:
- Criminal charges possible
- Civil liability
- Nursing license discipline
- Termination from employment

### Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma)

**Mechanism**: Violent shaking of infant/young child

**Classic Triad**:
1. Subdural hematoma (bleeding in brain)
2. Retinal hemorrhages
3. Brain swelling/injury

**Presentation**:
- Altered level of consciousness
- Seizures
- Vomiting
- Lethargy, irritability
- Respiratory distress
- May have no external signs of trauma

**Outcomes**: Often fatal or causes permanent brain damage, blindness, cerebral palsy

**NCLEX Tip**: Shaken baby syndrome should be suspected in infant with subdural hematoma and retinal hemorrhages, especially with no history of trauma.

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A nurse suspects a 4-year-old is being abused. What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Report suspicions to Child Protective Services.

**Rationale**: Nurses are mandatory reporters. Must report ANY suspicion of child abuse - don't need proof. This is a legal and ethical obligation. Failure to report can result in criminal charges and license discipline. Document objectively and report immediately.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: Which injury pattern is MOST suspicious for child abuse?</summary>

**Answer**: Bruises in various stages of healing on the back, buttocks, and inner thighs.

**Rationale**: Multiple bruises of different colors indicate injuries at different times. Location on back, buttocks, inner thighs is unusual for accidental injury (typical accidents: shins, knees, elbows, forehead). This pattern is highly suspicious for intentional abuse.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A woman with multiple bruises tells the nurse she "walked into a door." What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Document her statement and the injuries objectively, screen for intimate partner violence when alone.

**Rationale**: "Walking into door" is common excuse for IPV. Nurse should screen all women for IPV in private setting. Document exact statement in quotes. Provide resources even if patient denies. Don't argue with explanation but document concerns objectively.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: What is the MOST important action when interviewing a child about possible abuse?</summary>

**Answer**: Interview the child alone, without the parents present.

**Rationale**: Child will not disclose abuse with abuser present. Privacy is essential. Use age-appropriate language, don't lead or interrogate. Document child's exact words in quotes. Let forensic interviewer do detailed questioning.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: An elderly patient with dementia has multiple pressure ulcers, is malnourished, and has poor hygiene. Caregiver says patient refuses care. What should the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Elder neglect - report to Adult Protective Services.

**Rationale**: This represents neglect even if caregiver claims patient refuses. Caregiver has responsibility to provide care or arrange for alternative care. Multiple pressure ulcers, malnutrition, poor hygiene indicate failure to meet basic needs. Report to APS for investigation.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: What is the MOST dangerous time for a victim of intimate partner violence?</summary>

**Answer**: When leaving or attempting to leave the relationship.

**Rationale**: Homicide risk increases 70% when victim leaves. Abuser's need for control is threatened. This is why victims often return - genuine fear for safety. Safety planning and resources are crucial before leaving.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: What should the nurse document when abuse is suspected?</summary>

**Answer**: Objective findings, exact location and description of injuries, patient's statements in quotes.

**Rationale**: Documentation must be factual and objective. Describe injuries precisely (size, shape, color, location). Use quotes for patient's statements. Avoid opinions, accusations, or labels. This documentation may be used in legal proceedings.
</details>
