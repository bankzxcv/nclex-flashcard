### Bowlby: Four Phases of Grief

Developed for childhood grief, applicable to adults:

| Phase | Description | Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **1. Numbness** | Shock, disbelief, emotional outbursts | Hours to 1 week |
| **2. Yearning & Searching** | Preoccupation with deceased, searching behaviors | Months to years |
| **3. Disorganization & Despair** | Depression, apathy, difficulty functioning | Variable |
| **4. Reorganization** | New identity emerges, life goes on | Variable |

## ğŸ•Šï¸ Types of Grief

### Normal (Uncomplicated) Grief

**Characteristics**:
- Follows expected pattern
- Progresses over time
- Gradual return to functioning
- Able to find meaning
- Can experience joy again

**Duration**: Highly variable
- Acute grief: First 6-12 months most intense
- May take years to fully integrate loss

---

### Complicated Grief

**Definition**: Grief that is prolonged, intense, and interferes with functioning

**Types**:

#### 1. Chronic Grief
- Continues at high intensity indefinitely
- No progression toward acceptance
- Life remains focused on loss

#### 2. Delayed Grief
- Suppressed initially
- May emerge months or years later
- Triggered by another loss or event

#### 3. Exaggerated Grief
- Overwhelming, excessive response
- May develop into depression, anxiety, PTSD, substance abuse
- Requires psychiatric intervention

#### 4. Masked Grief
- Somatic symptoms without recognizing grief
- Physical complaints, behavior changes
- Underlying grief not acknowledged

#### 5. Disenfranchised Grief
- Loss not socially recognized or validated
- Examples: Miscarriage, loss of pet, ex-spouse, affair partner, non-traditional relationships
- Person may feel unable to openly mourn

---

### Anticipatory Grief

**Definition**: Grieving before actual loss occurs

**Occurs with**:
- Terminal diagnosis
- Progressive dementia
- Long illness

**Can Be**:
- **Adaptive**: Allows preparation, unfinished business addressed
- **Challenging**: May lead to premature detachment from dying person

**Nursing Support**:
- Normalize anticipatory grief
- Encourage meaningful time together
- Facilitate conversations
- Support practical planning (advance directives, funeral wishes)

- Decreased appetite
- Increased sleep

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient dying of cancer says "Why me? I've lived a good life!" What stage of grief is this?</summary>

**Answer**: Anger (KÃ¼bler-Ross Stage 2)

**Rationale**: The patient is expressing rage and questioning "why me," which is characteristic of the anger stage. The nurse should not take the anger personally, should allow the patient to express feelings, and acknowledge that anger is a normal response: "You have a right to feel angry about this."
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient's daughter says "If Dad just tries harder with physical therapy, he can get better." The patient has end-stage dementia and can no longer walk. What stage is the daughter in?</summary>

**Answer**: Bargaining (KÃ¼bler-Ross Stage 3)

**Rationale**: The daughter is trying to negotiate or find a way to reverse the inevitable. She's looking for a deal ("if he just tries harder, then..."). The nurse should gently present reality while supporting the daughter through her grief process.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: What is the BEST nursing response when a dying patient says "I'm scared"?</summary>

**Answer**: "Tell me what frightens you" or "I'm here with you."

**Rationale**: Open-ended question allows patient to express specific fears. Staying with patient provides comfort and support. False reassurance ("Don't be scared" or "Everything will be fine") is non-therapeutic and dismisses the patient's feelings.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient died 10 minutes ago. The family is present. What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Allow the family time alone with the body in a private, comfortable setting.

**Rationale**: The family needs time to process, say goodbye, and begin grieving. This is a priority after ensuring death has occurred. Postmortem care can wait. The nurse should offer privacy, ask if they need anything, and allow them to stay as long as they need.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: What indicates COMPLICATED grief requiring mental health referral?</summary>

**Answer**: Six months after spouse's death, widow remains in bed all day, unable to work, says life has no meaning.

**Rationale**: This describes complicated/prolonged grief with severe impairment in functioning. While grief is individual and has no set timeline, inability to function, severe depression, and lack of any improvement over time indicate need for professional help. Normal grief gradually improves with ability to function returning.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: A patient with terminal illness has "death rattle" (gurgling respirations from secretions). The family is distressed. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Explain that this is normal at end of life and patient is not in distress; may give anticholinergic medication (scopolamine) to reduce secretions.

**Rationale**: Death rattle sounds distressing to family but patient is typically unconscious and not suffering. Educating family reduces their distress. Suctioning is not recommended (uncomfortable, ineffective). Anticholinergics can reduce secretions. Repositioning may help.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: Which is appropriate for postmortem care?</summary>

**Answer**: Continue using standard precautions; place identification on body and toe tag; allow family time with body before transport.

**Rationale**: Standard precautions continue after death (body fluids remain infectious). Proper identification is required (patient band, toe tag). Family should be given time with their loved one. Body should be treated with dignity and respect throughout.
</details>
