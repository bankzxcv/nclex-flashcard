## âœ… Therapeutic Communication Techniques

### 1. Active Listening

**Definition**: Fully concentrating on what the patient is saying verbally and non-verbally

**Components**:
```
ACTIVE LISTENING INVOLVES:
  âœ“ Maintaining eye contact (culturally appropriate)
  âœ“ Open, relaxed posture
  âœ“ Leaning toward patient slightly
  âœ“ Nodding appropriately
  âœ“ Minimal verbal encouragement ("mm-hmm," "I see")
  âœ“ Avoiding interruptions
  âœ“ Focusing completely on patient
  âœ“ Observing non-verbal cues
```

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "I'm scared about the surgery tomorrow."
- **Nurse**: *[maintains eye contact, leans forward slightly, nods]* "Tell me more about what scares you."

---

### 2. Silence

**Purpose**: Allows patient time to think, organize thoughts, or experience emotions

**When to Use**:
- After asking important question
- When patient is emotional or crying
- When patient needs to process information
- To avoid filling every pause with words

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "I just found out I have cancer..." *[starts crying]*
- **Nurse**: *[sits quietly, offers tissue, maintains supportive presence]* *[waits]*
- **Patient**: *[after pause]* "I don't know how to tell my family."

**NCLEX Tip**: Silence is often the BEST answer when patient is emotional or processing difficult information.

---

### 3. Reflection

**Purpose**: Redirect patient's thoughts, feelings, or questions back to encourage exploration

**Types**:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Reflecting Feelings** | Identify and return emotion | P: "Everything's falling apart." N: "You're feeling overwhelmed." |
| **Reflecting Content** | Repeat key words/ideas | P: "I can't handle this anymore." N: "You feel you can't handle it?" |
| **Reflecting Questions** | Return question to patient | P: "Should I tell my kids?" N: "What do you think would be best?" |

---

### 4. Clarification

**Purpose**: Ensure understanding of patient's message

**Techniques**:
- "I'm not sure I understand. Can you explain that again?"
- "What do you mean when you say...?"
- "Help me understand what you're experiencing."
- "Can you give me an example?"

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "I'm feeling blue today."
- **Nurse**: "When you say 'blue,' can you tell me more about what you're experiencing?"

---

### 5. Open-Ended Questions

**Purpose**: Encourage detailed responses and exploration

**Format**: Questions that cannot be answered with "yes" or "no"

| Open-Ended âœ… | Closed-Ended âŒ |
|---------------|-----------------|
| "How are you feeling about your diagnosis?" | "Are you scared?" |
| "Tell me about your pain." | "Does it hurt?" |
| "What concerns do you have?" | "Are you worried?" |
| "How has this affected your daily life?" | "Can you sleep?" |
| "What helps you cope with stress?" | "Do you exercise?" |

---

### 6. Broad Openings

**Purpose**: Encourage patient to select topic and direction of conversation

**Examples**:
- "What would you like to talk about today?"
- "Where would you like to begin?"
- "What's on your mind?"
- "Tell me what's been happening."
- "What concerns you most?"

---

### 7. Focusing

**Purpose**: Direct conversation to important topic or help patient concentrate

**Examples**:
- "You mentioned feeling anxious. Let's talk more about that."
- "Let's go back to what you said about your family."
- "This seems to be important to you. Can we explore it further?"

**When to Use**: When patient is vague, tangential, or discussing multiple topics

---

### 8. Restating/Paraphrasing

**Purpose**: Show you're listening and verify understanding

**Technique**: Repeat what patient said in your own words

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "I've been having trouble sleeping, my appetite is gone, and I can't concentrate at work."
- **Nurse**: "So you're experiencing sleep problems, loss of appetite, and difficulty focusing?"

---

### 9. Summarizing

**Purpose**: Review and condense main points of conversation

**When to Use**:
- End of conversation
- Transition to new topic
- Ensure mutual understanding

**Example**:
- **Nurse**: "Let me make sure I understand. You're worried about going home because you live alone, you're having pain that's not controlled, and you're concerned about managing your medications. Is that right?"

---

### 10. Exploring

**Purpose**: Delve deeper into topic or feeling

**Examples**:
- "Tell me more about that."
- "Can you describe what that's like for you?"
- "What else?"
- "Go on..."
- "And then what happened?"

---

### 11. Empathy

**Definition**: Understanding and validating patient's feelings from their perspective

**Empathy vs. Sympathy**:

```
EMPATHY (Therapeutic) âœ…
  â€¢ "I can see this is very difficult for you."
  â€¢ "That sounds incredibly frustrating."
  â€¢ "I understand why you would feel that way."

SYMPATHY (Non-therapeutic) âŒ
  â€¢ "I feel so sorry for you."
  â€¢ "You poor thing."
  â€¢ "I pity you."
```

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "I feel like a burden to my family."
- **Nurse**: "It sounds like you're struggling with feeling dependent on others. That must be really hard."

---

### 12. Accepting

**Purpose**: Acknowledge and validate patient without agreeing or disagreeing

**Examples**:
- "I hear what you're saying."
- "You have a right to feel that way."
- "I understand this is important to you."
- "Go on, I'm listening."

**Note**: Accepting â‰  Agreeing. You validate the feeling, not necessarily the content.

---

### 13. Giving Information

**Purpose**: Provide facts and education

**Technique**:
- Provide when patient needs information
- Use simple, clear language
- Verify understanding
- Avoid information overload

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "What will happen during the procedure?"
- **Nurse**: "During the endoscopy, a thin tube with a camera will be passed through your mouth to look at your stomach. You'll be sedated, so you won't remember it. It usually takes about 20 minutes."

---

### 14. Suggesting Collaboration

**Purpose**: Offer to work together to solve problems

**Examples**:
- "Let's work on this together."
- "Perhaps we can come up with a plan."
- "What if we try...?"
- "Maybe together we can figure this out."

---

### 15. Presenting Reality

**Purpose**: Gently help patient distinguish reality from misperceptions (for psychotic patients)

**Technique**:
- State your perception
- Don't argue with delusions
- Don't reinforce delusions

**Example**:
- **Patient**: "The CIA is trying to poison my food."
- **Nurse**: "I understand you believe that, but I don't see any evidence of that. I've been here while your food was prepared. You're safe here."

### Scenario 1: Anxious Preoperative Patient

**Non-Therapeutic Responses** âŒ:

**Therapeutic Response** âœ…:
- "It sounds like you're in a lot of pain. Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" (Empathy + Direct assessment)

**Therapeutic Response** âœ…:
- "Tell me what happened when you took it before." (Exploring)

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: Patient says "I'm so worried about my test results." What is the MOST therapeutic response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Tell me what concerns you most about the results."

**Rationale**: This is an open-ended question that encourages the patient to explore and express feelings. It uses the therapeutic technique of exploring. Other options like "I'm sure they'll be fine" (false reassurance), "Why are you worried?" (asking why), or "Let's talk about something else" (changing subject) are non-therapeutic.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient is crying after receiving bad news. What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Sit quietly with the patient and offer tissues.

**Rationale**: Use of silence and supportive presence is most therapeutic when patient is emotional. This allows patient to experience emotions without feeling rushed. The nurse should not say "Don't cry" (minimizing), leave the patient alone (abandonment), or immediately ask questions (interrupting emotional processing).
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Patient states "Should I agree to this surgery?" What is the BEST response?</summary>

**Answer**: "What is your understanding of the surgery and its alternatives?"

**Rationale**: Reflecting the question back to the patient encourages autonomous decision-making. The nurse should assess understanding and help patient explore options, not give advice like "If I were you, I would" or "You should definitely do it."
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Which statement by the nurse is NON-therapeutic?</summary>

**Answer**: "Everything happens for a reason."

**Rationale**: This is a clichÃ©/stereotypical response that minimizes the patient's feelings and stops further exploration. It's philosophical rather than therapeutic. Therapeutic responses focus on patient's feelings and encourage expression.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: Patient with dementia says "I need to go home to make dinner for my husband" (husband died 10 years ago). What is the BEST response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Tell me about your husband. You must miss him."

**Rationale**: For patients with dementia, validate the feeling and redirect rather than argue with their reality. Saying "Your husband died 10 years ago" would be cruel and cause distress. Focus on the emotion (missing husband) rather than correcting the facts.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: Which demonstrates active listening?</summary>

**Answer**: Sitting at eye level, maintaining eye contact, and nodding while patient speaks.

**Rationale**: Active listening involves non-verbal behaviors that show attention and interest: appropriate eye contact, open posture, facing patient, nodding, and full attention. Standing at door, looking at chart, or interrupting frequently are barriers to active listening.
</details>
