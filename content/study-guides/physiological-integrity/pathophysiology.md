# Pathophysiology & Disease Processes

## Overview
Understanding disease processes and their nursing implications is essential for the NCLEX. This guide covers major pathophysiological concepts, disease mechanisms, and nursing management.

---

## ü´Ä Cardiovascular Pathophysiology

### Heart Failure (HF)

**Definition**: Heart unable to pump adequate blood to meet body's needs

**Types**:

#### Left-Sided Heart Failure

**Pathophysiology**: Left ventricle fails ‚Üí blood backs up into lungs ‚Üí pulmonary congestion

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Pulmonary congestion**: Dyspnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND)
- **Crackles** in lung bases
- **Frothy, pink-tinged sputum** (pulmonary edema)
- **S3 heart sound** (gallop)
- Cough (worse when lying down)
- Tachycardia
- Fatigue, weakness

**Mnemonic**: **"CHAMP"** (Pulmonary symptoms)
- **C**ough
- **H**emoptysis (pink frothy sputum)
- **A**bnormal breath sounds (crackles)
- **M**oist lung sounds
- **P**ulmonary congestion

#### Right-Sided Heart Failure

**Pathophysiology**: Right ventricle fails ‚Üí blood backs up into systemic circulation ‚Üí peripheral congestion

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Peripheral edema** (dependent: ankles, feet, legs)
- **Jugular vein distention (JVD)**
- **Hepatomegaly** (enlarged liver)
- **Ascites** (fluid in abdomen)
- **Weight gain**
- Anorexia, nausea
- Nocturia

**Mnemonic**: **"ABEDS"** (Systemic symptoms)
- **A**scites
- **B**loating
- **E**dema (peripheral)
- **D**istended neck veins (JVD)
- **S**ystemic congestion

**Note**: Left-sided HF often progresses to right-sided HF (biventricular failure)

---

### Nursing Management of Heart Failure

**Medications**:

| Class | Examples | Action | Nursing Considerations |
|-------|----------|--------|------------------------|
| **ACE Inhibitors** | Lisinopril, enalapril | ‚Üì Afterload, ‚Üì fluid retention | Monitor K+, dry cough, check BP |
| **Beta Blockers** | Carvedilol, metoprolol | ‚Üì Heart rate, ‚Üì workload | Check HR (hold if <60) |
| **Diuretics** | Furosemide (Lasix) | ‚Üì Fluid overload | Monitor K+, I&O, daily weights |
| **Digoxin** | Lanoxin | ‚Üë Contractility, ‚Üì HR | Check apical pulse (hold if <60), monitor digoxin level |
| **Aldosterone Antagonists** | Spironolactone | ‚Üì Fluid, K+-sparing | Monitor K+ (risk hyperkalemia) |

**Nursing Interventions**:
- **Daily weights** (same time, same scale, same clothing)
  - Report gain of **2-3 lbs in 1 day** or **5 lbs in 1 week**
- **Monitor I&O**
- **Sodium restriction** (2 g/day)
- **Fluid restriction** (if ordered)
- **Position**: High Fowler's (ease breathing)
- **Activity**: Balance rest and activity, avoid overexertion
- **Monitor**: Lung sounds, edema, JVD, vital signs
- **Oxygen therapy** (if needed)

**Patient Teaching**:
- **Weigh daily** - report significant gains
- **Take medications as prescribed** - don't skip
- **Low-sodium diet**
- **Report**: Increased dyspnea, edema, weight gain, cough
- **Elevate legs** when sitting

### Myocardial Infarction (MI)

**Pathophysiology**: Coronary artery occlusion ‚Üí ischemia ‚Üí necrosis of myocardium

**Risk Factors** (Mnemonic: **"DASH"**):
- **D**iabetes
- **A**therosclerosis, Age
- **S**moking, Stress
- **H**ypertension, Hyperlipidemia, Heredity

**Complications**:

| Complication | Timing | Signs |
|--------------|--------|-------|
| **Arrhythmias** | Any time | Irregular pulse, palpitations, syncope |
| **Heart failure** | 24-48 hours | Dyspnea, crackles, edema |
| **Cardiogenic shock** | Immediate | Hypotension, cool/clammy skin, decreased UO |
| **Pericarditis** | 2-3 days | Chest pain (worse with inspiration, lying flat), friction rub |
| **Ventricular aneurysm** | Weeks-months | Heart failure symptoms, arrhythmias |
| **Cardiac rupture** | 3-5 days | Sudden deterioration, cardiac tamponade |

**Nursing Management**: See Medical Emergencies guide

**Cardiac Enzymes**:
- **Troponin**: Rises 2-4 hrs, peaks 24 hrs, elevated for 7-10 days (most specific)
- **CK-MB**: Rises 3-6 hrs, peaks 12-24 hrs, returns to normal 48-72 hrs

## ü´Å Respiratory Pathophysiology

### Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

**Includes**: Chronic bronchitis, emphysema

#### Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater")

**Pathophysiology**: Inflammation ‚Üí excessive mucus production ‚Üí airway obstruction

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Chronic productive cough** (‚â•3 months for 2 consecutive years)
- **Dyspnea**
- **Cyanosis** (chronic hypoxemia)
- **Edema** (cor pulmonale - right heart failure)
- **Barrel chest**
- **Increased anteroposterior diameter**

**Mnemonic**: **"Blue bloater"** = Bronchitis
- **B**lue (cyanotic)
- **B**loated (edema)

#### Emphysema ("Pink Puffer")

**Pathophysiology**: Alveolar wall destruction ‚Üí loss of elastic recoil ‚Üí air trapping

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Dyspnea on exertion** (primary symptom)
- **Barrel chest** (hyperinflation)
- **Use of accessory muscles**
- **Pursed-lip breathing**
- **Tripod position**
- **Thin, wasted appearance**
- **Pink color** (adequate oxygenation until late)

**Mnemonic**: **"Pink puffer"** = Emphysema
- **P**ink (adequate oxygenation)
- **P**uffing (pursed-lip breathing)

---

### COPD Nursing Management

**Oxygen Therapy**:
- **Target SpO2: 88-92%** (NOT higher!)
  - **Why?** COPD patients depend on **hypoxic drive** to breathe
  - High O2 can suppress respiratory drive
- **Low-flow oxygen** (1-2 L/min nasal cannula)

**Medications**:

| Class | Examples | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Bronchodilators (rescue)** | Albuterol | Open airways during exacerbation |
| **Anticholinergics** | Ipratropium (Atrovent) | Bronchodilation, ‚Üì secretions |
| **Corticosteroids (inhaled)** | Fluticasone | Reduce inflammation (maintenance) |
| **Corticosteroids (systemic)** | Prednisone | Acute exacerbations |

**Inhaler Technique**:
1. **Bronchodilator FIRST** (opens airways)
2. Wait 5 minutes
3. **Corticosteroid SECOND**
4. **Rinse mouth** after corticosteroid (prevent thrush)

**Nursing Interventions**:
- **Breathing techniques**:
  - **Pursed-lip breathing**: Breathe in through nose, exhale slowly through pursed lips (reduces air trapping)
  - **Diaphragmatic breathing**: Use diaphragm, not accessory muscles
- **Position**: High Fowler's or tripod
- **Hydration**: 2-3 L/day (thin secretions)
- **Chest physiotherapy**
- **Energy conservation**
- **Small, frequent meals** (reduces dyspnea)
- **Vaccinations**: Pneumonia, influenza

**Patient Teaching**:
- **Stop smoking!** (most important)
- Avoid respiratory irritants
- Recognize signs of infection (increased sputum, color change, fever)
- Use oxygen as prescribed (don't increase on own)

### Asthma

**Pathophysiology**: Airway inflammation ‚Üí bronchospasm ‚Üí mucus production ‚Üí airway obstruction

**Triggers**:
- Allergens (pollen, dust, pet dander)
- Respiratory infections
- Exercise
- Cold air
- Stress
- Medications (aspirin, NSAIDs, beta blockers)

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Expiratory wheezing** (classic)
- Dyspnea, tachypnea
- Chest tightness
- Cough (especially at night)
- Use of accessory muscles
- Prolonged expiratory phase

**Severe Exacerbation** (Status Asthmaticus):
- **Silent chest** (no air movement - ominous!)
- Inability to speak full sentences
- Peak flow <50% predicted
- Decreased LOC

**Treatment**:
- **Quick-relief (rescue)**: Albuterol (inhaler or nebulizer)
- **Long-term control**: Inhaled corticosteroids
- **Severe exacerbation**:
  - Continuous albuterol nebulizer
  - Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone, methylprednisolone)
  - Oxygen
  - Magnesium sulfate IV
  - Intubation (if respiratory failure)

**Nursing Management**:
- Monitor peak flow
- Identify and avoid triggers
- **Asthma action plan**
- Proper inhaler technique

## üß† Neurological Pathophysiology

### Parkinson's Disease

**Pathophysiology**: Degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons in substantia nigra ‚Üí dopamine deficiency ‚Üí motor symptoms

**Classic Triad**:
1. **Tremor** (resting tremor - "pill-rolling")
2. **Rigidity** (muscle stiffness - "cogwheel rigidity")
3. **Bradykinesia** (slowness of movement)

**Additional Signs**:
- **Masked facies** (expressionless face)
- **Shuffling gait** (small, shuffling steps)
- **Stooped posture**
- **Difficulty initiating movement**
- **Postural instability** (fall risk)
- **Micrographia** (small handwriting)
- **Soft, monotone speech**
- **Drooling**, dysphagia

**Mnemonic**: **"TRAP"**
- **T**remor (resting)
- **R**igidity
- **A**kinesia/bradykinesia
- **P**ostural instability

**Medications**:

| Drug | Action | Nursing Considerations |
|------|--------|------------------------|
| **Levodopa/Carbidopa (Sinemet)** | ‚Üë Dopamine | Take with food; **"on-off" phenomenon** (fluctuating symptoms); may cause dyskinesias |
| **Dopamine agonists** | Stimulate dopamine receptors | Orthostatic hypotension, hallucinations |
| **MAO-B inhibitors** | Prevent dopamine breakdown | Avoid tyramine-rich foods |
| **Anticholinergics** | ‚Üì Tremor and rigidity | Dry mouth, urinary retention, confusion (use cautiously in elderly) |

**Nursing Management**:
- **Fall precautions** (postural instability)
- **Assistive devices** (walker, cane)
- **High-protein snacks** (levodopa competes with dietary protein)
- **Prevent aspiration** (dysphagia)
  - Upright position during meals
  - Thickened liquids if needed
  - Small bites, adequate time to chew/swallow
- **Prevent constipation** (common)
- **ROM exercises** (prevent contractures)
- **Encourage independence** (allow extra time for ADLs)

---

### Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

**Pathophysiology**: Autoimmune demyelination of CNS ‚Üí impaired nerve impulse transmission

**Pattern**: **Relapsing-remitting** (most common) - exacerbations and remissions

**Signs & Symptoms** (vary based on lesion location):
- **Visual**: Optic neuritis, diplopia, nystagmus
- **Motor**: Weakness, spasticity, paralysis
- **Sensory**: Numbness, tingling, paresthesias
- **Cerebellar**: Ataxia, intention tremor, dysarthria
- **Fatigue** (hallmark symptom)
- **Bladder dysfunction**: Urgency, retention
- **Cognitive changes**: Memory, concentration
- **Heat sensitivity**: Symptoms worsen with heat

**Mnemonic**: **"VITAMINS"**
- **V**ision problems
- **I**ntention tremor
- **T**ingling/numbness
- **A**taxia
- **M**uscle weakness/spasticity
- **I**ncontinence (bladder)
- **N**ystagmus
- **S**peech difficulties (scanning speech)

**Medications**:
- **Disease-modifying therapies**: Interferon beta, glatiramer acetate (reduce relapses)
- **Corticosteroids**: Methylprednisolone (acute exacerbations)
- **Muscle relaxants**: Baclofen (spasticity)
- **Immunosuppressants**

**Nursing Management**:
- **Energy conservation** (fatigue management)
  - Schedule activities during peak energy times
  - Frequent rest periods
- **Avoid triggers**:
  - **Heat** (hot baths, fever, hot weather)
  - Stress
  - Infections
- **Cool environment** (may reduce symptoms)
- **Bladder training** program
- **Prevent skin breakdown** (immobility)
- **Safety**: Fall precautions (ataxia, weakness)
- **Support groups**, counseling (chronic, unpredictable disease)

---

### Alzheimer's Disease

**Pathophysiology**: Progressive neurodegeneration ‚Üí amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles ‚Üí loss of neurons ‚Üí cognitive decline

**Stages**:

#### Stage 1: Mild (Early)
- **Memory loss** (recent events)
- Difficulty finding words
- Misplacing items
- Difficulty with complex tasks
- Personality changes

#### Stage 2: Moderate (Middle)
- **Increased memory loss** (remote memory affected)
- **Confusion**, disorientation
- Difficulty recognizing family/friends
- **Wandering**, getting lost
- **Sundowning** (increased confusion in evening)
- Behavioral changes (agitation, aggression)
- Requires assistance with ADLs

#### Stage 3: Severe (Late)
- **Loss of verbal communication**
- **Total dependence** for ADLs
- Weight loss
- Incontinence
- **Inability to recognize loved ones**
- Seizures (late stage)
- Increased infection risk (aspiration pneumonia, UTI)

**Nursing Management**:

**Safety**:
- **Wandering prevention**: Alarms, identification bracelet, supervision
- **Remove hazards**: Locks on cabinets (cleaning supplies, medications), stove covers
- **Fall prevention**

**Communication**:
- Simple, clear instructions
- One task at a time
- Maintain eye contact
- Allow time to respond
- Avoid arguing or correcting
- Use distraction for agitation

**Environment**:
- **Consistent routine**
- Familiar environment (reduce relocation)
- **Adequate lighting** (reduce confusion)
- **Reduce noise** (minimize overstimulation)

**Behavioral Management**:
- **Sundowning**: More light in evening, avoid naps, structured activities
- **Agitation**: Identify triggers, calm approach, distraction, music
- **Validation therapy**: Acknowledge feelings

**Caregiver Support**:
- Respite care
- Support groups
- Education about disease progression

## ü©∏ Hematological Pathophysiology

### Anemia

**Definition**: Decreased RBCs, hemoglobin, or hematocrit ‚Üí decreased oxygen-carrying capacity

**Types**:

#### Iron Deficiency Anemia

**Causes**: Blood loss (GI bleed, menstruation), inadequate intake, malabsorption

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Fatigue, weakness
- Pallor (skin, mucous membranes, conjunctiva)
- **Pica** (craving for non-food items like ice, dirt)
- **Brittle nails**, **spoon-shaped nails** (koilonychia)
- Glossitis (smooth, sore tongue)
- Tachycardia, dyspnea on exertion

**Labs**:
- ‚Üì Hemoglobin, ‚Üì hematocrit
- ‚Üì Serum iron
- ‚Üì Ferritin
- ‚Üë TIBC (total iron-binding capacity)
- **Microcytic, hypochromic** RBCs

**Treatment**:
- **Oral iron supplements** (ferrous sulfate)
  - Take with **vitamin C** (increases absorption)
  - Take on **empty stomach** (better absorption, but may cause GI upset)
  - **Separate from antacids, dairy, calcium** (decrease absorption)
  - **Black, tarry stools** are expected
- **Iron-rich diet**: Red meat, liver, beans, spinach, fortified cereals
- **Treat underlying cause** (stop bleeding source)

#### Vitamin B12 Deficiency Anemia (Pernicious Anemia)

**Causes**: Lack of intrinsic factor (needed for B12 absorption), malabsorption, strict vegan diet

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Anemia symptoms (fatigue, pallor)
- **Neurological**: Paresthesias, ataxia, decreased proprioception
- **Beefy red tongue**
- Dementia (if severe)

**Labs**: **Macrocytic** RBCs, ‚Üì B12

**Treatment**: **Vitamin B12 injections** (oral not effective if intrinsic factor absent)

#### Sickle Cell Anemia

**Pathophysiology**: Genetic disorder ‚Üí abnormal hemoglobin S ‚Üí RBCs become sickle-shaped under low O2 ‚Üí vaso-occlusion ‚Üí ischemia/pain

**Sickle Cell Crisis** (Vaso-Occlusive Crisis):
- **Severe pain** (bones, joints, abdomen)
- Triggered by: Infection, dehydration, hypoxia, stress, cold

**Treatment**:
- **Hydration** (IV and oral fluids)
- **Oxygen**
- **Pain management** (opioids often needed)
- **Avoid triggers**

**Chronic Complications**:
- Stroke
- Acute chest syndrome
- Splenic sequestration
- Organ damage (kidneys, liver, spleen)

**Nursing Management**:
- **Hydrate** (prevent sickling)
- **Oxygen** (if hypoxic)
- **Pain management**
- **Avoid cold**, high altitudes
- **Vaccinations** (pneumonia, meningitis - asplenic)
- **Folic acid supplementation**

---

### Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

**Pathophysiology**: Widespread clotting ‚Üí consumption of clotting factors and platelets ‚Üí bleeding

**Triggers**: Sepsis, trauma, obstetric complications (placental abruption), malignancy

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Bleeding from multiple sites**: IV sites, gums, nose
- **Petechiae, purpura**
- **Organ dysfunction** (kidneys, lungs)

**Labs**:
- ‚Üì Platelets
- ‚Üì Fibrinogen
- ‚Üë D-dimer, ‚Üë FDP (fibrin degradation products)
- Prolonged PT, aPTT

**Treatment**:
- **Treat underlying cause** (antibiotics for sepsis)
- **Replace clotting factors**: FFP (fresh frozen plasma), platelets, cryoprecipitate
- **Supportive care**

## üçÉ Endocrine Pathophysiology

### Diabetes Mellitus

#### Type 1 Diabetes

**Pathophysiology**: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells ‚Üí no insulin production

**Onset**: Usually childhood/young adult (but can occur at any age)

**Treatment**: **Insulin** (lifelong)

#### Type 2 Diabetes

**Pathophysiology**: Insulin resistance + relative insulin deficiency

**Risk Factors**: Obesity, sedentary lifestyle, family history, age >45

**Treatment**: Lifestyle modifications, oral antidiabetics, insulin (if needed)

---

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

**Pathophysiology**: Insulin deficiency ‚Üí cells can't use glucose ‚Üí breakdown of fat ‚Üí ketones ‚Üí metabolic acidosis

**Common in**: Type 1 diabetes

**Triggers**: Infection, inadequate insulin, illness, stress

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Metabolic** | Hyperglycemia (>250 mg/dL), **ketones in blood/urine**, **metabolic acidosis** (pH <7.3) |
| **Respiratory** | **Kussmaul respirations** (deep, rapid), **fruity breath odor** (acetone) |
| **GI** | Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain |
| **Neurological** | Confusion, decreased LOC |
| **General** | Dehydration (polyuria, polydipsia), weight loss |

**Mnemonic**: **"DKA"**
- **D**ehydration (from polyuria)
- **K**ussmaul respirations (blow off CO2 to compensate for acidosis)
- **A**cidosis (metabolic)

**Labs**:
- **Glucose >250 mg/dL**
- **pH <7.3**
- **Positive ketones**
- **Anion gap >12**

**Treatment**:
1. **IV fluids** (NS - aggressive rehydration)
2. **Regular insulin IV** (continuous infusion)
3. **Correct electrolytes**:
   - **Potassium** (insulin drives K+ into cells ‚Üí hypokalemia)
   - Add K+ to IV when K+ <5.0 and adequate urine output
4. **Monitor glucose** (goal: ‚Üì 50-75 mg/dL per hour)
5. When glucose ~200-250: Switch to **D5W** (prevent hypoglycemia) + continue insulin
6. **Transition to SubQ insulin** when acidosis resolved

**Critical**: **Check K+ before giving insulin!** Insulin causes hypokalemia

---

### Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

**Pathophysiology**: Severe hyperglycemia ‚Üí hyperosmolarity ‚Üí dehydration (no significant ketones)

**Common in**: Type 2 diabetes

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Severe hyperglycemia** (>600 mg/dL)
- **Severe dehydration**
- **Altered mental status** (confusion, coma)
- **NO acidosis, NO ketones** (differentiates from DKA)

**Treatment**: Similar to DKA (fluids, insulin) but **slower correction** (prevent cerebral edema)

**Comparison**:

| Feature | DKA | HHS |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Type of DM** | Type 1 | Type 2 |
| **Glucose** | >250 mg/dL | >600 mg/dL |
| **Ketones** | **Present** | **Absent** |
| **pH** | <7.3 (acidosis) | >7.3 (no acidosis) |
| **Onset** | Rapid (hours) | Gradual (days) |
| **Respirations** | Kussmaul | Normal |

---

### Hypoglycemia

**Glucose <70 mg/dL**

**Causes**: Excess insulin, inadequate food intake, increased exercise, alcohol

**Signs & Symptoms** (Mnemonic: **"TIRED"**):
- **T**remors, tachycardia
- **I**rritability
- **R**estlessness
- **E**xcessive hunger
- **D**iaphoresis

**Additional**: Confusion, blurred vision, headache, seizures, coma

**Treatment** - **"Rule of 15"**:
1. Give **15 grams fast-acting carbohydrate**:
   - 4 oz (¬Ω cup) juice or regular soda
   - 3-4 glucose tablets
   - 1 tablespoon honey or sugar
2. **Wait 15 minutes**
3. **Recheck glucose**
4. If still <70, repeat
5. Once >70: Give complex carb/protein snack

**If unconscious**: **Glucagon 1 mg IM/SubQ** or **D50W IV**

**NEVER** give oral intake if unconscious (aspiration risk)

### Thyroid Disorders

#### Hyperthyroidism (Graves' Disease)

**Pathophysiology**: Excessive thyroid hormone ‚Üí increased metabolism

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Weight loss** despite increased appetite
- **Heat intolerance**, diaphoresis
- **Nervousness**, irritability, anxiety
- **Tachycardia**, palpitations
- **Exophthalmos** (bulging eyes - Graves' disease)
- Tremors
- **Diarrhea**
- **Goiter** (enlarged thyroid)

**Mnemonic**: **"SWEATING"**
- **S**weating
- **W**eight loss
- **E**motional lability
- **A**ppetite increased
- **T**achycardia, tremors
- **I**ntolerance to heat
- **N**ervous
- **G**oiter

**Labs**: ‚Üì TSH, ‚Üë T4, ‚Üë T3

**Treatment**:
- **Antithyroid drugs**: Methimazole, propylthiouracil (PTU)
- **Beta blockers** (control symptoms)
- **Radioactive iodine** (destroys thyroid tissue)
- **Surgery** (thyroidectomy)

#### Thyroid Storm

**Medical emergency** - extreme hyperthyroidism

**Signs**: High fever (>104¬∞F), severe tachycardia, hypertension, delirium, heart failure

**Treatment**: IV antithyroid drugs, beta blockers, corticosteroids, cooling measures, supportive care

---

#### Hypothyroidism

**Pathophysiology**: Deficient thyroid hormone ‚Üí decreased metabolism

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Weight gain**
- **Cold intolerance**
- **Fatigue**, lethargy
- **Bradycardia**
- **Constipation**
- **Dry skin**, brittle hair
- **Myxedema** (facial/periorbital edema)
- **Depression**
- **Goiter** (if iodine deficiency)

**Mnemonic**: Everything **SLOW**
- **S**kin dry
- **L**ethargy
- **O**besity/weight gain
- **W**eakness

**Labs**: ‚Üë TSH, ‚Üì T4

**Treatment**: **Levothyroxine** (Synthroid)
- Take on **empty stomach**
- **Same time daily**
- Lifelong therapy

#### Myxedema Coma

**Medical emergency** - severe hypothyroidism

**Signs**: Hypothermia, hypotension, bradycardia, hypoventilation, decreased LOC

**Treatment**: IV levothyroxine, warming measures, supportive care

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Pathophysiology Concepts

**Heart Failure**:
- **Left-sided** = **Lungs** (pulmonary congestion)
- **Right-sided** = **Rest of body** (peripheral edema)

**COPD**:
- **Chronic bronchitis** = "Blue bloater"
- **Emphysema** = "Pink puffer"
- **Target O2: 88-92%** (hypoxic drive)

**Parkinson's**: **TRAP** (Tremor, Rigidity, Akinesia, Postural instability)

**MS**: Relapsing-remitting, **avoid heat**, fatigue management

**DKA vs. HHS**:
- **DKA**: Type 1, **ketones present**, **Kussmaul respirations**
- **HHS**: Type 2, **no ketones**, glucose >600

**Hypoglycemia**: **Rule of 15** - 15g carb, wait 15 min, recheck

**Thyroid**:
- **Hyper**: Everything FAST (weight loss, tachycardia, heat intolerance)
- **Hypo**: Everything SLOW (weight gain, bradycardia, cold intolerance)

**Sickle Cell**: **Hydration** (prevent crisis), avoid triggers

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient with heart failure has gained 5 lbs in 3 days and reports increased dyspnea. What is the priority assessment?</summary>

**Answer**: Auscultate lung sounds

**Rationale**: Weight gain (fluid retention) + dyspnea = fluid volume excess/pulmonary congestion. Priority is assessing lung sounds for crackles (pulmonary edema). Also assess for other signs: JVD, peripheral edema. Notify provider - may need diuretic dose adjustment. Reinforce daily weights, sodium restriction, medication compliance.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient with COPD has SpO2 of 98% on 6 L O2 via nasal cannula. The patient is lethargic. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Decrease oxygen to 1-2 L/min and notify provider

**Rationale**: COPD patients rely on hypoxic drive. High oxygen (and high SpO2) can suppress respiratory drive, causing lethargy/respiratory depression. Target SpO2 for COPD: 88-92%. Decrease oxygen, assess respiratory rate and effort, notify provider.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A patient with Type 1 diabetes has glucose 350 mg/dL, fruity breath, and Kussmaul respirations. What does the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

**Rationale**: Hyperglycemia + fruity breath (acetone) + Kussmaul respirations (compensating for metabolic acidosis) = DKA. Check for ketones, assess pH (will be <7.3). Treatment: IV fluids, insulin, correct electrolytes (especially potassium). This is a medical emergency.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient taking levothyroxine for hypothyroidism reports palpitations and heat intolerance. What does this indicate?</summary>

**Answer**: Excessive thyroid hormone (hyperthyroidism symptoms)

**Rationale**: Palpitations (tachycardia) and heat intolerance indicate hyperthyroid symptoms = levothyroxine dose is too high. Notify provider for dose adjustment. Check TSH level. Teach patient to report signs of hyperthyroidism.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient with Parkinson's disease is having difficulty swallowing. What is the priority nursing intervention?</summary>

**Answer**: Position patient upright during meals and assess swallowing ability

**Rationale**: Dysphagia is common in Parkinson's and increases aspiration risk. Priority is preventing aspiration: upright position, small bites, adequate time to chew/swallow, thickened liquids if needed. Monitor for coughing/choking. May need speech therapy evaluation.
</details>
