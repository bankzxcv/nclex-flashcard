# Body Systems Alterations & Nursing Care

## Overview
Understanding alterations in body systems and appropriate nursing care is essential for the NCLEX. This guide covers major body systems, common alterations, and nursing management.

---

## üß† Neurological System

### Stroke Assessment & Care

**Neurological Assessment** - Use **NIH Stroke Scale**:
- Level of consciousness
- Visual fields
- Facial palsy
- Motor function (arm/leg)
- Sensation
- Language/speech
- Neglect

**Post-Stroke Nursing Care**:

**Positioning**:
- **Affected side**: Position in functional alignment
  - Shoulder slightly forward
  - Arm supported on pillow
  - Hip neutral or slightly flexed
  - Knee slightly flexed
  - Foot at 90¬∞ angle (prevent footdrop)
- **Turn every 2 hours**
- **Elevate HOB 30¬∞** (prevent aspiration, reduce ICP)

**Mobility**:
- **Start from unaffected side** (stronger side)
- **Scan environment** (address visual field deficits)
- **Transfer toward unaffected side**
- **Assistive devices**: Cane on unaffected side

**Communication** (Aphasia):

| Type | Description | Nursing Approach |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Expressive (Broca's)** | Knows what to say but can't say it | Allow time, use yes/no questions, picture boards |
| **Receptive (Wernicke's)** | Can speak but doesn't understand | Simple commands, gestures, demonstrations |
| **Global** | Both expressive and receptive | Picture boards, patience, speech therapy |

**Dysphagia (Swallowing Difficulty)**:
- **NPO until swallow evaluation**
- **Upright position** 90¬∞ during and 30 min after meals
- **Chin tuck** when swallowing
- **Thickened liquids** if ordered
- **Small bites**, adequate time to chew/swallow
- **Monitor for aspiration**: Coughing, choking, wet voice

**Hemorrhagic Stroke Specifics**:
- **Blood pressure management**: Prevent rebound bleeding
  - Target: SBP 140-160 mmHg
  - Avoid aggressive lowering
- **Avoid anticoagulants/antiplatelets**
- **Monitor for rebleeding**

---

### Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP) Management

**Prevention**:
- **Elevate HOB 30¬∞**
- **Maintain head in midline** (neutral alignment)
- **Prevent Valsalva**:
  - Stool softeners
  - Avoid straining, coughing
  - Gentle suctioning (<10 seconds)
- **Avoid hip flexion >90¬∞**
- **Cluster care** (minimize stimulation)
- **Maintain normothermia** (fever ‚Üë ICP)

**Interventions if ICP Elevated**:
- **Osmotic diuretics**: Mannitol, hypertonic saline
- **Hyperventilation** (temporary - causes vasoconstriction)
- **Sedation** (reduce metabolic demand)
- **Seizure prophylaxis**
- **Surgical decompression** (if needed)

---

### Seizure Care

**During Seizure**:
1. **Protect from injury**
   - Turn on side (prevent aspiration)
   - Loosen restrictive clothing
   - Cushion head
   - Remove dangerous objects
2. **DO NOT**:
   - Restrain patient
   - Put anything in mouth
   - Force mouth open
3. **Time the seizure**
4. **Observe**: Type, duration, body parts involved
5. **Maintain airway**

**After Seizure** (Postictal Phase):
- **Position on side** (recovery position)
- **Suction** if needed
- **Allow to rest** (common: confusion, drowsiness, headache)
- **Reorient** patient
- **Monitor vital signs**

**Precautions**:
- Padded side rails (if high risk)
- Bed in lowest position
- Suction and oxygen at bedside
- IV access (if status epilepticus risk)

**Patient Teaching**:
- Take antiepileptic drugs as prescribed (**NEVER stop abruptly**)
- Wear medical alert bracelet
- Avoid triggers (lack of sleep, alcohol, flashing lights)
- No driving (state regulations vary)
- Shower instead of bath (drowning risk)
- Avoid swimming alone
- Avoid heights, dangerous machinery

## ü´Ä Cardiovascular System

### Hypertension Management

**Blood Pressure Categories**:
- **Normal**: <120/<80
- **Elevated**: 120-129/<80
- **Stage 1**: 130-139/80-89
- **Stage 2**: ‚â•140/‚â•90
- **Crisis**: >180/>120

**Nursing Care**:
- **Lifestyle modifications**:
  - **DASH diet** (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
  - **Sodium restriction** (<2300 mg/day, ideally <1500 mg/day)
  - **Weight loss** (if overweight)
  - **Regular exercise** (30 min most days)
  - **Limit alcohol**
  - **Stress management**
  - **Smoking cessation**

**Medication Classes**:
- ACE inhibitors (-pril)
- ARBs (-sartan)
- Beta blockers (-lol)
- Calcium channel blockers (-dipine)
- Diuretics (thiazide, loop)

**Patient Teaching**:
- **Home blood pressure monitoring**
- **Take medications daily** (even if feeling well)
- **Don't stop medications abruptly**
- Report: Severe headache, vision changes, chest pain

### Peripheral Vascular Disease (PVD)

#### Arterial Insufficiency

**Pathophysiology**: Decreased arterial blood flow (atherosclerosis)

**Assessment** - **"6 P's"**:
1. **P**ain (intermittent claudication - worse with activity)
2. **P**allor
3. **P**ulselessness (weak/absent pulses)
4. **P**aresthesias
5. **P**aralysis (late sign)
6. **P**oikilothermia (cool extremity)

**Additional Signs**:
- **Shiny, hairless skin**
- **Thick, brittle nails**
- **Dependent rubor** (redness when dependent)
- **Elevation pallor** (pale when elevated)
- **Pain WORSE with elevation** (decreased blood flow)
- **Pain RELIEVED by dangling legs** (gravity assists flow)

**Nursing Care**:
- **Position**: Keep legs **dependent** (below heart level)
- **Protect from injury** (decreased sensation)
- **Avoid**:
  - Crossing legs
  - Tight clothing
  - Heat/cold application (injury risk)
  - Smoking (**vasoconstriction**)
- **Foot care**:
  - Inspect daily
  - Wash with mild soap, dry thoroughly (especially between toes)
  - Moisturize (not between toes)
  - Proper-fitting shoes
  - **NO barefoot walking**
  - **Trim nails straight across**
- **Exercise**: Walking to point of pain, rest, resume (‚Üë collateral circulation)

---

#### Venous Insufficiency

**Pathophysiology**: Impaired venous return (valve incompetence)

**Assessment**:
- **Edema** (pitting, worse at end of day)
- **Aching, heaviness** (worse with prolonged standing)
- **Brown pigmentation** (hemosiderin staining)
- **Stasis dermatitis**
- **Venous ulcers**: Typically on medial ankle
- **Pain RELIEVED by elevation**
- Pulses present (unlike arterial)

**Nursing Care**:
- **Position**: **Elevate legs above heart** (improve venous return)
- **Compression stockings** (TED hose, compression wraps)
  - Apply in morning before getting out of bed
  - Remove at night
  - Assess circulation
- **Avoid prolonged standing/sitting**
- **Exercise**: Ankle pumps, walking
- **Weight management**

**Comparison**:

| Feature | Arterial Insufficiency | Venous Insufficiency |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Pain** | Intermittent claudication, rest pain | Aching, heaviness |
| **Ulcer Location** | Toes, lateral ankle | Medial ankle |
| **Ulcer Appearance** | Pale, well-defined edges | Irregular edges, ruddy |
| **Skin** | Shiny, hairless, cool | Brown pigmentation, warm |
| **Edema** | Minimal | Significant |
| **Pulses** | Diminished/absent | Present |
| **Position** | **Legs DOWN** (dependent) | **Legs UP** (elevated) |

**Mnemonic**: **Arterial = DOWN, Venous = UP**

## ü´Å Respiratory System

### Pneumonia Care

**Types**:
- **Community-acquired** (CAP): Outside hospital
- **Hospital-acquired** (HAP): ‚â•48 hrs after admission
- **Ventilator-associated** (VAP): ‚â•48 hrs after intubation

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Fever**, chills
- **Productive cough** (yellow/green/rust-colored sputum)
- **Dyspnea**, tachypnea
- **Crackles**, decreased breath sounds
- **Pleuritic chest pain** (worse with deep breath)
- Fatigue, malaise
- Confusion (especially elderly)

**Nursing Care**:

**Respiratory Support**:
- **Position**: High Fowler's or semi-Fowler's
- **Oxygen therapy** (maintain SpO2 >90%)
- **Incentive spirometry** q1-2h while awake
- **Deep breathing and coughing**
- **Chest physiotherapy** (if ordered)

**Medications**:
- **Antibiotics**: Start after blood cultures obtained
- **Antipyretics**: Acetaminophen for fever
- **Bronchodilators**: If wheezing

**Hydration & Nutrition**:
- **Increase fluids** (2-3 L/day) - thin secretions
- **Nutritious diet** (support immune system)
- Small, frequent meals (reduce dyspnea)

**Prevention** (VAP Prevention Bundle):
- **Elevate HOB 30-45¬∞**
- **Oral care** with chlorhexidine q2h
- **Suction subglottic secretions**
- **Daily sedation vacation**
- **DVT prophylaxis**
- **Stress ulcer prophylaxis**

**Patient Teaching**:
- **Complete full course of antibiotics**
- **Vaccinations**: Pneumonia vaccine, annual flu shot
- **Smoking cessation**
- **Hand hygiene**
- Report worsening symptoms

### Tuberculosis (TB)

**Pathophysiology**: Infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis

**Types**:
- **Latent TB**: Infected but not active, asymptomatic, not contagious
- **Active TB**: Active infection, symptomatic, **contagious**

**Signs & Symptoms** (Active TB):
- **Persistent cough** (>3 weeks)
- **Hemoptysis** (coughing up blood)
- **Night sweats**
- **Weight loss**
- **Fatigue**
- **Low-grade fever**
- **Chest pain**

**Diagnostic**:
- **Tuberculin skin test (TST/PPD)**: Induration ‚â•10 mm (general), ‚â•5 mm (immunocompromised/HIV)
- **Interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA)**: Blood test
- **Chest X-ray**: Cavitation, infiltrates
- **Sputum culture**: Definitive diagnosis (3 consecutive early morning specimens)

**Treatment**:

**Active TB**: **Multi-drug therapy** (4 drugs for 2 months, then 2 drugs for 4 months)
- **R**ifampin
- **I**soniazid (INH)
- **P**yrazinamide
- **E**thambutol

**Mnemonic**: **"RIPE"**

**Medication Side Effects**:
- **Rifampin**: Orange-colored body fluids (urine, tears, sweat)
- **Isoniazid**: Peripheral neuropathy (give vitamin B6), hepatotoxicity
- **Pyrazinamide**: Hepatotoxicity
- **Ethambutol**: Optic neuritis (vision changes)

**Nursing Care**:

**Isolation**:
- **Airborne precautions**
- **Negative pressure room** (door closed)
- **N95 respirator** for staff/visitors
- Continue isolation until **3 negative sputum cultures**
- Patient wears **surgical mask** if leaving room

**Medication Adherence**:
- **DOT (Directly Observed Therapy)**: Watch patient take medications
- **Long treatment duration** (6-9 months)
- **Must complete full course**
- **Never stop early** (drug-resistant TB)

**Patient Teaching**:
- Cover mouth/nose when coughing
- Dispose of tissues properly
- Hand hygiene
- Adequate ventilation at home
- **Take medications daily** (set alarm/reminder)
- Report: Vision changes, jaundice, numbness/tingling

## üçÉ Gastrointestinal System

### Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

**Types**:
- **Gastric ulcer**: Stomach (pain DURING/AFTER meals)
- **Duodenal ulcer**: Duodenum (pain 2-3 hrs after meals, **relieved by food**)

**Causes**:
- **H. pylori infection** (most common)
- **NSAIDs** (aspirin, ibuprofen)
- **Stress**
- Smoking, alcohol

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Epigastric pain** (burning, gnawing)
- Timing varies by type
- Nausea, vomiting
- **Hematemesis** (coffee-ground emesis)
- **Melena** (black, tarry stools)

**Complications**:
- **Hemorrhage**: Hematemesis, melena, hypotension
- **Perforation**: Sudden, severe abdominal pain, rigid abdomen, absent bowel sounds
- **Obstruction**: Vomiting, distention

**Treatment**:

**Medications**:
- **PPIs** (omeprazole): Reduce acid
- **H2 blockers** (ranitidine): Reduce acid
- **Antibiotics** (if H. pylori): Triple therapy
- **Antacids**: Neutralize acid
- **Mucosal protectants** (sucralfate): Coat ulcer

**Nursing Care**:
- **Avoid irritants**: NSAIDs, alcohol, caffeine, smoking
- **Small, frequent meals**
- **Stress reduction**
- **Monitor for complications**: Bleeding (VS, stool color), perforation (sudden pain)

**If Bleeding**:
- **NPO**
- **Large-bore IV access** √ó 2
- **Fluid resuscitation**
- **Type and crossmatch**
- **NG tube** for lavage/decompression
- **Prepare for endoscopy**

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

#### Crohn's Disease

**Pathophysiology**: Transmural inflammation, **"skip lesions"** (patchy), **any part of GI tract** (mouth to anus)

**Classic Location**: **Terminal ileum**

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Diarrhea** (non-bloody typically)
- **Abdominal pain** (RLQ cramping)
- **Weight loss**, malabsorption
- **Fever**
- **Fistulas, abscesses** (transmural inflammation)
- **Perianal disease**

**Mnemonic**: **"NESTS"**
- **N**o blood (usually)
- **E**ntire GI tract (mouth to anus)
- **S**kip lesions (patchy)
- **T**ransmural (full thickness)
- **S**ubmucosal fibrosis

---

#### Ulcerative Colitis

**Pathophysiology**: Mucosal inflammation, **continuous** (no skip lesions), **rectum and colon only**

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Bloody diarrhea** (hallmark)
- **Frequent, urgent stools** (10-20/day)
- **Tenesmus** (painful urgency)
- **Abdominal cramping** (LLQ)
- **Weight loss**
- **Anemia** (blood loss)

**Mnemonic**: **"CLOSEUP"**
- **C**ontinuous lesions (no skip)
- **L**imited to colon and rectum
- **O**nly superficial (mucosal)
- **S**urgery is curative (total colectomy)
- **E**xtra manifestations rare
- **U**lcers
- **P**seudopolyps

---

**Comparison**:

| Feature | Crohn's Disease | Ulcerative Colitis |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Location** | Mouth to anus (any part) | **Colon and rectum only** |
| **Pattern** | **Skip lesions** (patchy) | **Continuous** |
| **Depth** | **Transmural** (full thickness) | **Mucosal** (superficial) |
| **Bloody diarrhea** | Less common | **Hallmark** |
| **Fistulas** | Common | Rare |
| **Surgery** | Not curative | **Curative** (colectomy) |

**Nursing Care for IBD**:

**Nutrition**:
- **High-protein, high-calorie**
- **Low-fiber** during flare-ups
- **Avoid triggers**: Dairy, spicy foods, caffeine, alcohol
- **TPN** (if severe malabsorption)
- **Vitamin/mineral supplements** (especially B12, iron, folate)

**Medications**:
- **Anti-inflammatories**: Aminosalicylates (sulfasalazine)
- **Corticosteroids**: Prednisone (acute flare-ups)
- **Immunosuppressants**: Azathioprine
- **Biologics**: Infliximab (anti-TNF)

**Monitoring**:
- **I&O, daily weights**
- **Stool frequency, consistency, blood**
- **Electrolytes** (diarrhea causes imbalances)
- **Perianal skin integrity**
- **Signs of complications**: Perforation, toxic megacolon, abscess

**Patient Teaching**:
- Identify trigger foods
- Stress management
- **Medication compliance**
- **Increased colon cancer risk** - regular colonoscopies

### Hepatic Encephalopathy

**Pathophysiology**: Liver failure ‚Üí ammonia not converted to urea ‚Üí ammonia accumulates in blood ‚Üí crosses blood-brain barrier ‚Üí CNS toxicity

**Precipitating Factors**:
- **GI bleeding** (blood breakdown ‚Üí ammonia)
- **Infection**
- **Constipation**
- **High protein intake**
- **Diuretics** (dehydration)

**Signs & Symptoms** (Progressive):

| Stage | Mental Status | Neuromuscular |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| **I** | Euphoria, depression, forgetfulness | Tremor, slurred speech |
| **II** | Confusion, drowsiness | **Asterixis (flapping tremor)** |
| **III** | Severe confusion, incoherent | Hyperreflexia |
| **IV** | **Coma** | Decerebrate posturing |

**Asterixis**: Ask patient to extend arms and dorsiflex wrists - hands flap

**Treatment**:
- **Lactulose** (PO or enema):
  - Traps ammonia in colon ‚Üí excreted in stool
  - Goal: 2-3 soft stools/day
  - Monitor for diarrhea (dehydration risk)
- **Neomycin** or **rifaximin**: Decrease ammonia-producing bacteria
- **Low-protein diet** (if indicated)
- **Treat precipitating factors**

**Nursing Care**:
- **Monitor mental status** (early detection of worsening)
- **Safety**: Fall precautions, reorient, supervision
- **Prevent constipation** (lactulose, stool softeners)
- **Monitor for GI bleeding** (stool for occult blood)
- **Adequate calories** (prevent catabolism)

## üíß Renal System

### Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

**Definition**: Sudden decrease in kidney function

**Phases**:

#### 1. Onset/Initiation Phase
- Injury occurs
- Few hours to several days
- May be preventable if treated early

#### 2. Oliguric Phase
- **Urine output <400 mL/day** (<0.5 mL/kg/hr)
- **Fluid overload**: Edema, hypertension, pulmonary edema, JVD
- **Electrolyte imbalances**:
  - **Hyperkalemia** (life-threatening)
  - Hyperphosphatemia
  - Hypocalcemia
  - Hypermagnesemia
- **Metabolic acidosis**
- **Uremia**: Nausea, fatigue, confusion
- **Elevated BUN and creatinine**
- Lasts 1-2 weeks

#### 3. Diuretic Phase
- **Urine output >400 mL/day** (can be 3-5 L/day)
- **Risk**: Dehydration, hypokalemia, hyponatremia
- Gradual improvement in labs
- Lasts 1-3 weeks

#### 4. Recovery Phase
- Kidney function improves
- May take months to 1 year
- May not return to baseline

**Nursing Management**:

**Oliguric Phase**:
- **Strict I&O**
- **Daily weights**
- **Fluid restriction** (500-600 mL + previous day's output)
- **Monitor electrolytes** (especially K+)
- **Cardiac monitoring** (hyperkalemia)
- **Low-potassium, low-sodium diet**
- **Protein restriction** (decrease BUN)
- **Dialysis** (if needed)

**Diuretic Phase**:
- **Generous fluid replacement** (prevent dehydration)
- **Monitor electrolytes** (hypokalemia, hyponatremia)
- **Supplement K+** as needed

**Indications for Dialysis** - **"AEIOU"**:
- **A**cidosis (metabolic, refractory)
- **E**lectrolyte imbalances (hyperkalemia)
- **I**ntoxication (drug overdose)
- **O**verload (fluid, refractory)
- **U**remia (pericarditis, encephalopathy)

---

### Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

**Definition**: Progressive, irreversible loss of kidney function

**Stages**: Based on GFR (glomerular filtration rate)
- Stage 1: GFR ‚â•90 (normal with kidney damage)
- Stage 2: GFR 60-89 (mild decrease)
- Stage 3: GFR 30-59 (moderate decrease)
- Stage 4: GFR 15-29 (severe decrease)
- **Stage 5**: GFR <15 (**End-stage renal disease - ESRD**)

**Complications**:

| System | Manifestations |
|--------|----------------|
| **Fluid/Electrolyte** | Fluid overload, hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia |
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypertension, heart failure, pericarditis, atherosclerosis |
| **Hematologic** | **Anemia** (‚Üì erythropoietin), bleeding tendency |
| **GI** | Anorexia, N/V, uremic fetor (ammonia breath), GI bleeding |
| **Neurologic** | Confusion, seizures, peripheral neuropathy, restless leg syndrome |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Renal osteodystrophy (bone disease), fractures |
| **Integumentary** | **Uremic frost** (white crystals on skin), pruritus, pallor |

**Treatment**:

**Medications**:
- **Erythropoietin** (epoetin alfa): Treat anemia
- **Phosphate binders**: Control hyperphosphatemia (take with meals)
- **Calcium/Vitamin D**: Treat hypocalcemia, prevent bone disease
- **Antihypertensives**: Control BP (ACE inhibitors preferred)

**Dialysis**:
- **Hemodialysis**: 3-4 times per week, 3-5 hours per session
- **Peritoneal dialysis**: Daily, can be done at home

**Transplantation**: Definitive treatment

**Dietary Management**:
- **Protein restriction** (0.6-0.8 g/kg/day)
- **Sodium restriction** (2 g/day)
- **Potassium restriction**
- **Phosphorus restriction**
- **Fluid restriction** (based on urine output + 500-750 mL/day)
- **Adequate calories** (prevent catabolism)

**Nursing Care**:
- **Monitor**: I&O, daily weights, vital signs, electrolytes
- **Assess**: Edema, lung sounds, mental status
- **Prevent infection** (immunosuppression if transplant)
- **Skin care** (pruritus, dryness)
- **Emotional support** (chronic, life-altering disease)

### Hemodialysis Nursing Care

**Vascular Access**:

| Type | Description | Maturation Time | Care |
|------|-------------|-----------------|------|
| **AV Fistula** | Artery-vein connection | 2-3 months | **Best option** - lowest infection risk |
| **AV Graft** | Synthetic tube connects artery-vein | 2-3 weeks | Higher infection risk than fistula |
| **Central Venous Catheter** | Temporary access | Immediate | Highest infection risk |

**Fistula/Graft Care** - **Critical**:
- **Assess for bruit and thrill** (every shift)
  - **Bruit**: Swooshing sound (auscultate with stethoscope)
  - **Thrill**: Vibration/buzzing (palpate gently)
- **NEVER**:
  - Take BP on access arm
  - Draw blood from access arm
  - Start IV on access arm
  - Constrict access arm (tight clothing, jewelry, BP cuff)
- **Teach patient**:
  - Palpate for thrill daily
  - Report: Numbness, tingling, coolness, decreased thrill
  - Avoid sleeping on access arm

**During Dialysis**:
- **Pre-dialysis**: Weight, vital signs, assess access
- **During**: Monitor for complications
- **Post-dialysis**: Weight, vital signs, assess for bleeding from access site

**Complications**:

| Complication | Signs | Intervention |
|--------------|-------|--------------|
| **Hypotension** | Dizziness, nausea, diaphoresis | Decrease ultrafiltration rate, give saline bolus, Trendelenburg |
| **Muscle cramps** | Painful muscle contractions | Decrease ultrafiltration rate, give saline, massage |
| **Disequilibrium syndrome** | Headache, N/V, confusion, seizures (rapid shift of fluids) | **Slow dialysis** (especially first treatments) |
| **Infection** | Fever, redness at access | Antibiotics, sterile technique |
| **Bleeding** | From access site | Apply pressure, assess for heparin excess |
| **Air embolism** | Dyspnea, chest pain | **Left side, Trendelenburg**, 100% O2 |

**Post-Dialysis**:
- **Hold antihypertensives** before dialysis (hypotension risk)
- **No heavy lifting** with access arm (24 hours)
- Monitor access site for bleeding

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### System-Specific Priorities

**Neurological**:
- **ABC first**, then neuro assessment
- **Stroke**: Time is brain - note symptom onset
- **Seizure**: Protect from injury, turn on side, time it
- **ICP**: HOB 30¬∞, midline head, prevent Valsalva

**Cardiovascular**:
- **Arterial = Legs DOWN, Venous = Legs UP**
- **Heart failure**: Daily weights, I&O, sodium restriction
- **Never take BP on AV fistula arm**

**Respiratory**:
- **Position**: High Fowler's for dyspnea
- **COPD**: Low-flow O2, target SpO2 88-92%
- **TB**: Airborne precautions, N95 respirator
- **Pneumonia**: Hydration, incentive spirometry

**GI**:
- **Peptic ulcer perforation**: Sudden severe pain, rigid abdomen = emergency
- **Hepatic encephalopathy**: Lactulose, low-protein diet
- **IBD**: Crohn's = skip lesions, fistulas; UC = bloody diarrhea, colon only

**Renal**:
- **AKI oliguric phase**: Fluid restriction, monitor K+
- **AKI diuretic phase**: Generous fluids, monitor electrolytes
- **CKD**: Protein/K+/Na+ restriction, phosphate binders with meals
- **Dialysis**: Assess fistula bruit/thrill, never use access arm for BP/IV

### Delegation

**Can Delegate to UAP**:
- Vital signs (stable patients)
- I&O measurement
- Daily weights
- Feeding (stable patients, no dysphagia)
- Ambulation assistance
- Hygiene care

**Cannot Delegate** (RN only):
- Initial assessment
- Patient teaching
- Medication administration
- Complex care (trach suction, wound care)
- Unstable patients

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient post-stroke has left-sided weakness. When transferring from bed to wheelchair, which direction should the nurse move the patient?</summary>

**Answer**: Toward the right side (unaffected side)

**Rationale**: Transfer stroke patients toward their unaffected (stronger) side. The patient can use the stronger side to assist with the transfer. Position wheelchair on unaffected side. For this patient with left weakness, transfer to the right.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient with COPD is on 2 L O2 via nasal cannula. SpO2 is 88%. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Document the finding and continue current oxygen

**Rationale**: Target SpO2 for COPD patients is 88-92% (not 95-100%). This SpO2 is within target range. COPD patients depend on hypoxic drive; excessive oxygen can suppress respiratory drive. Do NOT increase oxygen without provider order.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A nurse assesses an AV fistula and notes no bruit or thrill. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Notify the provider immediately

**Rationale**: Absence of bruit/thrill indicates clotted fistula - a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention. This is the patient's dialysis access. Never take BP, start IV, or draw blood from access arm. Assess bruit (auscultate) and thrill (palpate) every shift.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient with cirrhosis develops confusion and asterixis (flapping tremor). Which medication should the nurse prepare to administer?</summary>

**Answer**: Lactulose

**Rationale**: Confusion and asterixis indicate hepatic encephalopathy (elevated ammonia). Lactulose traps ammonia in the colon so it's excreted in stool. Goal: 2-3 soft stools per day. Also give neomycin (decreases ammonia-producing bacteria), restrict protein, and treat precipitating factors.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient with peripheral arterial disease asks about leg positioning. What should the nurse teach?</summary>

**Answer**: Keep legs in a dependent position (below heart level)

**Rationale**: Arterial insufficiency = poor blood flow TO legs. Gravity helps blood flow down to extremities. Keep legs DEPENDENT (dangling or down). Avoid elevation, crossing legs, tight clothing. This is opposite of venous insufficiency (elevate legs). Mnemonic: Arterial = DOWN, Venous = UP.
</details>
