# Rest & Sleep

## Overview
Adequate rest and sleep are essential for physical restoration, cognitive function, and emotional well-being. Understanding sleep stages, disorders, and nursing interventions to promote healthy sleep is crucial for holistic patient care and frequently tested on the NCLEX.

## ğŸ˜´ Sleep Physiology

### Sleep Stages

**NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep**:

| Stage | Characteristics | % of Sleep | EEG Waves |
|-------|----------------|------------|-----------|
| **NREM 1** | Light sleep, easily awakened, transition | 5% | Theta waves |
| **NREM 2** | Deeper sleep, body temp drops, HR slows | 45-55% | Sleep spindles, K-complexes |
| **NREM 3** | Deep sleep (slow-wave sleep), restorative, hard to wake | 15-25% | Delta waves |

**REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep**:
- **Characteristics**: Vivid dreams, rapid eye movements, muscle paralysis (except diaphragm, eyes), brain activity similar to waking
- **Percentage**: 20-25% of sleep
- **Function**: Memory consolidation, learning, emotional processing
- **EEG**: Beta waves (similar to awake)

## ğŸ” Factors Affecting Sleep

### Physiological Factors
- **Illness**: Pain, dyspnea, nocturia, fever
- **Medications**: Stimulants, steroids, some antidepressants
- **Age**: Changes in sleep architecture
- **Circadian rhythms**: Internal body clock

### Psychological Factors
- **Stress, anxiety**: Difficulty falling asleep
- **Depression**: Early morning awakening, hypersomnia
- **PTSD**: Nightmares, hypervigilance

### Environmental Factors
- **Noise**: Disrupts sleep cycles
- **Light**: Suppresses melatonin
- **Temperature**: Too hot or cold
- **Bed comfort**

### Lifestyle Factors
- **Caffeine**: Stimulant, half-life 5-6 hours
- **Alcohol**: Disrupts REM sleep, causes early awakening
- **Exercise**: Beneficial if not too close to bedtime
- **Screen time**: Blue light suppresses melatonin
- **Irregular schedule**: Disrupts circadian rhythm

### Hospital/Healthcare Environment
```
HOSPITAL SLEEP DISRUPTORS:
  â€¢ Noise (alarms, staff, equipment)
  â€¢ Light (hallway lights, vital sign checks)
  â€¢ Interruptions (Q2H turns, vitals, meds)
  â€¢ Pain, discomfort
  â€¢ Anxiety about illness
  â€¢ Unfamiliar environment
  â€¢ Uncomfortable bed
  â€¢ Roommate disturbances

### Sleep Apnea

**Definition**: Breathing repeatedly stops during sleep

**Types**:

| Type | Cause | Characteristics |
|------|-------|----------------|
| **Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)** | Airway collapse/obstruction | Most common; snoring, gasping |
| **Central Sleep Apnea** | Brain doesn't send signal to breathe | Less common; no snoring |
| **Mixed** | Combination | Both components present |

**Risk Factors**:
- **Obesity** (largest risk factor)
- Large neck circumference (>17" men, >16" women)
- Male gender
- Age >40
- Family history
- Smoking, alcohol
- Anatomical abnormalities (large tonsils, deviated septum)

**Assessment**:
```

  During sleep:
    â€¢ Loud snoring (pauses in snoring)
    â€¢ Gasping, choking sounds
    â€¢ Apnea episodes (witnessed by partner)
    â€¢ Restless sleep

  Daytime:
    â€¢ Excessive daytime sleepiness (most common complaint)
    â€¢ Morning headache
    â€¢ Dry mouth/sore throat upon waking
    â€¢ Difficulty concentrating
    â€¢ Irritability, mood changes
    â€¢ Decreased libido
```

**Complications**:
- **Cardiovascular**: HTN, arrhythmias, MI, stroke, heart failure
- **Metabolic**: Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome
- **Cognitive**: Memory problems, decreased concentration
- **Increased accident risk**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Polysomnography** (sleep study): Gold standard
- **Home sleep apnea testing**: Alternative
- Measures: Apnea-hypopnea index (AHI)

**Treatment**:
```

    âœ“ CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)

      â€¢ Keeps airway open with positive pressure

**CPAP Nursing Considerations**:
- Education on use, cleaning

- Nasal congestion, dry mouth common initially
- Humidifier attachment helps
- Compliance essential for effectiveness

### Narcolepsy

**Definition**: Chronic neurological disorder causing excessive daytime sleepiness and sudden sleep attacks

**Characteristics**:
- **Excessive daytime sleepiness**: Overwhelming urge to sleep
- **Cataplexy** (70% of patients): Sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by emotions (laughter, surprise)
- **Sleep paralysis**: Temporary inability to move upon waking/falling asleep
- **Hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations**: Vivid hallucinations when falling asleep or waking
- **Disrupted nighttime sleep**

**Treatment**:
- Stimulants (modafinil, methylphenidate)
- Sodium oxybate (for cataplexy)
- Scheduled naps
- Avoid driving when sleepy
- Regular sleep schedule

---

### Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS)

**Definition**: Uncomfortable sensations in legs with irresistible urge to move them

**Characteristics**:
- Unpleasant leg sensations (crawling, tingling, aching)
- Worse at rest, especially evening/night
- Relieved by movement
- Disrupts sleep onset

**Causes/Risk Factors**:
- Iron deficiency
- Pregnancy
- Chronic kidney disease
- Diabetes
- Medications (antidepressants, antihistamines)
- Genetics

**Treatment**:
- Treat underlying cause (iron supplementation if deficient)
- Avoid triggers (caffeine, alcohol)
- Leg massage, warm bath
- Moderate exercise
- Medications: Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole), gabapentin

---

### Circadian Rhythm Disorders

**Types**:

| Disorder | Description | Treatment |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Delayed Sleep Phase** | Can't fall asleep until late, hard to wake | Light therapy, melatonin, chronotherapy |
| **Advanced Sleep Phase** | Fall asleep early, wake early | Light therapy (evening) |
| **Shift Work Disorder** | Work during normal sleep time | Strategic naps, light exposure, sleep hygiene |
| **Jet Lag** | Crossing time zones | Adjust to new time zone, melatonin |

---

### Parasomnias

**Definition**: Abnormal behaviors during sleep

**Types**:
- **Sleepwalking** (somnambulism): Walking during sleep (NREM 3)
- **Night terrors**: Sudden arousal with intense fear, screaming (NREM 3) - common in children
- **Nightmares**: Frightening dreams (REM) - patient remembers
- **REM sleep behavior disorder**: Acting out dreams (lack of muscle paralysis)
- **Sleep talking** (somniloquy): Talking during sleep

**Management**:
- Safety measures (lock doors/windows, remove hazards)
- Stress reduction
- Regular sleep schedule
- Medications if severe

## ğŸ§˜ Relaxation Techniques

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation
1. Tense muscle group 5-10 seconds
2. Release and relax 10-20 seconds
3. Notice difference between tension and relaxation
4. Progress through all muscle groups (feet â†’ head)

### Deep Breathing
- **4-7-8 technique**:
  1. Exhale completely
  2. Inhale through nose (count 4)
  3. Hold breath (count 7)
  4. Exhale through mouth (count 8)
  5. Repeat 3-4 times

### Guided Imagery
- Visualize peaceful, calming scene
- Engage all senses
- Focus on details

### Meditation/Mindfulness
- Focus on present moment
- Observe thoughts without judgment
- Return attention to breath

## ğŸ’Š Sleep Medications

### Medication Classes

| Class | Examples | Mechanism | Considerations |
|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Benzodiazepines** | Temazepam, Triazolam | GABA agonist | Dependence risk, tolerance, withdrawal; avoid long-term |
| **Non-benzodiazepine hypnotics** | Zolpidem (Ambien), Eszopiclone (Lunesta) | GABA agonist (selective) | Less dependence than benzos; complex sleep behaviors possible |
| **Melatonin receptor agonists** | Ramelteon (Rozerem) | Melatonin receptor | No dependence, good for circadian issues |
| **Orexin receptor antagonists** | Suvorexant (Belsomra) | Block wakefulness system | Newer class, well-tolerated |
| **Sedating antidepressants** | Trazodone, Mirtazapine | Various | Off-label for sleep, less dependence |
| **Antihistamines** | Diphenhydramine (Benadryl), Doxylamine | Histamine antagonist | OTC, tolerance develops, anticholinergic effects |

**Nursing Considerations**:
- Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration
- Non-pharmacological interventions first
- Avoid in older adults when possible (falls risk, confusion)
- Take 30 minutes before bed
- Avoid alcohol
- Don't drive or operate machinery
- May cause morning drowsiness

## ğŸ¯ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Points

1. **REM sleep**: Vivid dreams, memory consolidation, muscle paralysis (except eyes/diaphragm)
2. **NREM 3 (deep sleep)**: Physical restoration, tissue repair, hard to wake
3. **Sleep deprivation effects**: Decreased concentration, impaired judgment, irritability
4. **Insomnia**: Non-pharmacological interventions FIRST (sleep hygiene, CBT-I)
5. **Sleep apnea**: CPAP is gold standard treatment
6. **Hospital sleep**: Average 4-5 hours (inadequate), cluster care to minimize interruptions
7. **Older adults**: Sleep architecture changes normal, but excessive daytime sleepiness is NOT
8. **Sleep hygiene**: Consistent schedule, dark/cool/quiet room, avoid screens/caffeine before bed
9. **If can't sleep after 20 min**: Get out of bed, do quiet activity
10. **Benzodiazepines**: Short-term only (dependence risk), avoid in elderly

### Priority Nursing Diagnoses
- Disturbed Sleep Pattern
- Insomnia
- Sleep Deprivation
- Fatigue
- Risk for Injury (related to sleepiness)

### Teaching Priorities
- Sleep hygiene education
- Medication compliance (CPAP for sleep apnea)
- Safety (no driving if excessively sleepy)
- When to seek help (persistent insomnia, loud snoring with pauses)

## âœ… Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>1. Which nursing intervention is MOST important for promoting sleep in a hospitalized patient?</summary>

**Answer**: Cluster nursing care activities to minimize interruptions during the night

**Rationale**: Frequent interruptions are a major cause of sleep disruption in hospitals. Clustering care activities (vital signs, medications, turning) reduces awakenings and allows for longer periods of uninterrupted sleep. This is more effective than individual comfort measures.
</details>

<details>
<summary>2. Patient reports difficulty falling asleep despite feeling tired. What should the nurse teach FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: "Establish a consistent sleep and wake schedule, even on weekends."

**Rationale**: Consistent sleep-wake times regulate circadian rhythm and are foundational to sleep hygiene. This is a first-line, non-pharmacological intervention for insomnia. Other sleep hygiene measures are also important, but schedule consistency is fundamental.
</details>

<details>
<summary>3. Patient with sleep apnea is prescribed CPAP. Which statement indicates understanding?</summary>

**Answer**: "I need to use the CPAP every night for it to be effective."

**Rationale**: CPAP must be used consistently (nightly) to be effective. Compliance is essential. Skipping nights allows apnea to continue and negates the benefits.
</details>

<details>
<summary>4. Which patient is at HIGHEST risk for sleep apnea?</summary>

**Answer**: 55-year-old obese male who snores loudly

**Rationale**: Obesity is the largest risk factor for obstructive sleep apnea. Male gender, age >40, and loud snoring are also risk factors. This patient has multiple risk factors.
</details>

<details>
<summary>5. Older adult patient complains of waking frequently during the night. What is the nurse's BEST response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Some changes in sleep patterns are normal with aging. Let's discuss strategies to improve your sleep quality."

**Rationale**: Age-related sleep changes (lighter sleep, more awakenings) are normal. The nurse should acknowledge this while offering interventions to improve sleep quality. Excessive daytime sleepiness is NOT normal and should be evaluated.
</details>

<details>
<summary>6. Patient taking a benzodiazepine for sleep asks about stopping the medication. What should the nurse teach?</summary>

**Answer**: "Don't stop suddenly. Work with your doctor to gradually reduce the dose to prevent withdrawal."

**Rationale**: Benzodiazepines can cause physical dependence. Abrupt discontinuation can cause withdrawal symptoms (anxiety, tremors, seizures). Must taper gradually under medical supervision.
</details>

<details>
<summary>7. During which sleep stage does most physical restoration occur?</summary>

**Answer**: NREM Stage 3 (deep sleep)

**Rationale**: NREM Stage 3 (slow-wave sleep) is when physical restoration occurs: tissue repair, growth, immune function, energy restoration. This is the deepest stage of sleep.
</details>

<details>
<summary>8. Patient reports inability to sleep unless lying on back. Nurse notes loud snoring and gasping. What should the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Sleep apnea

**Rationale**: Loud snoring with gasping sounds strongly suggests sleep apnea. Supine position often worsens obstructive sleep apnea. Patient should be evaluated with sleep study.
</details>
