# Medical Emergencies

## Overview
Rapid recognition and appropriate intervention in medical emergencies can save lives. This guide covers emergency care, types of shock, code management, and trauma care essential for the NCLEX.

---

## üö® Emergency Assessment - Primary Survey (ABC)

### The ABCs of Emergency Care

**Primary Survey** - Assess and intervene in order:

1. **A - Airway**
   - Is airway patent?
   - Look for: Stridor, choking, inability to speak
   - Intervention: Clear airway, head-tilt/chin-lift, jaw thrust (if trauma)

2. **B - Breathing**
   - Is patient breathing adequately?
   - Look for: Respiratory rate, depth, effort, O2 sat
   - Intervention: Oxygen, ventilation support

3. **C - Circulation**
   - Is patient perfusing adequately?
   - Look for: Pulse, BP, capillary refill, skin color/temp
   - Intervention: IV access, fluids, control bleeding

4. **D - Disability** (Neurological Status)
   - Level of consciousness (AVPU: Alert, Verbal, Pain, Unresponsive)
   - Pupil response
   - Glasgow Coma Scale

5. **E - Exposure/Environment**
   - Fully expose patient (look for injuries)
   - Prevent hypothermia (warm blankets)

**Remember**: **Don't move to next step until current step is stabilized!**

## üíî Cardiac Emergencies

### Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI/Heart Attack)

**Pathophysiology**: Coronary artery occlusion ‚Üí myocardial ischemia ‚Üí tissue death

**Classic Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Chest pain/pressure**:
  - Substernal, crushing, "elephant on chest"
  - Radiates to left arm, jaw, neck, back
  - **Lasts >20 minutes** (not relieved by rest or nitroglycerin)
- Dyspnea
- Diaphoresis
- Nausea, vomiting
- Anxiety, sense of impending doom
- Pallor, cool/clammy skin

**Atypical Presentations** (especially women, elderly, diabetics):
- Fatigue
- Indigestion, epigastric pain
- Shortness of breath without chest pain
- Shoulder/back pain

**Diagnostic**:
- **ECG**: ST elevation (STEMI) or ST depression (NSTEMI)
- **Cardiac enzymes**: Troponin (gold standard), CK-MB
- Cardiac catheterization

**Immediate Nursing Actions** - **"MONA"**:

1. **M**orphine - pain relief, reduces anxiety, vasodilation
2. **O**xygen - if SpO2 <90% or dyspneic
3. **N**itroglycerin - sublingual (0.4 mg q5min √ó 3 doses)
   - **Hold if SBP <90 mmHg**
   - **Contraindicated**: Viagra/erectile dysfunction drugs (within 24-48 hrs)
4. **A**spirin - 160-325 mg chewable (antiplatelet)

**Additional Interventions**:
- **Call Rapid Response/activate cath lab**
- **12-lead ECG** within 10 minutes
- **IV access** (2 large-bore IVs)
- **Continuous cardiac monitoring**
- **Bedrest**, calm environment
- **Prepare for**:
  - **Fibrinolytics** (tPA) if STEMI and cath lab not available (<12 hrs from symptom onset)
  - **Cardiac catheterization** (PCI - percutaneous coronary intervention)

**Post-MI Care**:
- Beta blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins
- Anticoagulation (heparin, then warfarin or DOAC)
- Cardiac rehabilitation
- Risk factor modification

### Cardiac Arrest/Code Blue

**No pulse, no breathing**

**Immediate Actions** - **"CAB"** (Changed from ABC):

1. **C - Circulation**: Start chest compressions IMMEDIATELY
2. **A - Airway**: Open airway
3. **B - Breathing**: Rescue breaths

**High-Quality CPR**:
- **Rate**: 100-120 compressions/minute
- **Depth**: At least 2 inches (5 cm) in adults
- **Location**: Lower half of sternum (center of chest)
- **Allow full chest recoil** between compressions
- **Minimize interruptions** (<10 seconds)
- **Compression-to-ventilation ratio**: 30:2 (if no advanced airway)
- **Switch compressors every 2 minutes** (prevent fatigue)

**Defibrillation** (AED/Manual):
- **Analyze rhythm every 2 minutes**
- **Shockable rhythms**: V-fib, pulseless V-tach
  - **Immediate defibrillation** (single shock)
  - Resume CPR immediately after shock
- **Non-shockable rhythms**: Asystole, PEA (pulseless electrical activity)
  - Continue CPR, give epinephrine

**Medications** (IV/IO):
- **Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO**: Every 3-5 minutes
- **Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO**: For refractory V-fib/V-tach (after 2nd shock)
- **Vasopressin**: Alternative to epinephrine

**Advanced Airway**:
- Endotracheal intubation or supraglottic airway
- Once placed: **Continuous compressions** (100-120/min), 1 breath every 6 seconds

**Post-Resuscitation Care**:
- Transfer to ICU
- Therapeutic hypothermia (if indicated)
- Monitor for complications

### Arrhythmias

#### Ventricular Fibrillation (V-fib)

**Chaotic, quivering ventricles - NO cardiac output**

**Treatment**: **IMMEDIATE DEFIBRILLATION**

---

#### Ventricular Tachycardia (V-tach)

**Rapid ventricular rate (>100 bpm), wide QRS**

**With Pulse** (Stable):
- Oxygen
- IV access
- **Amiodarone** or **lidocaine** IV
- Synchronized cardioversion if unstable

**Pulseless V-tach**:
- **Same as V-fib**: Immediate defibrillation + CPR

---

#### Asystole (Flatline)

**No electrical activity**

**Treatment**:
- **CPR** (NOT shockable)
- **Epinephrine**
- Identify/treat reversible causes (**H's and T's**):

**H's**:
- **H**ypovolemia
- **H**ypoxia
- **H**ydrogen ion (acidosis)
- **H**ypokalemia/Hyperkalemia
- **H**ypothermia

**T's**:
- **T**oxins
- **T**amponade (cardiac)
- **T**ension pneumothorax
- **T**hrombosis (coronary/pulmonary)
- **T**rauma

## ü©∏ Types of Shock

### Overview of Shock

**Definition**: Inadequate tissue perfusion ‚Üí cellular hypoxia ‚Üí organ dysfunction

**Stages**:
1. **Initial**: Compensatory mechanisms activated
2. **Compensatory**: Body attempts to maintain perfusion (BP normal initially)
3. **Progressive**: Compensatory mechanisms failing (hypotension develops)
4. **Refractory**: Irreversible, organ failure, death

**Common Signs** (Progressive/Decompensated Shock):
- Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)
- Tachycardia
- Tachypnea
- Cool, clammy skin
- Decreased urine output (<30 mL/hr)
- Altered mental status (restlessness ‚Üí lethargy)
- Weak, thready pulse

---

### Hypovolemic Shock

**Cause**: **Decreased circulating blood volume**

**Types**:
- **Hemorrhagic**: Blood loss (trauma, GI bleed, surgery)
- **Non-hemorrhagic**: Fluid loss (vomiting, diarrhea, burns, diuretics)

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Hypotension, tachycardia
- Cool, pale, clammy skin
- Decreased urine output
- Flat neck veins
- Weak, thready pulse
- Thirst
- Decreased CVP (central venous pressure)

**Treatment**:
- **Control bleeding** (if hemorrhagic)
- **Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation**:
  - 2-3 L isotonic crystalloid (NS, LR)
  - Large-bore IVs (14-16 gauge) √ó 2
- **Blood transfusion** (if hemorrhagic)
- **Position flat or Trendelenburg** (feet elevated 20¬∞)
- **Oxygen**
- **Monitor**: VS, urine output, LOC
- **Vasopressors** (if fluid resuscitation inadequate)

**Goals**:
- MAP >65 mmHg
- Urine output >30 mL/hr
- Improved mental status

---

### Cardiogenic Shock

**Cause**: **Heart failure** - pump cannot effectively circulate blood

**Common Causes**:
- Acute MI (most common)
- Severe heart failure
- Cardiomyopathy
- Dysrhythmias

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Hypotension, tachycardia
- **Pulmonary edema**: Dyspnea, crackles, frothy sputum
- **Distended neck veins (JVD)**
- **Cool, clammy skin**
- Decreased urine output
- Chest pain (if MI)
- S3 heart sound

**Treatment**:
- **Position**: High Fowler's (reduce preload, ease breathing)
- **Oxygen** (high-flow)
- **Treat underlying cause**:
  - MI ‚Üí Cardiac cath, revascularization
  - Arrhythmia ‚Üí Antiarrhythmics, cardioversion
- **Medications**:
  - **Inotropes** (dobutamine, milrinone) - increase contractility
  - **Vasopressors** (norepinephrine, dopamine) - if hypotensive
  - **Diuretics** (furosemide) - reduce fluid overload
- **Intra-aortic balloon pump (IABP)** or **mechanical circulatory support**
- **Avoid excessive fluid** (worsens pulmonary edema)

**Critical Difference from Hypovolemic**:
- **JVD present** in cardiogenic (fluid backs up)
- **JVD absent** in hypovolemic (volume depleted)

---

### Distributive Shock (Vasodilation)

#### Septic Shock

**Cause**: Overwhelming infection ‚Üí systemic inflammatory response ‚Üí vasodilation

**Pathophysiology**: Bacteria/toxins ‚Üí massive vasodilation + increased capillary permeability

**Risk Factors**:
- Immunosuppression
- Invasive procedures/devices
- Burns, wounds
- Chronic illness (diabetes, cancer)

**Stages**:

**Early (Warm/Hyperdynamic)**:
- **Warm, flushed skin**
- **Bounding pulse**
- Fever or hypothermia
- Tachycardia, tachypnea
- Normal or high BP initially
- Normal or increased urine output

**Late (Cold/Hypodynamic)**:
- Cool, clammy skin
- Hypotension
- Decreased urine output
- Altered mental status
- Multiple organ dysfunction

**Diagnostic Criteria**:
- **Suspected/confirmed infection**
- **2 or more SIRS criteria**:
  - Temp >100.4¬∞F or <96.8¬∞F
  - HR >90 bpm
  - RR >20 or PaCO2 <32
  - WBC >12,000 or <4,000
- **Organ dysfunction** (lactate >2, hypotension, altered mental status)

**Treatment** - **"Sepsis Six"** (within 1 hour):
1. **Oxygen** (target SpO2 >94%)
2. **Blood cultures** (before antibiotics)
3. **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics**
4. **IV fluid resuscitation** (30 mL/kg crystalloid)
5. **Measure lactate**
6. **Measure urine output** (Foley catheter)

**Additional**:
- **Vasopressors** (norepinephrine) if hypotensive after fluids
- **Source control** (drain abscess, remove infected device)
- **ICU monitoring**

**Critical**: **Early recognition and treatment = survival!** ("Hour 1 Bundle")

---

#### Anaphylactic Shock

**Cause**: Severe allergic reaction ‚Üí massive histamine release ‚Üí vasodilation

**Common Triggers**: Foods (peanuts, shellfish), medications (penicillin, NSAIDs), insect stings, latex

**Signs & Symptoms** (Rapid onset - minutes):
- **Respiratory**: Dyspnea, wheezing, stridor, laryngeal edema
- **Cardiovascular**: Hypotension, tachycardia, weak pulse
- **Integumentary**: Urticaria (hives), angioedema (face/lips/tongue swelling), pruritus
- **GI**: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramping
- **Neurological**: Anxiety, sense of impending doom

**Treatment** - **"STOP-CALL-EPI"**:
1. **STOP** causative agent
2. **CALL** for help (Rapid Response)
3. **EPI** - **Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM** (anterolateral thigh)
   - **Repeat q5-15min** if needed
4. **Airway management** (prepare for intubation)
5. **Oxygen** (high-flow)
6. **Position**: Supine with legs elevated (modified Trendelenburg)
7. **IV fluids** (aggressive - 1-2 L NS bolus)
8. **Secondary medications**:
   - Antihistamines (diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV)
   - H2 blocker (ranitidine)
   - Corticosteroids (prevent biphasic reaction)
   - Bronchodilators (albuterol) if bronchospasm

**Critical**: **Epinephrine is FIRST-LINE** - don't wait for IV access!

---

#### Neurogenic Shock

**Cause**: Spinal cord injury (usually C5 or above) ‚Üí loss of sympathetic tone ‚Üí vasodilation

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Hypotension**
- **Bradycardia** (loss of sympathetic stimulation)
- **Warm, dry skin** (below injury level)
- **Hypothermia** (loss of temperature regulation)
- **Flaccid paralysis** below injury

**Treatment**:
- **IV fluid resuscitation** (cautiously - risk of fluid overload)
- **Vasopressors** (dopamine, norepinephrine)
- **Atropine** for bradycardia
- **Spinal stabilization**
- **Prevent complications**: DVT prophylaxis, pressure injury prevention

---

### Obstructive Shock

**Cause**: Physical obstruction of blood flow

**Types**:
- **Cardiac tamponade**: Fluid in pericardial sac compresses heart
- **Tension pneumothorax**: Air in pleural space compresses heart/great vessels
- **Massive pulmonary embolism**: Clot blocks pulmonary artery

**Treatment**: **Remove obstruction**
- Cardiac tamponade ‚Üí **Pericardiocentesis**
- Tension pneumothorax ‚Üí **Needle decompression/chest tube**
- Pulmonary embolism ‚Üí **Anticoagulation/thrombolytics**

## ü´Å Respiratory Emergencies

### Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

**Pathophysiology**: Acute lung injury ‚Üí increased capillary permeability ‚Üí fluid in alveoli ‚Üí impaired gas exchange

**Causes** (think: **"ARDS"**):
- **A**spiration
- **R**adiation, **R**e-expansion pulmonary edema
- **D**rugs/toxins (heroin, smoke inhalation)
- **S**epsis, **S**hock, **S**evere pneumonia, **S**urgery (thoracic)

**Also**: Trauma, pancreatitis, massive transfusion, drowning

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Severe dyspnea**, tachypnea
- **Refractory hypoxemia** (doesn't improve with oxygen)
- Decreased lung compliance (stiff lungs)
- Bilateral infiltrates on chest X-ray ("white out")
- Crackles
- Cyanosis

**Treatment**:
- **Mechanical ventilation** (usually required)
  - **Low tidal volumes** (6 mL/kg) - lung protective strategy
  - **PEEP** (positive end-expiratory pressure) - keep alveoli open
- **Prone positioning** (improves oxygenation)
- **Treat underlying cause**
- **Fluid management** (balance - avoid overload but maintain perfusion)
- **Sedation/paralysis** (if needed for ventilator synchrony)

---

### Tension Pneumothorax

**Medical emergency** - air in pleural space ‚Üí lung collapse ‚Üí mediastinal shift ‚Üí cardiac compromise

**Causes**: Trauma, mechanical ventilation, central line insertion

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Severe dyspnea**, tachypnea
- **Chest pain** (affected side)
- **Tracheal deviation** (away from affected side)
- **Absent breath sounds** on affected side
- **Hyperresonance** to percussion
- **Hypotension**, tachycardia (late - cardiac compromise)
- **Distended neck veins**
- **Subcutaneous emphysema** (crepitus)

**Treatment**:
- **EMERGENCY**: **Needle decompression** (2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line)
- **Chest tube insertion** (definitive treatment)
- **Oxygen**
- Monitor vital signs

---

### Status Asthmaticus

**Severe, life-threatening asthma exacerbation unresponsive to treatment**

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Severe dyspnea**, use of accessory muscles
- **Inability to speak in full sentences**
- **Decreased/absent breath sounds** (no air movement - ominous sign!)
- Wheezing (may be absent if severe airway obstruction)
- Tachypnea, tachycardia
- **Peak flow <50% predicted**
- **Anxiety**, altered mental status (hypoxia)
- **Tripod position**

**Treatment**:
- **High-flow oxygen**
- **Bronchodilators**:
  - **Continuous albuterol nebulizer**
  - Ipratropium (Atrovent)
- **Systemic corticosteroids** (methylprednisolone IV)
- **Magnesium sulfate IV** (bronchodilator)
- **Heliox** (helium-oxygen mixture)
- **Intubation/mechanical ventilation** if respiratory failure

**Critical Sign**: **"Silent chest"** (no breath sounds) = impending respiratory arrest!

## üß† Neurological Emergencies

### Stroke (CVA - Cerebrovascular Accident)

**Time = Brain!** - "Time is Brain"

**Types**:
- **Ischemic** (87%): Clot blocks blood flow
- **Hemorrhagic** (13%): Bleeding into brain

**Signs & Symptoms** - **"BE FAST"**:
- **B**alance - sudden loss of balance/coordination
- **E**yes - vision changes
- **F**acial droop - one side of face droops (ask to smile)
- **A**rm weakness - one arm drifts down (raise both arms)
- **S**peech difficulty - slurred speech, unable to repeat phrase
- **T**ime - time to call 911!

**Additional Signs**:
- Sudden severe headache (hemorrhagic)
- Numbness/weakness (unilateral)
- Confusion

**Immediate Nursing Actions**:
1. **Activate stroke protocol/code stroke**
2. **ABC assessment**
3. **Note time of symptom onset** (critical for tPA eligibility)
4. **Keep patient NPO** (dysphagia risk)
5. **Obtain vitals** (don't lower BP unless severely elevated)
6. **Elevate head of bed 30¬∞**
7. **Establish IV access** (NS)
8. **Blood glucose** (rule out hypoglycemia)
9. **CT head STAT** (rule out hemorrhage before tPA)

**Ischemic Stroke Treatment**:
- **tPA (alteplase)** - if within **3-4.5 hours** of symptom onset AND no bleeding on CT
  - **Contraindications**: Recent surgery, bleeding, anticoagulation, hemorrhagic stroke
  - **During/After tPA**: Neuro checks q15min, monitor for bleeding, **NO anticoagulants/antiplatelets for 24 hours**
- **Mechanical thrombectomy** (if large vessel occlusion, <24 hours)
- **Aspirin** (if not receiving tPA)

**Hemorrhagic Stroke Treatment**:
- **Reverse anticoagulation** (if applicable)
- **Blood pressure management** (prevent further bleeding)
- **Surgical intervention** (if indicated)

---

### Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

**Normal ICP**: 5-15 mmHg

**Causes**: Head trauma, brain tumor, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, stroke

**Signs & Symptoms**:

**Early**:
- Headache
- Nausea, vomiting (projectile)
- Blurred vision, diplopia
- Restlessness, irritability

**Late** (Cushing's Triad - ominous sign):
1. **Hypertension** with widening pulse pressure
2. **Bradycardia**
3. **Irregular respirations** (Cheyne-Stokes)

**Additional Late Signs**:
- Decreased level of consciousness
- **Pupil changes**: Dilated, sluggish, unequal
- **Posturing**:
  - **Decorticate**: Flexion of arms, extension of legs (cortex damage)
  - **Decerebrate**: Extension of arms and legs (brainstem damage - worse)

**Treatment**:
- **Elevate head of bed 30¬∞** (promote venous drainage)
- **Maintain head in midline** (no flexion/rotation)
- **Hyperosmolar therapy**: Mannitol or hypertonic saline (reduce cerebral edema)
- **Prevent increased ICP**:
  - Avoid Valsalva (straining)
  - Stool softeners
  - **Limit suctioning** (<10 seconds)
  - Avoid hip flexion >90¬∞
  - **Quiet environment**, limit visitors
- **Maintain normothermia** (fever increases ICP)
- **Sedation** (reduce metabolic demand)
- **Mechanical ventilation** (if needed) - maintain PaCO2 30-35 (hyperventilation causes vasoconstriction)

---

### Seizures & Status Epilepticus

**Status Epilepticus**: Continuous seizure >5 minutes OR ‚â•2 seizures without full recovery between

**Medical emergency** - can cause brain damage, death

**During Seizure**:
1. **Protect from injury**:
   - Turn on side (prevent aspiration)
   - Loosen tight clothing
   - Cushion head
   - Remove nearby objects
2. **DO NOT**:
   - Restrain
   - Put anything in mouth
   - Force mouth open
3. **Time the seizure**
4. **Observe** seizure activity (for documentation)
5. **After seizure**: Position on side, suction if needed, allow to rest

**Status Epilepticus Treatment**:
1. **Airway management** (oxygen, suction, possible intubation)
2. **IV access**
3. **Benzodiazepines**:
   - **Lorazepam (Ativan) 4 mg IV** OR
   - **Diazepam (Valium) 10 mg IV**
   - Repeat once if seizure continues
4. **If continues**: **Fosphenytoin or phenytoin IV**
5. **If still continues**: **Phenobarbital, propofol, or midazolam** (may need intubation)
6. **Check glucose** (treat hypoglycemia if present)
7. **Monitor**: ABCs, vital signs, neuro status

## ü©∏ Trauma Emergencies

### Hemorrhage Control

**External Bleeding**:
1. **Direct pressure** (most important)
2. **Elevate** extremity (if no fracture)
3. **Pressure dressing**
4. **Pressure points** (if direct pressure ineffective)
5. **Tourniquet** (last resort - life over limb)

**Internal Bleeding**:
- Signs: Hypotension, tachycardia, abdominal distention/rigidity, decreased LOC
- Treatment: IV access, fluid resuscitation, blood transfusion, surgery

---

### Hypovolemic Shock from Trauma

**Classes of Hemorrhage**:

| Class | Blood Loss | HR | BP | Mental Status |
|-------|------------|----|----|---------------|
| **I** | <15% (<750 mL) | Normal | Normal | Anxious |
| **II** | 15-30% (750-1500 mL) | >100 | Normal | Anxious |
| **III** | 30-40% (1500-2000 mL) | >120 | Decreased | Confused |
| **IV** | >40% (>2000 mL) | >140 | Severely decreased | Lethargic |

**Treatment**: See Hypovolemic Shock section

---

### Spinal Cord Injury

**PRIORITY**: **Immobilization** - prevent further injury

**Cervical Spine Precautions**:
- **Assume spinal injury until ruled out** in trauma patients
- **Maintain in-line stabilization**
- **Rigid cervical collar**
- **Log roll only** (minimum 4 people)
- **Backboard**

**Spinal Shock** (immediately after injury):
- Flaccid paralysis below level of injury
- Loss of reflexes
- Loss of sensation
- Bradycardia, hypotension
- Inability to regulate temperature

**Autonomic Dysreflexia** (T6 or above, after spinal shock resolves):
- **Medical emergency**
- **Triggers**: Bladder distention (most common), bowel impaction, tight clothing
- **Signs**: **Severe hypertension**, pounding headache, bradycardia, flushing above injury, pallor below injury, diaphoresis
- **Treatment**:
  1. **Sit patient upright** (lower BP)
  2. **Remove stimulus** (check catheter, empty bladder, remove tight clothing)
  3. **Loosen clothing**
  4. **Antihypertensives** (if needed)

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### Priority Actions in Emergencies

**Always ABC first!**

**Code Blue**: CPR ‚Üí Defibrillation (if shockable) ‚Üí Epinephrine

**MI**: **MONA** - Morphine, Oxygen (if needed), Nitroglycerin, Aspirin

**Stroke**: Time of symptom onset, CT head, **tPA if within 3-4.5 hours**

**Anaphylaxis**: **Epinephrine IM first** (0.3-0.5 mg)

**Shock**: Identify type ‚Üí Treat underlying cause + support circulation

### High-Yield Points

1. **"Time is brain"** - stroke care
2. **"Time is muscle"** - MI care
3. **Chest compressions FIRST** in cardiac arrest (CAB, not ABC)
4. **Defibrillation for V-fib/pulseless V-tach**
5. **Epinephrine first for anaphylaxis** - IM, not IV
6. **JVD present** = cardiogenic shock; **JVD absent** = hypovolemic shock
7. **Sepsis Six within 1 hour**
8. **Cushing's Triad** = Late sign of increased ICP (bad!)
9. **Silent chest in asthma** = Impending respiratory arrest
10. **Autonomic dysreflexia** = Sit up + remove stimulus

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient is found unresponsive with no pulse. What is the FIRST action?</summary>

**Answer**: Begin chest compressions immediately

**Rationale**: CAB approach - Compressions first! High-quality CPR with minimal interruptions is the priority. Compress at 100-120/min, depth at least 2 inches. Call for help/activate code. Apply AED/defibrillator as soon as available. Establish airway and breathing after compressions started.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient with suspected MI has chest pain unrelieved by 3 nitroglycerin doses. BP is 88/50. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold further nitroglycerin and notify provider

**Rationale**: Nitroglycerin is contraindicated when SBP <90 mmHg (causes further hypotension). Continue other interventions: oxygen (if needed), aspirin, morphine for pain. This patient may be in cardiogenic shock. Call Rapid Response, prepare for cardiac catheterization.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A trauma patient has hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds, and narrow pulse pressure. What does the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Cardiac tamponade

**Rationale**: Beck's triad: hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds. This is obstructive shock - fluid in pericardial sac compresses the heart. Medical emergency requiring pericardiocentesis. Narrow pulse pressure is also a key finding.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient develops sudden severe hypertension (200/110), pounding headache, and bradycardia. Patient has T6 spinal cord injury. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Sit the patient upright and check for bladder distention

**Rationale**: This is autonomic dysreflexia - a medical emergency in spinal cord injury T6 or above. Priority: sit patient up (lowers BP) and remove triggering stimulus (usually full bladder or bowel impaction). Check catheter patency, empty bladder. If not relieved, give antihypertensives. Can cause stroke if untreated.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient in anaphylaxis is dyspneic with audible wheezing and hypotension. What is the priority medication?</summary>

**Answer**: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM

**Rationale**: Epinephrine is the FIRST-LINE treatment for anaphylaxis. Give IM in anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis). Do NOT wait for IV access. Epinephrine causes vasoconstriction (increases BP), bronchodilation (improves breathing), and stabilizes mast cells. Antihistamines and steroids are secondary treatments.
</details>
