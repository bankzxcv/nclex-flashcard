# Medication Administration

## Overview
Safe medication administration is a fundamental nursing responsibility and one of the most heavily tested areas on the NCLEX. Understanding the rights of medication administration, routes, and safety principles is essential.

---

## üíä The Rights of Medication Administration

### Traditional 5 Rights
1. **Right Patient** - Verify using 2 identifiers
2. **Right Medication** - Check label 3 times
3. **Right Dose** - Calculate carefully
4. **Right Route** - Verify ordered route
5. **Right Time** - Administer at scheduled time

### Expanded Rights (10 Rights)
6. **Right Documentation** - Document immediately after administration
7. **Right Reason** - Know why patient is receiving medication
8. **Right to Refuse** - Patient can refuse; document and notify provider
9. **Right Assessment** - Assess before administering (BP before antihypertensive)
10. **Right Evaluation** - Evaluate response and effectiveness

---

## üîç Medication Safety

### Before Administration - The 3 Checks

1. **Check #1**: When removing medication from storage
2. **Check #2**: When preparing medication
3. **Check #3**: Before returning medication to storage OR at bedside before administration

### Patient Identification
**Verify using 2 identifiers**:
- Full name
- Date of birth
- Medical record number
- **NEVER** use room number

**Best Practice**: Scan patient armband barcode + ask patient to state name and DOB

### High-Alert Medications (Require Extra Caution)
- **Insulin**
- **Heparin** and anticoagulants
- **Opioids**
- **Chemotherapy**
- **Sedatives**
- **Potassium** (IV)
- **Neuromuscular blockers**

**Safety measures**: Double-check calculations, independent verification by second nurse

## üíâ Routes of Administration

### Oral (PO)

**Advantages**: Convenient, safe, economical
**Disadvantages**: Slower onset, cannot use if NPO/vomiting/unconscious

**Administration Tips**:
- Give with water unless contraindicated
- Stay and watch patient swallow (don't leave at bedside)
- Crush tablets only if not enteric-coated or extended-release
- Empty capsules only if appropriate
- Never crush: ER, SR, XL, CR, EC (extended/sustained release, enteric-coated)

**Special Considerations**:
- **Sublingual** (SL): Under tongue, do not swallow (e.g., nitroglycerin)
- **Buccal**: Between cheek and gum

### Intramuscular (IM)

**Common sites**:
- **Ventrogluteal** (preferred for adults - safer, less nerves/vessels)
- **Deltoid** (vaccines, small volumes <1 mL)
- **Vastus lateralis** (infants, children)
- **Dorsogluteal** (avoid due to sciatic nerve risk)

**Technique**:
- **Needle size**: 1-1.5 inch, 21-25 gauge
- **Angle**: 90 degrees
- **Volume**: Up to 3 mL adults, 1 mL deltoid
- **Z-track method**: For irritating medications (pull skin laterally before insertion)
- **Aspirate?**: Generally NO (current evidence), except if giving in dorsogluteal

**Site Selection**:
```
Ventrogluteal Landmark:
- Place heel of hand on greater trochanter
- Point fingers toward patient's head
- Index finger on anterior superior iliac spine
- Middle finger toward iliac crest
- Inject in center of triangle
```

### Subcutaneous (SubQ/SC)

**Common sites**: Abdomen (fastest absorption), outer arm, thigh, back

**Technique**:
- **Needle size**: 5/8 inch, 25-27 gauge
- **Angle**: 45-90 degrees (depending on needle length and adipose tissue)
- **Volume**: 0.5-1 mL maximum
- **Rotate sites**: Especially for insulin (prevents lipodystrophy)
- **Do NOT aspirate** (can cause tissue damage)
- **Do NOT massage** after injection (alters absorption)

**Common SubQ medications**:
- Insulin
- Heparin
- Lovenox (enoxaparin)
- Epinephrine

**Insulin Administration**:
- Rotate sites within same anatomical area
- Abdomen absorbs fastest, then arms, then thighs
- Give 30 minutes before meals (regular insulin)
- Give 15 minutes before meals (rapid-acting)

### Intravenous (IV)

**Advantages**: Immediate onset, 100% bioavailability, precise control
**Disadvantages**: Risk of infection, phlebitis, infiltration, extravasation

**IV Push (Bolus)**:
- Administer slowly over specified time
- Check compatibility with existing IV fluids
- Flush before and after with saline

**IV Piggyback (IVPB)**:
- Hang secondary bag higher than primary
- Back-prime to prevent air
- Common for antibiotics

**Continuous Infusion**:
- Use infusion pump for accuracy
- Monitor site frequently
- Label tubing with date/time

**Complications**:

| Complication | Signs | Intervention |
|--------------|-------|--------------|
| **Infiltration** | Swelling, coolness, pallor, pain | Stop IV, elevate extremity, warm compress |
| **Phlebitis** | Warmth, redness, pain along vein | Stop IV, apply warm compress |
| **Extravasation** | Severe tissue damage from vesicant | STOP immediately, don't flush, notify provider, antidote if available |
| **Air embolism** | Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypotension | Place on LEFT side in Trendelenburg |
| **Infection** | Redness, warmth, drainage at site | Remove IV, culture site if ordered |

## üßÆ Dosage Calculations

### Basic Formula Method
```
Desired Dose / Have on Hand √ó Vehicle (tablet, mL) = Amount to Give
```

**Example**: Order: Amoxicillin 500 mg PO. Have: 250 mg/tablet
```
500 mg / 250 mg √ó 1 tablet = 2 tablets
```

### Ratio-Proportion Method
```
Have : Vehicle :: Desired : X
```

**Example**: Order: Morphine 8 mg IV. Have: 10 mg/mL
```
10 mg : 1 mL :: 8 mg : X mL
10X = 8
X = 0.8 mL
```

### IV Flow Rate Calculations

**Formula**:
```
Flow Rate (mL/hr) = Total Volume (mL) / Total Time (hours)
```

**Example**: Infuse 1000 mL over 8 hours
```
1000 mL / 8 hr = 125 mL/hr
```

**Drops per minute (gtt/min)**:
```
(Volume √ó Drop Factor) / Time in minutes = gtt/min
```

**Example**: Infuse 100 mL over 1 hour. Drop factor = 15 gtt/mL
```
(100 √ó 15) / 60 = 25 gtt/min
```

### Pediatric Dosage (Weight-Based)

**Formula**:
```
Ordered dose (mg/kg/day) √ó Child's weight (kg) = Total daily dose
```

**Example**: Order: 10 mg/kg/day. Child weighs 20 kg
```
10 mg/kg √ó 20 kg = 200 mg/day
```

**Always verify**: Is calculated dose within safe range?

### BSA (Body Surface Area) Calculations
Used for chemotherapy, burn patients
```
BSA (m¬≤) = ‚àö[(Height (cm) √ó Weight (kg)) / 3600]
```

## üìä Medication Classifications

### Cardiovascular

| Class | Suffix | Action | Example | Nursing Considerations |
|-------|--------|--------|---------|----------------------|
| **ACE Inhibitors** | -pril | Lower BP, reduce afterload | Lisinopril | Monitor K+, dry cough common |
| **Beta Blockers** | -lol | Decrease HR/BP | Metoprolol | Check HR before giving; hold if <60 |
| **Calcium Channel Blockers** | -pine, -dipine | Vasodilation | Amlodipine | Monitor BP, peripheral edema |
| **Diuretics (Loop)** | -semide | Decrease fluid volume | Furosemide | Monitor K+, I&O, daily weights |
| **Anticoagulants** | | Prevent clots | Warfarin, Heparin | Monitor PT/INR (warfarin), aPTT (heparin) |

### Respiratory

| Medication | Use | Nursing Considerations |
|------------|-----|----------------------|
| **Albuterol** | Bronchodilator (rescue) | Use BEFORE corticosteroid inhaler |
| **Fluticasone** | Corticosteroid (maintenance) | Rinse mouth after use to prevent thrush |
| **Montelukast** | Leukotriene modifier | Take daily, NOT for acute symptoms |

### Gastrointestinal

| Medication | Use | Nursing Considerations |
|------------|-----|----------------------|
| **PPIs** (-prazole) | Reduce stomach acid | Take before breakfast |
| **H2 Blockers** (-tidine) | Reduce stomach acid | Less effective than PPIs |
| **Ondansetron** | Antiemetic | Monitor for QT prolongation |

### Endocrine

| Medication | Use | Nursing Considerations |
|------------|-----|----------------------|
| **Levothyroxine** | Hypothyroidism | Take on empty stomach, same time daily |
| **Insulin** | Diabetes | Rotate sites, know onset/peak/duration |
| **Metformin** | Diabetes (Type 2) | Take with meals, hold before contrast dye |

### Pain Management

| Type | Examples | Nursing Considerations |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| **Opioids** | Morphine, Fentanyl, Hydrocodone | Monitor respiratory rate (hold if <12), assess pain level, have Naloxone available |
| **NSAIDs** | Ibuprofen, Ketorolac | GI upset, bleeding risk, nephrotoxic |
| **Acetaminophen** | Tylenol | Max 4 g/day, hepatotoxic |

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Medication Interactions

### Drug-Drug Interactions

**Warfarin** interactions (many!):
- **Increases anticoagulation**: NSAIDs, antibiotics, garlic, ginkgo
- **Decreases anticoagulation**: Vitamin K foods (leafy greens), St. John's Wort

**MAOIs** (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors):
- **Avoid**: Tyramine-rich foods (aged cheese, wine, smoked meats)
- **Risk**: Hypertensive crisis

**Grapefruit Juice**:
- **Avoid with**: Many statins, calcium channel blockers
- **Effect**: Increases drug levels

### Drug-Food Interactions

| Drug | Avoid/Include | Reason |
|------|---------------|--------|
| **Levothyroxine** | Take on empty stomach | Food decreases absorption |
| **MAOIs** | Avoid tyramine | Hypertensive crisis |
| **Warfarin** | Consistent Vitamin K intake | Affects INR |
| **Tetracycline** | Avoid dairy | Decreased absorption |
| **Potassium-sparing diuretics** | Limit high-K+ foods | Risk hyperkalemia |

## üö® Adverse Drug Reactions

### Recognizing Anaphylaxis

**Signs/Symptoms** (rapid onset):
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing
- Swelling of face/lips/tongue
- Hives, itching
- Hypotension, tachycardia
- Sense of impending doom

**Immediate Actions**:
1. **STOP medication**
2. Call for help/Rapid Response
3. Maintain airway
4. Give **Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM** (anterolateral thigh)
5. IV access, fluids
6. Oxygen, possibly intubation
7. Antihistamines, corticosteroids (secondary)

### Common Side Effects vs. Adverse Reactions

| Side Effect (Expected) | Adverse Reaction (Serious) |
|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Drowsiness from antihistamine | Anaphylaxis |
| Dry mouth from anticholinergic | Respiratory depression from opioid |
| Nausea from antibiotic | Stevens-Johnson syndrome |
| Dizziness from antihypertensive | Agranulocytosis |

## üìù Documentation

### What to Document

**Before administration**:
- Assessment data (VS, pain level, etc.)
- Rationale if PRN medication

**After administration**:
- Date and time
- Medication name, dose, route
- Site (if injectable)
- Patient response/effectiveness
- Any adverse reactions
- Refusal (if applicable) + provider notification

**Never document before giving!**

### If Error Occurs

1. Assess patient, provide care
2. Notify provider immediately
3. Complete incident report (NOT in chart)
4. Document factually in chart (what happened, interventions, patient response)
5. Monitor patient closely

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Points

1. **Always check HR before** beta blockers, digoxin
2. **Always check BP before** antihypertensives
3. **Always check respiratory rate before** opioids
4. **Two patient identifiers** - NEVER room number
5. **Never leave medication** at bedside (stay and watch patient take)
6. **Insulin** - most NCLEX questions about timing and site rotation
7. **Warfarin** - know INR therapeutic range (2.0-3.0)
8. **Heparin** - know aPTT therapeutic range (1.5-2√ó control)

### Question Strategies

**Priority questions**: What to do FIRST?
- Usually: **Assess** before intervening
- Exception: Life-threatening situations (stop medication in anaphylaxis)

**Delegation**: Can UAP give medications?
- **NO** - only licensed nurses can administer medications
- **Exception**: Stable patient, oral route, UAP has special training (some states)

**Teaching questions**:
- Focus on safety, adherence, side effects to report

## ‚úÖ Common NCLEX Scenarios

<details>
<summary>1. Nurse is preparing to give digoxin. HR = 58 bpm. What action?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold medication and notify provider

**Rationale**: Hold digoxin if HR <60 bpm. Digoxin decreases heart rate, giving it could cause severe bradycardia.
</details>

<details>
<summary>2. Patient refuses medication. What should nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Document refusal and notify provider

**Rationale**: Patient has right to refuse. Do not force or coerce. Document and inform provider.
</details>

<details>
<summary>3. Which site is best for IM injection in adult?</summary>

**Answer**: Ventrogluteal

**Rationale**: Preferred site - large muscle, away from major nerves/vessels, less pain.
</details>

<details>
<summary>4. When teaching about warfarin, what should patient avoid?</summary>

**Answer**: Aspirin, NSAIDs, and activities with high fall/injury risk

**Rationale**: Avoid medications that increase bleeding risk. Maintain consistent Vitamin K intake (don't avoid, just be consistent).
</details>

<details>
<summary>5. Patient on IV has swelling, coolness at site. What is this?</summary>

**Answer**: Infiltration

**Rationale**: IV fluid leaking into surrounding tissue. STOP IV, elevate extremity, apply warm compress, restart IV elsewhere.
</details>
