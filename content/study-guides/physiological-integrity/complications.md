# Post-Operative Complications

## Overview
Recognizing and preventing post-operative complications is essential for patient safety and heavily tested on the NCLEX. This guide covers assessment, prevention, and management of common surgical complications.

---

| Complication | Peak Time | Priority Assessment |
|--------------|-----------|---------------------|

## ü´Å Respiratory Complications

### Atelectasis (Collapsed Alveoli)

**Most common post-op respiratory complication**

**Pathophysiology**: Alveolar collapse due to retained secretions, inadequate lung expansion

**Risk Factors**:
- General anesthesia
- Upper abdominal/thoracic surgery
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Immobility
- Pain limiting deep breathing

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Low-grade fever** (first 24-48 hours)
- Diminished breath sounds
- Decreased O2 saturation
- Dyspnea, tachypnea
- Crackles on auscultation

**Prevention** (Most Important):
1. **Incentive spirometry** - 10 times every hour while awake
2. **Deep breathing and coughing** exercises
3. **Early ambulation**
4. **Turn, cough, deep breathe** every 2 hours
5. **Adequate pain control** (enables deep breathing)
6. **Semi-Fowler's or high Fowler's position**
7. **Splint surgical site** when coughing

**Treatment**:
- Continue prevention measures
- Chest physiotherapy
- Mucolytics
- Oxygen therapy

**Mnemonic for Prevention**: **"I CAN BREATHE"**
- **I**ncentive spirometry
- **C**ough and deep breathe
- **A**mbulate early
- **N**o smoking

---

### Pneumonia

**Second most common post-op respiratory complication**

**Risk Factors**:
- Prolonged immobility
- Aspiration
- Impaired swallowing
- Immunosuppression
- Mechanical ventilation
- Older age

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Fever** (usually >101¬∞F)
- **Productive cough** (yellow/green sputum)
- Dyspnea, tachypnea
- Crackles, decreased breath sounds
- Elevated WBC
- Chest X-ray: Infiltrate/consolidation

**Prevention**:
- Same as atelectasis prevention
- **Aspiration precautions**:
  - NPO until fully awake and gag reflex returns
  - Elevate head of bed during/after meals
  - Assess swallowing ability
- Hand hygiene
- Oral care

**Treatment**:
- Antibiotics (culture and sensitivity)
- Oxygen therapy
- Hydration
- Chest physiotherapy

---

### Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

**Life-threatening complication**

**Pathophysiology**: Blood clot (usually from DVT) travels to pulmonary artery, blocking blood flow

**Risk Factors**:
- Immobility
- DVT
- Orthopedic surgery (hip, knee)
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Hypercoagulability disorders
- Oral contraceptives

**Classic Triad**:
1. **Sudden dyspnea** (most common)
2. **Chest pain** (pleuritic - worse with breathing)
3. **Hemoptysis** (coughing up blood)

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Sudden onset** dyspnea, tachypnea
- Chest pain (sharp, stabbing)
- Tachycardia
- Anxiety, sense of impending doom
- Decreased O2 saturation
- Cough, hemoptysis
- Syncope (if massive PE)

**Diagnostic Tests**:
- **CT pulmonary angiography** (gold standard)
- D-dimer (elevated)
- ABG (hypoxemia, respiratory alkalosis)
- V/Q scan

**Immediate Nursing Actions**:
1. **Elevate head of bed** (high Fowler's)
2. **Administer oxygen** (high-flow)
3. **Call provider/Rapid Response** immediately
4. **IV access**
5. **Monitor vital signs continuously**
6. Stay with patient, provide reassurance

**Treatment**:
- **Anticoagulation** (heparin, then warfarin or DOAC)
- Oxygen therapy
- Thrombolytics (if massive PE)
- Possible embolectomy

**Prevention**:
- Early ambulation
- Sequential compression devices (SCDs)
- Anticoagulation prophylaxis (low-dose heparin, enoxaparin)
- Hydration
- Avoid crossing legs
- Leg exercises

## ü©∏ Circulatory Complications

### Hemorrhage/Shock

**Leading cause of post-op death in first 48 hours**

**Types**:
- **Primary**: During surgery
- **Intermediate**: First few hours after surgery
- **Secondary**: 7-10 days post-op (infection, slipped ligature)

**Signs & Symptoms** (Progressive):

**Early Shock** (Compensated):
- **Restlessness, anxiety** (earliest sign)
- Increased heart rate (>100 bpm)
- Cool, pale skin
- Delayed capillary refill
- Decreased urine output (<30 mL/hr)

**Late Shock** (Decompensated):
- **Hypotension** (SBP <90)
- Tachycardia (>120 bpm)
- Tachypnea
- Altered level of consciousness
- Weak, thready pulse
- Cold, clammy skin

**Nursing Assessment**:
- **Vital signs** every 15 minutes
- **Surgical site/dressings** - amount and type of drainage
- **Drains** - note amount, color, consistency
- **I&O** - urine output (should be >30 mL/hr)
- **LOC** - restlessness is early sign

**Immediate Interventions**:
1. **Call for help** - notify surgeon immediately
2. **Place flat or Trendelenburg** (feet elevated)
3. **Apply direct pressure** to bleeding site
4. **Administer oxygen**
5. **Large-bore IV access** √ó 2
6. **Rapid IV fluid resuscitation** (NS or LR)
7. **Monitor vital signs** continuously
8. **Prepare for transfusion** (type and crossmatch)
9. **Prepare to return to OR** if needed

**Prevention**:
- Monitor drainage closely
- Reinforce dressings (don't remove first dressing)
- Avoid activities that increase bleeding (Valsalva, straining)

### Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

**Blood clot formation in deep veins, usually lower extremities**

**Virchow's Triad** (3 factors promoting clot formation):
1. **Venous stasis** (immobility)
2. **Vessel wall injury** (surgery, trauma)
3. **Hypercoagulability** (dehydration, malignancy)

**Risk Factors**:
- Surgery (especially orthopedic, pelvic, abdominal)
- Immobility
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Oral contraceptives
- History of DVT/PE
- Cancer
- Prolonged sitting (flights, bed rest)

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Unilateral** leg swelling, warmth, redness
- **Calf pain/tenderness** (often in calf)
- Pain with dorsiflexion (**Homan's sign** - unreliable, don't routinely assess)
- Palpable cord
- **May be asymptomatic**

**Diagnostic Tests**:
- **Venous duplex ultrasound** (Doppler)
- D-dimer (elevated)

**Treatment**:
- **Anticoagulation**:
  - Heparin IV (immediate)
  - Transition to warfarin or DOAC
- **Bedrest initially** (prevent embolization)
- **Elevate affected leg** above heart
- **Warm, moist compresses**
- **DO NOT massage** (risk dislodging clot)

**Prevention** (Same as PE):
- Early ambulation
- SCDs (sequential compression devices)
- TED stockings (anti-embolism stockings)
- Anticoagulation prophylaxis
- Leg exercises
- Adequate hydration

**Critical Point**: **Never massage a potentially thrombosed leg!** Risk of PE!

## ü§ï Wound Complications

### Surgical Site Infection (SSI)

**Timing**: Usually 3-5 days post-op (can occur up to 30 days)

**Risk Factors**:
- Diabetes
- Obesity
- Malnutrition
- Immunosuppression
- Prolonged surgery
- Contaminated wound
- Poor surgical technique

**Signs & Symptoms** (Think: **"REEDA"**):
- **R**edness around incision
- **E**dema/swelling
- **E**cchymosis (bruising)
- **D**rainage (purulent - thick, yellow/green, foul-smelling)
- **A**pproximation of wound edges (separation = dehiscence)

**Additional Signs**:
- **Fever** (>100.4¬∞F)
- **Warmth** at site
- Elevated WBC
- Increased pain (sudden worsening)

**Treatment**:
- **Culture wound drainage** before antibiotics
- **Antibiotics** (broad-spectrum, then specific to culture)
- **Wound care** (irrigate, debride if needed)
- May need to open and drain wound

**Prevention**:
- **Preoperative antibiotics** (within 1 hour of incision)
- Aseptic technique during dressing changes
- Hand hygiene
- **Glucose control** in diabetics
- Adequate nutrition
- Avoid shaving (use clippers if needed)

---

### Wound Dehiscence

**Partial or complete separation of wound edges**

**Timing**: Usually 4-14 days post-op

**Risk Factors**:
- Obesity
- Malnutrition
- Diabetes
- Infection
- Increased intra-abdominal pressure (vomiting, coughing, straining)
- Corticosteroid use
- Advanced age
- Poor wound healing

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Sudden "popping" sensation** (patient may report "something gave way")
- Increased serosanguineous drainage
- Visible separation of wound edges
- Pink drainage on dressing

**Nursing Actions**:
1. **Stay calm** (don't alarm patient)
2. **Place patient in low Fowler's position** (flexed knees - reduces tension)
3. **Cover wound with sterile saline-soaked gauze**
4. **Notify surgeon immediately**
5. **NPO** (may need to return to OR)
6. **Monitor vital signs**
7. **Provide reassurance**

**Prevention**:
- **Splint incision** when coughing/moving
- Avoid Valsalva maneuver (straining)
- Antiemetics for nausea
- Adequate nutrition (protein, vitamin C, zinc)
- **Binder/abdominal support** for high-risk patients

---

### Evisceration

**MEDICAL EMERGENCY - Protrusion of organs through wound**

**More serious than dehiscence**

**Timing**: Usually 4-14 days post-op

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Visible organs protruding through wound (usually intestines)
- Sudden gush of serosanguineous fluid
- Patient reports "something gave way" or "popping"

**IMMEDIATE Nursing Actions**:
1. **STAY WITH PATIENT** - call for help
2. **Place patient in low Fowler's** with knees flexed (reduces tension)
3. **Cover organs with sterile saline-soaked gauze** (NEVER attempt to reinsert organs!)
4. **Keep moist** (add saline as needed)
5. **Cover with sterile towel** (retain moisture/warmth)
6. **NPO** immediately
7. **Notify surgeon STAT** (emergency surgery required)
8. **IV access** if not present
9. **Monitor vital signs** (watch for shock)
10. **Provide emotional support** (very frightening for patient)

**DO NOT**:
- Try to replace organs
- Remove any dressings
- Give anything by mouth

## ü§¢ Gastrointestinal Complications

### Paralytic Ileus

**Temporary cessation of peristalsis**

**Common after abdominal surgery**

**Pathophysiology**: Bowel manipulation during surgery ‚Üí temporary loss of peristalsis

**Risk Factors**:
- Abdominal/pelvic surgery
- Anesthesia (especially opioids)
- Electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia)
- Immobility

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Absent or decreased bowel sounds**
- **Abdominal distention**
- Nausea, vomiting
- No flatus or bowel movement
- Diffuse, cramping abdominal pain
- Hiccups

**Nursing Assessment**:
- Auscultate **all 4 quadrants** for 5 minutes before documenting absent bowel sounds
- Measure abdominal girth
- Assess for distention
- Monitor for flatus, bowel movements

**Treatment**:
- **NPO** until bowel sounds return
- **NG tube** for decompression if severe
- **IV fluids**
- **Ambulation** (stimulates peristalsis)
- **Correct electrolytes** (especially potassium)
- Reduce opioid use (causes constipation)
- **Chewing gum** (stimulates GI motility)

**Prevention**:
- Early ambulation
- Minimize opioid use
- Adequate hydration
- Early feeding when appropriate

**Expected Resolution**: 24-72 hours post-op

---

### Nausea & Vomiting

**Very common post-op (20-30% of patients)**

**Causes**:
- Anesthesia
- Opioid pain medications
- Hypotension
- Gastric distention
- Pain
- Anxiety
- Delayed gastric emptying

**Complications**:
- Dehydration
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Aspiration
- Increased pain
- Wound dehiscence (increased intra-abdominal pressure)

**Interventions**:
- **Antiemetics** (ondansetron, promethazine, metoclopramide)
- Position on **side** (prevent aspiration)
- Maintain NPO initially, advance diet slowly
- IV fluids
- Deep breathing, relaxation
- Avoid sudden movements

**Prevention**:
- Prophylactic antiemetics for high-risk patients
- Minimize opioid use
- Gradual diet advancement
- Adequate hydration

## üíß Urinary Complications

### Urinary Retention

**Inability to void or incomplete bladder emptying**

**Common in first 6-8 hours post-op**

**Risk Factors**:
- Anesthesia (impairs bladder sensation)
- Opioid pain medications
- Anticholinergic medications
- Pelvic/abdominal surgery
- Males with BPH
- Immobility
- Pain

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Bladder distention** (palpable, dull on percussion)
- Lower abdominal discomfort
- **Unable to void** OR
- **Frequent small voids** (25-50 mL) - overflow incontinence
- Restlessness
- Diaphoresis

**Assessment**:
- Palpate bladder (above symphysis pubis)
- **Bladder scan** (if available) - >400-600 mL indicates retention
- Monitor **I&O** - should void within 6-8 hours post-op
- Assess for voiding: frequency, amount

**Interventions**:
1. **Non-invasive measures first**:
   - Privacy
   - Assist to bathroom/bedside commode if able
   - Run water, warm water over perineum
   - Warm compress to lower abdomen
   - Male: stand to void if able
2. **If unsuccessful**: Straight catheterization (one-time)
3. **If repeated retention**: Indwelling Foley catheter

**Prevention**:
- Adequate fluid intake (unless contraindicated)
- Early ambulation
- Privacy for voiding
- Minimize opioid use

---

### Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

**Most common healthcare-associated infection**

**Risk Factors**:
- Urinary catheterization (main risk)
- Female
- Urinary retention
- Inadequate fluid intake
- Immobility
- Diabetes

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- **Dysuria** (burning on urination)
- Urinary frequency, urgency
- Cloudy, foul-smelling urine
- Suprapubic pain
- Fever (possible)
- Hematuria (possible)

**Treatment**:
- **Urinalysis and culture** before antibiotics
- Antibiotics
- Increase fluid intake
- Phenazopyridine for dysuria (turns urine orange)

**Prevention**:
- **Avoid catheterization** if possible
- **Remove catheter ASAP**
- Maintain closed drainage system
- Keep drainage bag below bladder
- Perineal care
- Adequate hydration

## üß† Neurological Complications

### Post-Operative Delirium

**Acute confusion, especially in elderly**

**Risk Factors**:
- Advanced age (>65 years)
- Dementia
- Prolonged surgery
- Multiple medications
- Sensory impairment
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Hypoxia
- Pain
- Sleep deprivation

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Acute onset confusion (fluctuates)
- Disorientation
- Agitation or lethargy
- Hallucinations
- Altered sleep-wake cycle
- Inattention

**Prevention**:
- Reorient frequently (person, place, time)
- Glasses/hearing aids if needed
- Adequate pain control
- Maintain sleep-wake cycle (lights on during day, dim at night)
- Early mobilization
- Minimize medications (especially anticholinergics, benzodiazepines)
- Family presence
- Avoid restraints

**Treatment**:
- Identify and treat underlying cause
- Frequent reorientation
- Safe environment
- Consider haloperidol for severe agitation (last resort)

## üéØ NCLEX High-Yield Points

### Prevention is Key!

**Remember**: **Most complications are preventable!**

**Post-Op Priority Interventions** (Mnemonic: **"DEEP STEAM"**):
- **D**eep breathing and coughing
- **E**arly ambulation
- **E**levate legs (prevent DVT)
- **P**ain management
- **S**equential compression devices
- **T**urn every 2 hours
- **E**xercises (leg exercises)
- **A**ntiemetics as needed
- **M**onitor I&O

### Immediate Post-Op Priorities (ABC)

1. **A**irway - patent, breath sounds
2. **B**reathing - O2 sat, respiratory rate/effort
3. **C**irculation - VS, bleeding, perfusion

### Critical Signs Requiring Immediate Notification

- **Hemorrhage**: Hypotension, tachycardia, restlessness, decreased LOC
- **Pulmonary embolism**: Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia
- **Evisceration**: Organs protruding through wound
- **Shock**: Hypotension, tachycardia, cool/clammy skin
- **Wound dehiscence**: Sudden "popping," increased drainage

### Delegation

**Can Delegate to UAP**:
- Vital signs (stable patients)
- I&O measurement
- Assisting with ambulation
- Applying SCDs

**Cannot Delegate** (RN only):
- Initial post-op assessment
- Pain assessment and management
- Wound assessment
- Patient teaching
- Abnormal findings

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient 2 days post-op abdominal surgery suddenly reports "something gave way." The nurse notes serosanguineous drainage on the dressing. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Place patient in low Fowler's position with knees flexed and cover wound with sterile saline-soaked gauze.

**Rationale**: This indicates wound dehiscence (separation of wound edges). Low Fowler's with flexed knees reduces tension on the wound. Cover with sterile saline-soaked gauze to keep moist. Notify surgeon immediately. Patient will likely need to return to OR. Do NOT remove dressing or attempt to approximate edges.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A post-op patient develops sudden dyspnea, chest pain, and tachycardia on day 7. What does the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Pulmonary embolism (PE)

**Rationale**: Classic triad: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hemoptysis. Timing (7 days post-op) is consistent with PE. This is a medical emergency. Immediate actions: High Fowler's position, administer oxygen, call Rapid Response, IV access. Prepare for anticoagulation and diagnostic tests (CT angiography, D-dimer).
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: What is the MOST important intervention to prevent atelectasis?</summary>

**Answer**: Incentive spirometry 10 times every hour while awake

**Rationale**: Atelectasis is the most common post-op respiratory complication. Incentive spirometry promotes deep breathing and lung expansion, preventing alveolar collapse. Other important measures: early ambulation, turn/cough/deep breathe every 2 hours, adequate pain control (enables deep breathing).
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A patient with organs protruding through a surgical wound. What is the FIRST action?</summary>

**Answer**: Cover with sterile saline-soaked gauze and stay with patient

**Rationale**: This is evisceration - a medical emergency. NEVER attempt to replace organs. Cover with sterile saline-soaked gauze to keep organs moist. Place patient in low Fowler's with knees flexed (reduces tension). Call for help, notify surgeon STAT, NPO, monitor VS. Patient needs emergency surgery.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient has absent bowel sounds and abdominal distention on day 2 post-op abdominal surgery. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Keep patient NPO, encourage ambulation, and notify provider

**Rationale**: This indicates paralytic ileus (temporary cessation of peristalsis). Common after abdominal surgery. Interventions: NPO until bowel sounds return, ambulation (stimulates peristalsis), IV fluids, possibly NG tube for decompression. Auscultate all 4 quadrants for 5 full minutes before documenting absent bowel sounds.
</details>
