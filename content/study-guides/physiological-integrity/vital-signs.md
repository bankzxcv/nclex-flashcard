# Vital Signs Assessment

## Overview
Vital signs are critical indicators of physiological status and must be assessed accurately. This guide covers proper assessment techniques, normal values, abnormal findings, and nursing implications for the NCLEX.

### Temperature Variations

**Fever (Pyrexia)**:
- **Low-grade**: 99-101¬∞F (37.2-38.3¬∞C)
- **Moderate**: 101-103¬∞F (38.3-39.4¬∞C)
- **High-grade**: 103-105¬∞F (39.4-40.5¬∞C)
- **Hyperpyrexia**: >105¬∞F (>40.5¬∞C) - medical emergency!

**Hypothermia**: <95¬∞F (<35¬∞C)

### Nursing Implications

**Fever**:
- **Assess for cause**: Infection, inflammation, dehydration, medications
- **Interventions**:
  - Antipyretics (acetaminophen, ibuprofen)
  - Cooling measures (remove blankets, cool cloths, cooling blanket)
  - **Increase fluids** (100 mL/¬∞F above normal)
  - Monitor for febrile seizures in children
- **NEVER** give aspirin to children (Reye's syndrome risk)

**Hypothermia**:
- **Passive rewarming**: Warm blankets, warm room
- **Active rewarming**: Warming blanket, warm IV fluids
- Monitor for arrhythmias

**Critical Temperatures**:
- **>105¬∞F (40.5¬∞C)**: Risk of seizures, brain damage
- **<95¬∞F (35¬∞C)**: Risk of cardiac arrhythmias, death

## ‚ù§Ô∏è Pulse

### Normal Values by Age

| Age Group | Normal Heart Rate (bpm) |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Newborn (0-1 month)** | 100-180 |
| **Infant (1-12 months)** | 100-160 |
| **Toddler (1-3 years)** | 90-150 |
| **Preschooler (3-6 years)** | 80-140 |
| **School-age (6-12 years)** | 70-120 |
| **Adolescent (12-18 years)** | 60-100 |
| **Adult** | **60-100** |
| **Older Adult** | 60-100 |
| **Well-conditioned athlete** | 40-60 |

### Assessment Technique

**Radial Pulse** (most common):
1. Use first 2-3 fingers (NOT thumb - has own pulse)
2. Apply gentle pressure to radial artery
3. Count for **30 seconds and multiply by 2** (routine)
4. Count for **FULL 60 seconds** if irregular, on cardiac medications, or critical patient

**Apical Pulse** (most accurate):
1. **Always use for**:
   - Infants and children <2 years
   - Before cardiac medications (digoxin, beta blockers)
   - Irregular radial pulse
   - Weak radial pulse
2. Auscultate at **5th intercostal space, midclavicular line** (apex of heart)
3. Count for **FULL 60 seconds**

**Apical-Radial Pulse** (pulse deficit):
- Two nurses simultaneously assess apical and radial pulse
- **Pulse deficit** = Apical rate - Radial rate
- Indicates inadequate perfusion

### Pulse Characteristics

| Characteristic | Assessment |
|----------------|------------|
| **Rate** | Bradycardia (<60), Normal (60-100), Tachycardia (>100) |
| **Rhythm** | Regular or irregular |
| **Strength** | 0 (absent), 1+ (weak), 2+ (normal), 3+ (bounding) |
| **Equality** | Compare bilateral pulses |

### Abnormal Findings

**Tachycardia (>100 bpm)**:
- **Causes**: Fever, pain, anxiety, hypoxia, hypovolemia, heart failure, hyperthyroidism, medications (albuterol)
- **Interventions**: Treat underlying cause, monitor rhythm, assess for chest pain/dyspnea

**Bradycardia (<60 bpm)**:
- **Causes**: Vagal stimulation, medications (beta blockers, digoxin), hypothermia, heart block, athletes
- **Interventions**:
  - **Hold cardiac medications if HR <60** (especially digoxin, beta blockers)
  - Assess for symptoms (dizziness, syncope, chest pain)
  - May need atropine or pacing if symptomatic

**Irregular Pulse**:
- **Always count for full 60 seconds**
- Assess with apical pulse
- May indicate atrial fibrillation, PACs, PVCs
- Notify provider

### Nursing Implications

**Before Giving These Medications, CHECK PULSE**:

| Medication | Hold If | Rationale |
|------------|---------|-----------|
| **Digoxin** | HR <60 | Slows heart rate; risk of bradycardia/heart block |
| **Beta blockers** (-lol) | HR <60 | Decreases heart rate |
| **Calcium channel blockers** (-dipine) | HR <60 (some) | Some decrease heart rate (diltiazem, verapamil) |

## ü´Å Respirations

### Normal Values by Age

| Age Group | Normal Respiratory Rate (breaths/min) |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Newborn** | 30-60 |
| **Infant (1-12 months)** | 25-40 |
| **Toddler (1-3 years)** | 20-30 |
| **Preschooler (3-6 years)** | 20-25 |
| **School-age (6-12 years)** | 18-22 |
| **Adolescent (12-18 years)** | 12-20 |
| **Adult** | **12-20** |
| **Older Adult** | 12-20 |

### Assessment Technique

1. **Count AFTER pulse** (patient unaware - prevents conscious control)
2. Observe **chest rise and fall**
3. Count for **30 seconds and multiply by 2** (if regular)
4. Count for **FULL 60 seconds** if irregular or abnormal
5. **Don't tell patient** you're counting respirations

### Respiratory Characteristics to Assess

| Characteristic | What to Observe |
|----------------|-----------------|
| **Rate** | Breaths per minute |
| **Depth** | Shallow, normal, deep |
| **Rhythm** | Regular, irregular |
| **Effort** | Easy, labored, use of accessory muscles |
| **Sound** | Silent (normal), wheezing, stridor, crackles |

### Abnormal Findings

**Tachypnea (>20 breaths/min in adults)**:
- **Causes**: Hypoxia, fever, pain, anxiety, pulmonary disease, heart failure, acidosis
- **Interventions**: Assess O2 saturation, administer oxygen, position upright, treat cause

**Bradypnea (<12 breaths/min)**:
- **Causes**: Opioid overdose, sedatives, increased intracranial pressure, hypothyroidism
- **Interventions**:
  - **If RR <12 and on opioids: HOLD opioid, consider naloxone**
  - Stimulate to breathe if sedated
  - Assess LOC, oxygen saturation

**Apnea**: Absence of breathing
- **Medical emergency** - begin CPR if no pulse

### Breathing Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Associated Conditions |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Eupnea** | Normal breathing | Health |
| **Tachypnea** | Rapid, shallow | Fever, anxiety, hypoxia |
| **Bradypnea** | Slow breathing | Opioid overdose, increased ICP |
| **Apnea** | Absence of breathing | Respiratory arrest, sleep apnea |
| **Cheyne-Stokes** | Periods of deep breathing alternating with apnea | Heart failure, stroke, increased ICP, end of life |
| **Kussmaul** | Deep, rapid, labored | Diabetic ketoacidosis (metabolic acidosis) |
| **Biot's** | Irregular depth and rate with periods of apnea | Meningitis, increased ICP |
| **Hyperventilation** | Deep, rapid breathing | Anxiety, pain, metabolic acidosis |
| **Hypoventilation** | Shallow, slow breathing | COPD, opioid overdose |

### Nursing Implications

**Before Giving Opioids, CHECK RESPIRATIONS**:
- **Hold if RR <12 breaths/min**
- Risk of respiratory depression
- Have **naloxone (Narcan)** available

## ü©∏ Blood Pressure

### Normal Values by Age

| Age Group | Normal BP (mmHg) |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Newborn** | 60-90/40-60 |
| **Infant** | 80-100/50-70 |
| **Child (1-10 years)** | 90-110/55-75 |
| **Adolescent** | 110-120/65-80 |
| **Adult** | **<120/<80** (Normal)<br>120-129/<80 (Elevated)<br>130-139/80-89 (Stage 1 HTN)<br>‚â•140/‚â•90 (Stage 2 HTN)<br>>180/>120 (Hypertensive Crisis) |

### Assessment Technique

**Proper Technique**:
1. **Patient position**: Seated, feet flat, arm at heart level, back supported
2. **Rest 5 minutes** before measurement
3. **Correct cuff size**: Bladder encircles 80% of arm
   - Cuff too small ‚Üí falsely high reading
   - Cuff too large ‚Üí falsely low reading
4. **Placement**: 1 inch above antecubital fossa, over brachial artery
5. **Inflate** 20-30 mmHg above palpated systolic pressure
6. **Deflate** slowly (2-3 mmHg per second)
7. **First sound** (Korotkoff I) = Systolic BP
8. **Last sound** (Korotkoff V) = Diastolic BP
9. **Repeat** in other arm on first assessment (use arm with higher reading for subsequent measurements)

### Factors Affecting Blood Pressure

**False High Reading**:
- Cuff too small
- Arm below heart level
- Bladder full
- Talking during measurement
- Crossed legs

**False Low Reading**:
- Cuff too large
- Arm above heart level
- Patient lying down

**Avoid Taking BP on**:
- Arm with IV
- Arm with AV fistula/shunt (dialysis)
- Arm on side of mastectomy (lymphedema risk)
- Paralyzed arm
- Injured arm

### Hypertension Categories (Adult)

| Category | Systolic | Diastolic | Action |
|----------|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Normal** | <120 | AND <80 | Maintain healthy lifestyle |
| **Elevated** | 120-129 | AND <80 | Lifestyle modifications |
| **Stage 1 HTN** | 130-139 | OR 80-89 | Lifestyle + medication (if high CV risk) |
| **Stage 2 HTN** | ‚â•140 | OR ‚â•90 | Lifestyle + medication |
| **Hypertensive Crisis** | >180 | AND/OR >120 | **Emergency! Call provider immediately** |

### Hypotension

**Definition**: SBP <90 mmHg or 20-30 mmHg below patient's baseline

**Causes**:
- Dehydration, hemorrhage, shock
- Medications (antihypertensives, diuretics, vasodilators)
- Heart failure
- Sepsis
- Anaphylaxis

**Orthostatic Hypotension** (Postural Hypotension):
- **Definition**: Drop of ‚â•20 mmHg systolic OR ‚â•10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing
- **Assessment**:
  1. BP lying down (after 5 min rest)
  2. Stand patient
  3. BP immediately, then at 1 min and 3 min
- **Symptoms**: Dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope
- **Interventions**: Change positions slowly, dangle legs, adequate hydration

### Nursing Implications

**Before Giving Antihypertensives, CHECK BP**:
- **Hold if SBP <90 or 20-30 mmHg below baseline**
- Common antihypertensives to check:
  - ACE inhibitors (-pril)
  - ARBs (-sartan)
  - Beta blockers (-lol)
  - Calcium channel blockers (-dipine)
  - Diuretics

**Hypertensive Crisis (>180/>120)**:
- **Medical emergency** - risk of stroke, MI, organ damage
- Notify provider immediately
- Prepare for IV antihypertensives
- Frequent vital sign monitoring
- Assess for symptoms: headache, blurred vision, chest pain, dyspnea

## ü´Ä Pulse Oximetry (SpO2)

### Normal Values

| Population | Normal SpO2 |
|------------|-------------|
| **Healthy adults** | **95-100%** |
| **COPD patients** | 88-92% (lower baseline acceptable) |
| **Newborns (first 24 hrs)** | 95-100% |

### Assessment Technique

1. **Site selection**: Finger (most common), toe, earlobe, bridge of nose
2. Ensure site is **warm and has good circulation**
3. Remove **nail polish** (especially blue, green, black)
4. Apply sensor correctly (light source and detector opposite each other)
5. Allow reading to stabilize

### Factors Affecting Accuracy

**False Low Reading**:
- Nail polish (especially dark colors)
- Poor circulation (cold extremity, vasoconstriction)
- Movement/shivering
- Anemia (severe)
- Carbon monoxide poisoning (SpO2 appears normal but O2 saturation actually low)

**Limitations**:
- Measures oxygen saturation, NOT adequacy of ventilation (can have normal SpO2 but elevated CO2)
- Carbon monoxide poisoning: SpO2 appears falsely high

### Nursing Implications

**SpO2 <90%**:
- **Hypoxemia** - tissue hypoxia likely
- **Immediate interventions**:
  1. Assess patient (respiratory rate, effort, lung sounds, LOC)
  2. Position upright (high Fowler's)
  3. Administer oxygen (per protocol or provider order)
  4. Assess airway patency
  5. Notify provider

**Target SpO2**:
- **General patients**: ‚â•95%
- **COPD patients**: 88-92% (higher O2 can suppress respiratory drive)

## üå°Ô∏è Pain Assessment (The "5th Vital Sign")

### Pain Scales

**Numeric Rating Scale (0-10)**:
- 0 = No pain
- 1-3 = Mild pain
- 4-6 = Moderate pain
- 7-10 = Severe pain

**FACES Pain Scale**:
- For children, non-verbal patients, language barriers
- Pictures of faces from smiling (no pain) to crying (worst pain)

**FLACC Scale** (Behavioral - for infants, non-verbal):
- **F**ace
- **L**egs
- **A**ctivity
- **C**ry
- **C**onsolability
- Each scored 0-2, total 0-10

### Comprehensive Pain Assessment (PQRST)

| Letter | Component | Questions |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **P** | **Provokes/Palliates** | What makes it better/worse? |
| **Q** | **Quality** | What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, burning, aching) |
| **R** | **Region/Radiates** | Where is it? Does it spread? |
| **S** | **Severity** | On scale of 0-10, how bad is it? |
| **T** | **Timing** | When did it start? Constant or intermittent? |

### Nursing Implications

**Remember**:
- **Pain is subjective** - "Pain is what the patient says it is"
- **Never** doubt or minimize patient's pain
- Reassess pain **30 minutes after IV medication**, **60 minutes after oral medication**
- Document pain assessment before and after interventions

**Non-pharmacological interventions**:
- Positioning
- Heat/cold therapy
- Distraction, relaxation, music
- Massage
- Guided imagery

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Testing Points

1. **Apical pulse for 60 seconds** before digoxin, beta blockers
2. **Hold cardiac meds if HR <60** (digoxin, beta blockers)
3. **Hold opioids if RR <12**
4. **Hold antihypertensives if SBP <90**
5. **Tympanic temperature contraindicated** with ear infection
6. **Rectal temperature contraindicated** with rectal surgery, cardiac patients (vagal stimulation), low platelets
7. **Orthostatic hypotension** = drop ‚â•20/10 mmHg when standing
8. **Normal adult VS**: Temp 97-99¬∞F, HR 60-100, RR 12-20, BP <120/<80, SpO2 95-100%
9. **Don't take BP on arm with** IV, AV fistula, mastectomy side, paralysis
10. **Kussmaul respirations** = DKA (deep, rapid)
11. **Cheyne-Stokes** = end of life, heart failure (periodic breathing)

### Delegation

**Can Delegate to UAP** (Unlicensed Assistive Personnel):
- Routine vital signs on **stable** patients
- Using standard equipment

**Cannot Delegate**:
- First set of vital signs (initial assessment)
- Unstable or critical patients
- Abnormal vital signs requiring interpretation
- Vital signs requiring assessment of response to treatment

**RN Responsibility**:
- **Always validate abnormal values**
- Interpret findings
- Determine nursing actions

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A nurse prepares to give digoxin. The patient's apical pulse is 58 bpm. What action should be taken?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold the medication and notify the provider.

**Rationale**: Digoxin slows the heart rate. Hold if apical pulse is <60 bpm. Always count apical pulse for FULL 60 seconds before administering digoxin. Giving digoxin when HR is already low can cause dangerous bradycardia or heart block.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient's blood pressure is 182/118 mmHg. The patient reports severe headache and blurred vision. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Notify the provider immediately.

**Rationale**: This is a hypertensive crisis (>180/>120) with symptoms of end-organ damage (headache, vision changes). This is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment to prevent stroke, MI, or other organ damage. Do NOT give routine antihypertensive without provider order in this situation.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A patient receiving morphine has a respiratory rate of 10 breaths/min. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold the morphine and continue to monitor closely. If RR continues to drop or patient is unresponsive, administer naloxone.

**Rationale**: Hold opioids if RR <12 breaths/min due to risk of respiratory depression. Assess patient's level of consciousness and oxygen saturation. Naloxone (Narcan) is the antidote for opioid overdose/respiratory depression.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Which arm should the nurse avoid when taking blood pressure?</summary>

**Answer**: Arm with AV fistula (dialysis access), arm on side of mastectomy, arm with IV infusion, paralyzed arm

**Rationale**: BP cuff compression can damage AV fistula, cause lymphedema on mastectomy side, or affect IV flow. Use the other arm or lower extremity if necessary.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient stands up and reports feeling dizzy. BP lying down was 128/76. BP standing is 98/62. What is this?</summary>

**Answer**: Orthostatic hypotension

**Rationale**: Drop of ‚â•20 mmHg systolic or ‚â•10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing = orthostatic hypotension. Interventions: sit/lie down immediately, change positions slowly, dangle legs before standing, adequate hydration. Assess medications (antihypertensives, diuretics) and volume status.
</details>
