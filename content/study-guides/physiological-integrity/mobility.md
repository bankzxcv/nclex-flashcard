# Mobility & Immobility

## Overview
Mobility is essential for maintaining physical and psychological health. Understanding the effects of immobility, proper positioning, range of motion exercises, and assistive devices is crucial for preventing complications and promoting patient recovery.

---

## üö∂ Normal Mobility & Movement

### Types of Movement

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Active ROM** | Patient moves joint independently | Patient lifts own arm overhead |
| **Passive ROM** | Nurse/therapist moves joint for patient | Nurse moves unconscious patient's limbs |
| **Active-Assisted ROM** | Patient initiates, nurse assists | Patient starts arm lift, nurse helps complete motion |
| **Resistive ROM** | Movement against resistance | Weight training, resistance bands |

### Factors Affecting Mobility

```
FACTORS INFLUENCING MOBILITY:
  ‚Ä¢ Musculoskeletal: Bones, muscles, joints, tendons, ligaments
  ‚Ä¢ Neurological: CNS coordination, balance, motor control
  ‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular: Adequate perfusion to muscles
  ‚Ä¢ Respiratory: Oxygen supply for muscle function
  ‚Ä¢ Pain: Limits willingness to move
  ‚Ä¢ Energy: Fatigue, weakness, illness
  ‚Ä¢ Psychological: Depression, motivation, fear of falling
  ‚Ä¢ Developmental: Age-related changes
  ‚Ä¢ Medications: Sedatives, muscle relaxants
```

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Hazards of Immobility

### Cardiovascular System

**Effects**:
- **Orthostatic hypotension**: Blood pools in extremities when upright
- **Increased cardiac workload**: 10-15% increase
- **Venous stasis**: Slowed blood flow
- **Thrombus formation**: DVT risk
- **Decreased cardiac reserve**

**Prevention**:
- Early mobilization
- ROM exercises
- Sequential compression devices (SCDs)
- Anticoagulation therapy (if ordered)
- Change position frequently
- Gradual position changes (dangle before standing)

---

### Respiratory System

**Effects**:
- **Decreased lung expansion**: Shallow breathing
- **Pooling of secretions**: Especially in dependent areas
- **Hypostatic pneumonia**: Inflammation from stagnant secretions
- **Decreased cough reflex**
- **Atelectasis**: Alveolar collapse

**Prevention**:
```
RESPIRATORY INTERVENTIONS:
  ‚úì Turn, cough, deep breathe every 2 hours
  ‚úì Incentive spirometry every 1-2 hours while awake
  ‚úì Position changes (prevents pooling in one area)
  ‚úì HOB elevated (promotes lung expansion)
  ‚úì Early ambulation
  ‚úì Adequate hydration (thins secretions)
  ‚úì Chest physiotherapy if needed
```

**Mnemonic - Respiratory complications: "CHAPS"**:
- **C**ough (decreased)
- **H**ypostatic pneumonia
- **A**telectasis
- **P**ooled secretions
- **S**hallow respirations

---

### Musculoskeletal System

**Effects**:
- **Muscle atrophy**: 1-3% muscle mass lost per day
- **Decreased muscle strength**: 10-15% per week
- **Contractures**: Permanent muscle shortening
- **Joint stiffness**: Decreased ROM
- **Osteoporosis**: Bone demineralization
- **Fracture risk**: Decreased bone density

**Prevention**:
- **Active/passive ROM** exercises at least 2-3 times daily
- **Resistive exercises** when able
- **Proper positioning**: Maintain functional alignment
- **Weight-bearing activities** as soon as possible
- **Adequate protein** and **calcium/vitamin D** intake

**Contracture Prevention**:
- Most common: **Hip, knee, ankle, shoulder, elbow, wrist, fingers**
- **Foot drop**: Use foot board, high-top sneakers, splints
- **Hip flexion contracture**: Avoid prolonged sitting, pillow under knee

---

### Integumentary System

**Effects**:
- **Pressure injuries** (bedsores)
- **Skin breakdown**: Decreased tissue perfusion
- **Delayed wound healing**

**Pressure Injury Stages**:

| Stage | Description | Appearance |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| **Stage 1** | Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin | Red area that doesn't turn white when pressed |
| **Stage 2** | Partial-thickness skin loss | Shallow open ulcer, blister, or abrasion |
| **Stage 3** | Full-thickness skin loss | Deep crater, subcutaneous fat visible |
| **Stage 4** | Full-thickness tissue loss | Muscle, bone, tendon exposed |
| **Unstageable** | Full-thickness loss, depth unknown | Covered by slough/eschar |
| **Deep Tissue Injury** | Persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple | Intact or non-intact skin |

**High-Risk Areas**:
```
PRESSURE POINTS:
  Supine: Occiput, scapulae, sacrum, coccyx, heels
  Side-lying: Ears, shoulders, ribs, greater trochanter, knees, ankles
  Prone: Forehead, chin, chest, genitals, knees, toes
  Sitting: Sacrum, coccyx, ischial tuberosities
```

**Prevention - "PRESSURE"**:
- **P**osition changes every 2 hours
- **R**educe friction and shear
- **E**valuate skin daily
- **S**upport surfaces (special mattress)
- **S**kin care (keep clean and dry)
- **U**nload pressure (cushions, heel protectors)
- **R**isk assessment (Braden Scale)
- **E**ducation of patient/family

---

### Gastrointestinal System

**Effects**:
- **Decreased peristalsis**: Constipation
- **Impaired digestion**
- **Anorexia**: Decreased appetite
- **Difficulty eating**: Position-dependent

**Prevention**:
- Increase fiber intake
- Adequate fluid intake (2000-3000 mL/day)
- Stool softeners/laxatives as needed
- Abdominal exercises
- Privacy for bowel movements
- Upright position for meals

---

### Urinary System

**Effects**:
- **Urinary stasis**: Incomplete bladder emptying
- **Urinary tract infections** (UTIs)
- **Renal calculi** (kidney stones): Calcium released from bones
- **Urinary incontinence**

**Prevention**:
- Adequate fluid intake (2000-3000 mL/day)
- Avoid indwelling catheters when possible
- Acidify urine (cranberry juice)
- Encourage voiding every 3-4 hours
- Upright position to void

---

### Metabolic System

**Effects**:
- **Negative nitrogen balance**: Protein breakdown exceeds synthesis
- **Decreased metabolic rate**
- **Hyperglycemia**: Insulin resistance
- **Hypercalcemia**: Bone resorption
- **Decreased appetite**

**Prevention**:
- High-protein diet (if not contraindicated)
- Adequate caloric intake
- Activity/exercise
- Monitor blood glucose
- Monitor calcium levels

---

### Psychological Effects

**Effects**:
- **Depression, anxiety**
- **Decreased self-esteem**
- **Social isolation**
- **Sleep disturbances**
- **Cognitive decline**
- **Sensory deprivation**
- **Loss of independence**

**Prevention**:
- Encourage social interaction
- Mental stimulation (reading, puzzles, TV)
- Involve in decision-making
- Set realistic goals
- Encourage maximum independence
- Therapeutic communication

---

## üõèÔ∏è Positioning

### Basic Principles

```
POSITIONING PRINCIPLES:
  ‚úì Maintain body alignment
  ‚úì Support all body parts
  ‚úì Prevent pressure on bony prominences
  ‚úì Change position every 2 hours
  ‚úì Use pillows, wedges, rolls strategically
  ‚úì Avoid pulling/dragging (causes shear)
  ‚úì Keep bed as flat as tolerated (reduces shearing)
  ‚úì Assess skin with each position change
```

---

### Supine (Back-Lying) Position

**Uses**: Rest, post-operative, various procedures

**Positioning**:
- **Head**: Small pillow under head/neck
- **Arms**: At sides or on pillows at sides, slightly flexed
- **Hands**: Hand roll to maintain functional position
- **Lower back**: Small lumbar pillow if needed
- **Legs**: Pillow under lower legs (popliteal area to ankles) - heels off bed
- **Feet**: Footboard or high-top sneakers to prevent foot drop

**Disadvantages**:
- Pressure on occiput, scapulae, sacrum, heels
- Decreased lung expansion (weight of abdomen)
- External hip rotation (use trochanter roll)

---

### Prone (Face-Down) Position

**Uses**: Post-spinal surgery, pressure relief on back, drainage

**Positioning**:
- **Head**: Turn to side, small pillow
- **Arms**: Flexed at elbows, at sides or above head
- **Abdomen**: Pillow under abdomen (reduces lumbar lordosis)
- **Lower legs**: Pillow under shins/ankles (toes off bed)

**Advantages**: Excellent for preventing hip flexion contractures

**Disadvantages**:
- Pressure on face, chest, genitals, knees, toes
- Neck rotation strain
- Not for patients with respiratory/cardiac issues
- Contraindicated in pregnancy, recent abdominal surgery

**Contraindications**:
- Spinal cord injury (unstable)
- Respiratory distress
- Increased intracranial pressure

---

### Lateral (Side-Lying) Position

**Uses**: Pressure relief, sleeping, injections

**Positioning**:
- **Head**: Pillow to support head/neck alignment
- **Upper arm**: Pillow to support arm in flexion
- **Upper leg**: Pillow between legs (hip to ankle) - prevents internal rotation
- **Back**: Pillow behind back for support if needed

**30-Degree Lateral Position**:
- Best for pressure relief
- Reduces pressure on trochanter
- Supported by pillows/wedges

**Disadvantages**:
- Pressure on ears, shoulders, ribs, trochanter, knees, ankles
- May compress lower lung

---

### Sims' Position (Semi-Prone)

**Uses**: Enemas, rectal exams, rectal temperature, unconscious patients (drainage)

**Positioning**:
- Patient on side (usually left)
- Lower arm behind back
- Upper arm flexed, supported by pillow
- Lower leg slightly flexed
- Upper leg flexed at hip and knee, supported by pillow

**Advantages**:
- Excellent for oral drainage in unconscious patients
- Reduces pressure on sacrum/coccyx
- Good for rectal procedures

---

### Fowler's Position (Semi-Sitting)

**Types**:
- **High Fowler's**: 80-90¬∞ (sitting up)
- **Fowler's**: 45-60¬∞
- **Semi-Fowler's**: 30-45¬∞
- **Low Fowler's**: 15-30¬∞

**Uses**:
- **Respiratory distress** (promotes lung expansion)
- Eating meals
- Conversations
- Watching TV
- Post-cardiac/thoracic surgery

**Positioning**:
- HOB elevated to desired angle
- Pillow under head/neck
- Arms supported on pillows or armrests
- Pillow under lower legs (prevent sliding)
- Feet supported

**Disadvantages**:
- Pressure on sacrum/coccyx (increased with sliding)
- Shearing force if slides down
- Hip/knee flexion contractures with prolonged use

**PREVENT SLIDING**: Keep bed flat (max 30¬∞ unless needed), pillow under calves

---

### Trendelenburg Position

**Uses**:
- Shock (controversial - not first-line)
- Postural drainage
- Central line insertion

**Positioning**: Entire bed tilted, head down 15-30¬∞

**Contraindications**:
- Head injury (increases ICP)
- Respiratory distress
- Recent eye surgery

---

### Reverse Trendelenburg

**Uses**: Gastric reflux prevention, some surgeries

**Positioning**: Entire bed tilted, feet down, head up

## üîÑ Range of Motion (ROM)

### Joint Movements

| Movement | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Flexion** | Decreasing angle between bones | Bending elbow |
| **Extension** | Increasing angle between bones | Straightening elbow |
| **Hyperextension** | Extension beyond neutral | Head tilting back |
| **Abduction** | Moving away from midline | Arm out to side |
| **Adduction** | Moving toward midline | Arm to side of body |
| **Rotation** | Moving around central axis | Turning head side-to-side |
| **Circumduction** | Circular movement | Arm circles |
| **Pronation** | Turning downward | Palm down |
| **Supination** | Turning upward | Palm up |
| **Dorsiflexion** | Toes toward shin | Foot up |
| **Plantar flexion** | Toes pointing down | Foot down |
| **Inversion** | Sole of foot inward | |
| **Eversion** | Sole of foot outward | |

### ROM Exercise Guidelines

```
ROM EXERCISE PRINCIPLES:
  ‚úì Perform 2-3 times daily
  ‚úì Each exercise 3-5 repetitions
  ‚úì Slow, smooth movements
  ‚úì Support limb above and below joint
  ‚úì Move to point of resistance, not pain
  ‚úì Follow same sequence (head to toe)
  ‚úì Encourage active ROM when possible
  ‚úì Never force a joint
```

### ROM by Body Part

**Neck**:
- Flexion, extension, hyperextension
- Lateral flexion (ear to shoulder)
- Rotation

**Shoulder**:
- Flexion, extension, hyperextension
- Abduction, adduction
- Internal/external rotation
- Circumduction

**Elbow**:
- Flexion, extension

**Forearm**:
- Pronation, supination

**Wrist**:
- Flexion, extension, hyperextension
- Radial/ulnar deviation

**Fingers**:
- Flexion, extension, hyperextension
- Abduction, adduction

**Thumb**:
- Opposition (touch each fingertip)

**Hip**:
- Flexion, extension, hyperextension
- Abduction, adduction
- Internal/external rotation
- Circumduction

**Knee**:
- Flexion, extension

**Ankle**:
- Dorsiflexion, plantar flexion

**Foot**:
- Inversion, eversion

**Toes**:
- Flexion, extension

## ü¶Ω Assistive Devices

### Canes

**Types**:
- **Standard cane**: Single point, minimal support
- **Quad cane**: Four points, more stability

**Fitting**: Top of cane at greater trochanter (wrist level when arm at side), elbow flexed 15-30¬∞

**Use**:
1. Hold cane on **stronger side**
2. Move cane forward
3. Move **weaker leg** forward (with cane)
4. Move **stronger leg** forward

**Stairs**:
- **UP**: "Good leg first" (up with the good)
- **DOWN**: "Bad leg first" (down with the bad)

**Mnemonic: "Up with the good, down with the bad"**

---

### Crutches

**Types**:
- **Axillary crutches**: Most common, underarm support
- **Forearm (Lofstrand) crutches**: Cuff around forearm
- **Platform crutches**: For arthritis, weak grip

**Fitting (Axillary)**:
- **Standing**: 2-3 finger widths between axilla and crutch top (prevents brachial plexus injury)
- **Hand grips**: Elbow flexed 15-30¬∞, wrist extended
- **Length**: From axilla to 6 inches lateral to foot

**Crutch Gaits**:

| Gait | Weight-Bearing | Pattern |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Four-point** | Partial weight-bearing both legs | R crutch ‚Üí L foot ‚Üí L crutch ‚Üí R foot (slowest, most stable) |
| **Three-point** | Non-weight-bearing one leg | Both crutches + weak leg ‚Üí strong leg |
| **Two-point** | Partial weight-bearing both legs | R crutch + L foot ‚Üí L crutch + R foot (faster than 4-point) |
| **Swing-to** | No weight-bearing both legs | Both crutches forward ‚Üí swing legs TO crutches |
| **Swing-through** | No weight-bearing both legs | Both crutches forward ‚Üí swing legs PAST crutches (fastest) |

**Safety**:
- **NEVER rest weight on axilla** (brachial plexus damage)
- Rubber tips in good condition
- Remove throw rugs
- Wear non-skid shoes

---

### Walkers

**Types**:
- **Standard walker**: Lift and move forward, most stable
- **Rolling walker**: Wheels on front legs
- **Four-wheeled walker (Rollator)**: Wheels on all legs, hand brakes, seat

**Fitting**: Top of walker at wrist level, elbows flexed 15-30¬∞

**Use**:
1. Lift walker, move forward
2. Step forward with **weaker leg**
3. Step forward with **stronger leg**

---

### Wheelchairs

**Safety**:
- Lock brakes before transfers
- Position footrests up or out before transfer
- Use seatbelt if needed
- Back up to curbs going down
- Don't let patient slide forward

**Pressure Relief**: Lift self up or shift weight every 15-20 minutes

---

## üîß Transfer Techniques

### General Principles

```
SAFE TRANSFER GUIDELINES:
  ‚úì Assess patient's ability to help
  ‚úì Explain procedure
  ‚úì Use assistive devices/equipment
  ‚úì Lock bed, wheelchair brakes
  ‚úì Use proper body mechanics
  ‚úì Transfer toward stronger side
  ‚úì Use gait belt
  ‚úì Get help if needed (don't risk injury)
  ‚úì Non-skid footwear
  ‚úì Clear pathway
```

### Body Mechanics for Nurses

```
BODY MECHANICS:
  ‚úì Wide base of support (feet shoulder-width)
  ‚úì Bend knees, not back (use legs, not back)
  ‚úì Keep back straight
  ‚úì Hold object/patient close to body
  ‚úì Avoid twisting (pivot feet)
  ‚úì Push/pull rather than lift
  ‚úì Use major muscle groups
  ‚úì Get help for heavy/dependent patients
```

---

### Bed to Chair Transfer

**Steps**:
1. Position chair at **45¬∞ angle to bed** on patient's **stronger side**
2. Lock wheelchair brakes, remove/swing away footrests
3. Raise HOB, assist to sitting (dangle)
4. Assess for dizziness/orthostatic hypotension
5. Apply gait belt
6. Stand patient (on count of 3, patient pushes up)
7. Pivot toward stronger leg
8. Lower into chair (bend knees, patient reaches for armrests)

**Key Points**:
- Transfer toward **stronger side**
- Allow patient to help as much as able
- Protect weaker side
- Gait belt for safety

---

### Dangling (Sitting on Edge of Bed)

**Purpose**: Prevents orthostatic hypotension before standing

**Steps**:
1. Raise HOB
2. Move patient to edge of bed
3. Lower legs over side
4. Assist to sitting
5. Sit 1-5 minutes
6. Assess vital signs, dizziness

---

### Mechanical Lift (Hoyer Lift)

**Indications**: Total assist, very heavy patient, unable to bear weight

**Steps**:
1. Two staff required
2. Position sling under patient
3. Attach sling to lift
4. Raise slowly, support patient
5. Move to chair/commode
6. Lower slowly

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Points

1. **Change position every 2 hours** to prevent pressure injuries
2. **Heels off bed** (use pillow under calves)
3. **Cane on stronger side**, move weaker leg with cane
4. **Stairs**: "Up with good, down with bad"
5. **Three-point crutch gait**: Both crutches + weak leg together
6. **Foot drop prevention**: Footboard, high-top sneakers, splints
7. **Orthostatic hypotension**: Gradual position changes, dangle first
8. **ROM**: 2-3 times daily, passive for unconscious patients
9. **Turn, cough, deep breathe** every 2 hours prevents pneumonia
10. **NEVER rest weight on crutch axilla** (nerve damage)

### Priority Nursing Diagnoses

- Impaired Physical Mobility
- Risk for Disuse Syndrome
- Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity
- Risk for Falls
- Risk for Injury

### Delegation

**Can delegate to UAP**:
- Position changes
- ROM (if trained, stable patient)
- Ambulation with assistive device (stable patient)

**Cannot delegate to UAP**:
- Initial mobility assessment
- Teaching
- Unstable patients
- Complex patients

## ‚úÖ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>1. Patient is using a cane. Which observation indicates correct use?</summary>

**Answer**: Patient holds the cane on the stronger side and moves the weaker leg forward with the cane

**Rationale**: Cane should be held on the stronger side to provide support for the weaker side. When walking, move the cane and weak leg together, then the strong leg. This provides maximum support and stability.
</details>

<details>
<summary>2. What is the PRIORITY intervention to prevent complications of immobility in a bedridden patient?</summary>

**Answer**: Reposition the patient every 2 hours

**Rationale**: Repositioning every 2 hours prevents pressure injuries (most common complication) and also helps prevent respiratory complications, contractures, and circulatory problems. This is the most comprehensive intervention.
</details>

<details>
<summary>3. Patient is learning crutch walking after ankle surgery (non-weight-bearing). Which gait should the nurse teach?</summary>

**Answer**: Three-point gait

**Rationale**: Three-point gait is used when one leg cannot bear weight. Both crutches and the affected leg move forward together, then the unaffected leg follows. This prevents weight on the injured ankle.
</details>

<details>
<summary>4. Which position is BEST for a patient with dyspnea?</summary>

**Answer**: High Fowler's position (80-90 degrees)

**Rationale**: High Fowler's position promotes maximum lung expansion and eases breathing by using gravity to pull abdominal contents away from the diaphragm. This is the best position for patients with respiratory distress.
</details>

<details>
<summary>5. Nurse is positioning a patient in Sims' position. Where should the nurse place the patient?</summary>

**Answer**: On the side with the lower arm behind the back and the upper leg flexed

**Rationale**: Sims' is a semi-prone position: on side, lower arm behind back, upper arm flexed forward on pillow, upper leg flexed more than lower leg. Used for rectal exams, enemas, and drainage in unconscious patients.
</details>

<details>
<summary>6. Patient with right-sided weakness is transferring from bed to wheelchair. Where should the nurse place the wheelchair?</summary>

**Answer**: On the patient's left side (stronger side)

**Rationale**: Wheelchair should be positioned on the patient's stronger side. This allows the patient to use the stronger side for support during transfer and lead with the stronger leg. Transfer toward strength.
</details>

<details>
<summary>7. Which action by the nurse prevents foot drop in an immobilized patient?</summary>

**Answer**: Apply high-top sneakers and ensure feet are supported in dorsiflexion

**Rationale**: Foot drop (plantar flexion contracture) is prevented by maintaining the foot in dorsiflexion using footboards, high-top sneakers, or splints. This keeps the ankle at 90 degrees.
</details>

<details>
<summary>8. Patient is going up stairs with crutches. What should the nurse teach?</summary>

**Answer**: "Move your unaffected leg up first, then the crutches and affected leg."

**Rationale**: When going up stairs: "Up with the good" - good leg goes up first, then crutches and bad leg follow. When going down: "Down with the bad" - crutches and bad leg go down first, then good leg. Mnemonic: "Up with good, down with bad."
</details>
