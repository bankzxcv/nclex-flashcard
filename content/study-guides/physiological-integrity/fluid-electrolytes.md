# Fluid & Electrolyte Balance

## Overview
Understanding fluid and electrolyte balance is essential for the NCLEX and clinical practice. This guide covers fluid compartments, imbalances, IV therapy, and nursing management.

---

## üíß Fluid Balance Basics

### Body Fluid Compartments

**Total Body Water**: ~60% of body weight (varies by age, gender, body composition)

| Compartment | % of Body Weight | Description |
|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Intracellular Fluid (ICF)** | 40% | Inside cells |
| **Extracellular Fluid (ECF)** | 20% | Outside cells |
| - Intravascular (plasma) | 5% | Inside blood vessels |
| - Interstitial | 15% | Between cells and blood vessels |

### Fluid Movement

**Osmosis**: Water moves from low solute concentration ‚Üí high solute concentration

**Filtration**: Fluid moves from high pressure ‚Üí low pressure

**Active Transport**: Movement against concentration gradient (requires energy)

### Fluid Regulation

**Hormones**:
- **ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone)**: ‚Üë Water reabsorption ‚Üí ‚Üì urine output
- **Aldosterone**: ‚Üë Sodium/water reabsorption, ‚Üë K+ excretion
- **ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide)**: ‚Üë Sodium/water excretion

**Thirst Mechanism**: Triggered by ‚Üë osmolality or ‚Üì blood volume

## üìä Fluid Imbalances

### Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD) - Hypovolemia/Dehydration

**Causes**:
- **Decreased intake**: NPO, dysphagia, altered LOC
- **Increased losses**:
  - GI: Vomiting, diarrhea, NG suction, fistulas
  - Renal: Diuretics, diabetes insipidus, diabetes mellitus
  - Skin: Burns, fever, diaphoresis
  - Third-spacing: Ascites, edema, burns

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypotension, orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, weak/thready pulse, flat neck veins |
| **Neurological** | Dizziness, confusion, lethargy, syncope |
| **Renal** | Decreased urine output (<30 mL/hr), concentrated urine, ‚Üë specific gravity (>1.030) |
| **Integumentary** | Poor skin turgor (tenting), dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes |
| **General** | Weight loss (acute: 1 kg = 1 L fluid), thirst, weakness |

**Lab Values**:
- ‚Üë Hematocrit (hemoconcentration)
- ‚Üë BUN
- ‚Üë Serum osmolality
- ‚Üë Urine specific gravity (>1.030)
- ‚Üë Sodium (if dehydration)

**Treatment**:
- **Oral rehydration** (if able to drink)
- **IV fluid replacement**:
  - **Isotonic solutions** (NS, LR) - first-line for hypovolemia
  - Rate depends on severity
- **Monitor**:
  - Vital signs
  - I&O (goal: urine output >30 mL/hr)
  - Daily weights
  - Skin turgor, mucous membranes
  - Lab values

---

### Fluid Volume Excess (FVE) - Hypervolemia/Fluid Overload

**Causes**:
- **Excessive intake**: Overhydration, rapid IV infusion
- **Decreased excretion**:
  - Heart failure
  - Renal failure
  - Liver cirrhosis (ascites)
  - Corticosteroid use
  - SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH)

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypertension, bounding pulse, distended neck veins (JVD), S3 heart sound |
| **Respiratory** | Dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles, pulmonary edema, frothy sputum |
| **Renal** | Increased urine output (unless renal failure), dilute urine, ‚Üì specific gravity |
| **Integumentary** | Edema (dependent areas), pitting edema, taut/shiny skin |
| **General** | Weight gain (acute: 1 kg = 1 L fluid retained) |

**Pitting Edema Scale**:
- **1+**: 2 mm depression, disappears rapidly
- **2+**: 4 mm depression, disappears in 10-15 seconds
- **3+**: 6 mm depression, lasts >1 minute
- **4+**: 8 mm depression, lasts 2-5 minutes

**Lab Values**:
- ‚Üì Hematocrit (hemodilution)
- ‚Üì BUN
- ‚Üì Serum osmolality
- ‚Üì Urine specific gravity (<1.010)
- ‚Üì Sodium (dilutional hyponatremia)

**Treatment**:
- **Fluid restriction**
- **Diuretics** (furosemide, bumetanide)
- **Sodium restriction**
- **Position**: High Fowler's (ease breathing)
- **Daily weights** (same time, same scale, same clothing)
- **Monitor**:
  - Vital signs
  - Lung sounds
  - I&O
  - Edema
  - Lab values
  - **Potassium** (diuretics cause loss)

**Critical**: **Pulmonary edema is life-threatening!**

## ‚ö° Electrolyte Imbalances

### Sodium (Na+) - Normal: 135-145 mEq/L

**Function**: Major extracellular cation, fluid balance, nerve/muscle function

#### Hyponatremia (<135 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **Dilutional**: SIADH, excess water intake, heart failure
- **Depletion**: Diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, burns, NPO with D5W
- **Renal losses**: Kidney disease, diuretics

**Signs & Symptoms** (Severity depends on how fast Na+ drops):

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **CNS** | Headache, confusion, lethargy, seizures, coma |
| **GI** | Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping |
| **Muscular** | Weakness, muscle cramps |
| **General** | Weight gain (if dilutional) |

**Mnemonic**: **"SALT LOSS"**
- **S**tupor/coma
- **A**norexia, nausea
- **L**ethargy
- **T**endon reflexes decreased
- **L**imp muscles (weakness)
- **O**rthostatic hypotension
- **S**eizures
- **S**tomach cramping

**Treatment**:
- **If hypovolemic**: IV 0.9% NaCl (normal saline)
- **If hypervolemic**: Fluid restriction, treat underlying cause
- **If severe (<120 mEq/L)**: 3% NaCl (hypertonic saline) - SLOWLY (risk of osmotic demyelination syndrome)
  - **Max correction**: 10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours
- **Sodium-rich diet**
- **Monitor**:
  - Neuro status (seizure precautions)
  - Serum sodium levels
  - I&O

**Critical**: **Rapid correction can cause central pontine myelinolysis!**

---

#### Hypernatremia (>145 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **Water loss**: Diabetes insipidus, diarrhea, fever, hyperventilation
- **Sodium gain**: Excessive sodium intake, near-drowning in salt water
- **Inadequate water intake**: Elderly, altered LOC, dysphagia

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **CNS** | Restlessness, agitation, confusion, seizures |
| **Integumentary** | Flushed skin, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor |
| **Muscular** | Muscle twitching, irritability |
| **General** | Intense thirst, fever |

**Mnemonic**: **"FRIED"**
- **F**ever (low-grade)
- **R**estless/irritable
- **I**ncreased fluid retention and BP
- **E**dema (peripheral and pitting)
- **D**ecreased urine output, dry mouth

**Treatment**:
- **Increase water intake** (PO or IV)
- **IV hypotonic solutions** (0.45% NaCl, D5W)
- **Correct slowly**: Max 10 mEq/L per 24 hours (prevent cerebral edema)
- **Treat underlying cause**
- **Monitor**:
  - Neuro status
  - Serum sodium
  - I&O

---

### Potassium (K+) - Normal: 3.5-5.0 mEq/L

**Function**: Major intracellular cation, cardiac/muscle function, nerve impulses

**Critical**: **K+ imbalances are life-threatening due to cardiac effects!**

#### Hypokalemia (<3.5 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **GI losses**: Vomiting, diarrhea, NG suction, laxative abuse
- **Renal losses**: Diuretics (loop, thiazide), hyperaldosteronism
- **Insufficient intake**: NPO, anorexia
- **Intracellular shift**: Insulin, alkalosis
- **Medications**: Corticosteroids, amphotericin B

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiac** | **Life-threatening arrhythmias**, weak/irregular pulse, ECG changes (flat T wave, U wave, ST depression) |
| **Muscular** | Weakness, fatigue, leg cramps, decreased DTRs |
| **GI** | Decreased motility, constipation, paralytic ileus, N/V |
| **Renal** | Polyuria |
| **Respiratory** | Shallow respirations, respiratory arrest (if severe) |

**ECG Changes**:
- Flat or inverted T waves
- Prominent U wave (most specific)
- ST depression
- Prolonged PR interval
- Dysrhythmias (PVCs, V-tach)

**Mnemonic**: **"A 10 MEDS"** (6 L's)
- **A** - Alkalosis
- **1** - 1 shallow respirations
- **0** - 0 urine (polyuria)
- **M** - Muscle weakness
- **E** - ECG changes
- **D** - Decreased bowel sounds/DTRs
- **S** - Seizures

**Treatment**:
- **Oral K+ supplements**: Tablets, liquid (with food/juice)
- **IV potassium**:
  - **NEVER give IV push** (lethal!)
  - **Dilute** in IV fluid
  - **Max concentration**: 40 mEq/L peripheral line (higher for central line)
  - **Max rate**: 10-20 mEq/hr (faster rates need cardiac monitoring)
  - **Painful** - warn patient
- **Potassium-rich foods**: Bananas, oranges, potatoes, spinach, tomatoes
- **Monitor**:
  - **Cardiac monitoring** (ECG)
  - Serum K+ levels
  - I&O
  - **Digoxin toxicity risk** (hypokalemia potentiates digoxin)

**Critical Points**:
- **NEVER give K+ IV push!**
- Assess **urine output ‚â•30 mL/hr** before giving K+ (K+ is excreted by kidneys)
- **K+ is irritating to veins** - dilute well, infuse slowly

---

#### Hyperkalemia (>5.0 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **Renal failure** (most common - K+ not excreted)
- **Excessive intake**: K+ supplements, salt substitutes, blood transfusions
- **Intracellular release**: Tissue damage (burns, crush injuries, hemolysis), acidosis
- **Medications**: K+-sparing diuretics (spironolactone), ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiac** | **Life-threatening arrhythmias**, **cardiac arrest**, ECG changes (tall peaked T waves, wide QRS, prolonged PR) |
| **Muscular** | Early: Muscle twitching, cramps<br>Late: Flaccid paralysis |
| **GI** | Diarrhea, hyperactive bowel sounds, abdominal cramping |
| **Neurological** | Anxiety, irritability, paresthesias |

**ECG Changes** (Progressive):
1. **Tall, peaked T waves** (earliest sign)
2. Flat P waves
3. Prolonged PR interval
4. Widened QRS complex
5. **V-fib ‚Üí Asystole** (if untreated)

**Mnemonic**: **"MURDER"**
- **M**uscle weakness
- **U**rine (oliguria/anuria)
- **R**espiratory failure
- **D**ecreased cardiac contractility
- **E**CG changes (peaked T waves)
- **R**eflexes (hyperreflexia ‚Üí flaccid paralysis)

**Treatment** (Based on severity and ECG changes):

**IMMEDIATE** (if ECG changes or K+ >6.5):

1. **Calcium gluconate IV** (10-20 mL of 10% solution over 2-5 min)
   - Protects heart (stabilizes cardiac membrane)
   - **Does NOT lower K+**
   - Onset: 1-3 minutes, duration: 30-60 minutes

2. **Shift K+ into cells** (temporary):
   - **Regular insulin 10 units IV + D50W** (25-50 g glucose)
   - **Sodium bicarbonate** (if acidotic)
   - **Albuterol nebulizer**

3. **Remove K+ from body** (definitive treatment):
   - **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) - if kidneys functioning
   - **Kayexalate (sodium polystyrene sulfonate)** PO or rectally
     - Onset: hours
   - **Dialysis** (if renal failure or refractory)

**Ongoing**:
- **Restrict potassium intake**
- **Stop K+ supplements/K+-sparing diuretics**
- **Continuous cardiac monitoring**
- **Serial K+ levels**

**Critical**: **Hyperkalemia >6.5 mEq/L = MEDICAL EMERGENCY!**

### Calcium (Ca¬≤‚Å∫) - Normal: 8.5-10.5 mg/dL (or 4.5-5.5 mEq/L)

**Function**: Bone strength, muscle contraction, nerve function, blood clotting

**Note**: 50% bound to albumin - check albumin levels when interpreting calcium

#### Hypocalcemia (<8.5 mg/dL)

**Causes**:
- **Hypoparathyroidism** (post-thyroidectomy)
- **Vitamin D deficiency**
- **Chronic renal failure** (‚Üì activation of vitamin D)
- **Hypoalbuminemia**
- **Pancreatitis**
- **Excessive loss**: Diarrhea, wound drainage
- **Medications**: Loop diuretics, phosphate

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Neuromuscular** | **Tetany**, muscle spasms, twitching, seizures, hyperreflexia, paresthesias (perioral, fingers, toes) |
| **Cardiac** | Prolonged QT interval, arrhythmias, decreased cardiac contractility |
| **Respiratory** | Laryngospasm, bronchospasm |
| **GI** | Increased peristalsis, diarrhea, cramping |

**Assessment Signs**:
- **Trousseau's sign**: Carpopedal spasm when BP cuff inflated above systolic for 3 min
- **Chvostek's sign**: Facial twitching when tapping facial nerve (below ear)

**Mnemonic**: **"CATS go numb"**
- **C**onvulsions
- **A**rrhythmias
- **T**etany, Trousseau's/Chvostek's
- **S**pasms (muscle)

**Treatment**:
- **IV calcium gluconate** (preferred) or calcium chloride
  - Give **slowly** over 10-20 minutes
  - **Cardiac monitoring** during infusion
  - **Infiltration = tissue necrosis** - ensure patent IV
- **Oral calcium supplements** with vitamin D
- **High-calcium diet**: Dairy, leafy greens
- **Monitor**:
  - Trousseau's and Chvostek's signs
  - Serum calcium, albumin
  - **Seizure precautions**
  - **Airway** (risk of laryngospasm)

**Critical**: Keep **calcium gluconate at bedside** after thyroidectomy!

---

#### Hypercalcemia (>10.5 mg/dL)

**Causes**:
- **Hyperparathyroidism**
- **Malignancy** (bone metastases, parathyroid tumors)
- **Prolonged immobilization** (bone resorption)
- **Excess vitamin D**
- **Thiazide diuretics**

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| System | Findings |
|--------|----------|
| **Neuromuscular** | Muscle weakness, fatigue, decreased DTRs, lethargy, confusion, coma |
| **Cardiac** | Shortened QT interval, bradycardia, hypertension, arrhythmias, **digitalis toxicity** |
| **GI** | Anorexia, N/V, constipation, abdominal pain |
| **Renal** | Polyuria, polydipsia, kidney stones, flank pain |

**Mnemonic**: **"Stones, Bones, Groans, Moans"**
- **Stones**: Kidney stones
- **Bones**: Bone pain
- **Groans**: Abdominal pain (constipation, N/V)
- **Moans**: Mental status changes (confusion, lethargy)

**Treatment**:
- **IV normal saline** (aggressive hydration)
- **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) - after hydration
- **Calcitonin** (rapid onset, short duration)
- **Bisphosphonates** (slow onset, long duration)
- **Low-calcium diet**
- **Increase mobility** (prevents bone resorption)
- **Monitor**:
  - Cardiac monitoring
  - I&O
  - Renal function

### Magnesium (Mg¬≤‚Å∫) - Normal: 1.5-2.5 mEq/L

**Function**: Enzyme reactions, muscle/nerve function, bone health

**Note**: Often occurs with calcium and potassium imbalances

#### Hypomagnesemia (<1.5 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **Malnutrition**, chronic alcoholism
- **GI losses**: Diarrhea, NG suction, malabsorption
- **Renal losses**: Diuretics
- **Medications**: PPIs, aminoglycosides

**Signs & Symptoms**:
- Similar to hypocalcemia: **Tetany, tremors, seizures**
- **Positive Trousseau's and Chvostek's signs**
- **Cardiac arrhythmias**
- **Hyperreflexia**

**Treatment**:
- **IV magnesium sulfate**
- **Oral magnesium supplements**
- **Magnesium-rich diet**: Nuts, seeds, whole grains, leafy greens

---

#### Hypermagnesemia (>2.5 mEq/L)

**Causes**:
- **Renal failure** (decreased excretion)
- **Excessive intake**: Antacids, laxatives (Milk of Magnesia)
- **Magnesium sulfate** (pre-eclampsia treatment)

**Signs & Symptoms**:

| Mg¬≤‚Å∫ Level | Findings |
|------------|----------|
| **>4 mEq/L** | Hypotension, bradycardia, N/V, flushing, warmth |
| **>5 mEq/L** | Drowsiness, **decreased/absent DTRs** |
| **>7 mEq/L** | Muscle weakness, **respiratory depression** |
| **>10 mEq/L** | **Complete heart block** |
| **>13 mEq/L** | **Cardiac arrest** |

**Mnemonic**: **"LOW Mag = HYPER, HIGH Mag = HYPO"**
- Low Mg = Hyperactive (twitching, seizures)
- High Mg = Hypoactive (decreased reflexes, sedation)

**Treatment**:
- **STOP magnesium** (antacids, laxatives)
- **IV calcium gluconate** (antidote)
- **Loop diuretics** + IV fluids (increase excretion)
- **Dialysis** (if severe or renal failure)
- **Monitor**:
  - **Deep tendon reflexes** (loss of reflexes = toxicity)
  - **Respiratory rate** (if <12, hold magnesium)
  - Cardiac monitoring

**Critical**: When giving **magnesium sulfate** (pre-eclampsia), monitor:
- DTRs (patellar reflex)
- Respiratory rate (‚â•12)
- Urine output (‚â•30 mL/hr)
- **Have calcium gluconate at bedside** (antidote)

## üíâ IV Fluid Therapy

### Types of IV Solutions

#### Isotonic Solutions (Same osmolality as blood ~275-295 mOsm/L)

| Solution | Use | Considerations |
|----------|-----|----------------|
| **0.9% NaCl (Normal Saline)** | Fluid resuscitation, hypovolemia, blood transfusions | Can cause hypernatremia, fluid overload |
| **Lactated Ringer's (LR)** | Hypovolemia, burns, GI losses | Contains K+ (don't use in hyperkalemia or renal failure); contraindicated with blood transfusions |
| **D5W** | Initially isotonic, becomes hypotonic when dextrose metabolized | Free water replacement |

#### Hypotonic Solutions (Lower osmolality than blood)

| Solution | Use | Considerations |
|----------|-----|----------------|
| **0.45% NaCl (Half-Normal Saline)** | Hypernatremia, cellular dehydration | **Risk: Cellular swelling, hypotension** |
| **D5W** (after dextrose metabolized) | Free water replacement | Monitor for hyponatremia |

#### Hypertonic Solutions (Higher osmolality than blood)

| Solution | Use | Considerations |
|----------|-----|----------------|
| **3% NaCl** | Severe hyponatremia (<120 mEq/L) | **Risk: Fluid overload, pulmonary edema**; requires ICU monitoring |
| **D10W** | Hypoglycemia | High glucose concentration |
| **TPN** | Nutrition support | Central line required |

---

### IV Complications

| Complication | Signs | Prevention | Intervention |
|--------------|-------|------------|--------------|
| **Infiltration** | Swelling, coolness, pallor, pain | Secure catheter, monitor site | STOP IV, elevate extremity, warm compress |
| **Phlebitis** | Redness, warmth, pain along vein | Rotate sites q72-96hr, avoid lower extremities | STOP IV, apply warm compress |
| **Extravasation** (vesicant leaks) | Severe pain, tissue necrosis | Use central line for vesicants | STOP immediately, don't flush, antidote per protocol |
| **Fluid overload** | Dyspnea, crackles, JVD, edema | Monitor I&O, lung sounds, VS | Slow/stop infusion, elevate HOB, diuretics, O2 |
| **Air embolism** | Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypotension | Remove air from tubing, secure connections | Left side, Trendelenburg, 100% O2, call provider |
| **Infection** | Redness, warmth, drainage at site, fever | Aseptic technique, change per protocol | Remove IV, culture site/tip, antibiotics |

## üéØ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Points

1. **"Sodium follows water, water follows sodium"**
2. **K+ >6.5 or ECG changes** = Emergency! Calcium gluconate first
3. **Never give K+ IV push** - always dilute
4. **Check urine output before giving K+** (‚â•30 mL/hr)
5. **Trousseau's/Chvostek's** = Hypocalcemia OR Hypomagnesemia
6. **Peaked T waves** = Hyperkalemia
7. **Flat T waves, U waves** = Hypokalemia
8. **Hypokalemia** = Increased risk of **digoxin toxicity**
9. **Magnesium antidote** = Calcium gluconate
10. **Monitor DTRs when giving magnesium sulfate** (loss of reflexes = toxicity)
11. **Daily weights**: Same time, same scale, same clothing (1 kg = 1 L fluid)
12. **Low Mg = Hyperactive**, **High Mg = Hypoactive**

### Priority Assessments

**Before Giving K+**:
- Urine output ‚â•30 mL/hr (K+ excreted by kidneys)
- Serum K+ level
- Cardiac rhythm

**Before Giving Magnesium Sulfate**:
- Deep tendon reflexes (patellar)
- Respiratory rate ‚â•12
- Urine output ‚â•30 mL/hr
- Have calcium gluconate at bedside

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient's lab shows K+ = 6.8 mEq/L with peaked T waves on ECG. What is the priority intervention?</summary>

**Answer**: Administer calcium gluconate IV immediately

**Rationale**: K+ >6.5 with ECG changes is a life-threatening emergency. Calcium gluconate stabilizes the cardiac membrane and protects the heart. It does NOT lower potassium but prevents cardiac arrest. Then give insulin + D50W to shift K+ into cells, followed by kayexalate or dialysis to remove K+ from the body.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient receiving furosemide (loop diuretic) reports muscle weakness and leg cramps. Which lab is priority to check?</summary>

**Answer**: Potassium level

**Rationale**: Loop diuretics cause potassium loss. Muscle weakness and leg cramps are classic signs of hypokalemia. This patient is at high risk for cardiac arrhythmias and digoxin toxicity (if taking digoxin). Check K+ level and supplement as ordered.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A patient post-thyroidectomy develops circumoral paresthesias and hand spasms. What should the nurse do first?</summary>

**Answer**: Check Trousseau's and Chvostek's signs, then notify provider

**Rationale**: This indicates hypocalcemia from accidental parathyroid removal/damage during thyroidectomy. Trousseau's (carpopedal spasm with BP cuff) and Chvostek's (facial twitching) confirm hypocalcemia. This can progress to tetany, seizures, and laryngospasm. Keep calcium gluconate at bedside. Notify provider for calcium replacement order.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A pregnant patient receiving magnesium sulfate for pre-eclampsia has absent patellar reflexes. What action should the nurse take?</summary>

**Answer**: STOP the magnesium sulfate infusion immediately and notify provider

**Rationale**: Loss of deep tendon reflexes indicates magnesium toxicity. This can progress to respiratory depression and cardiac arrest. STOP infusion. Check respiratory rate, vital signs. Prepare to give calcium gluconate (antidote). When giving magnesium sulfate, monitor: DTRs, RR ‚â•12, UO ‚â•30 mL/hr.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient with heart failure has crackles in lung bases, JVD, and 3+ pitting edema in ankles. What is the priority intervention?</summary>

**Answer**: Administer prescribed diuretic and restrict fluids

**Rationale**: These are signs of fluid volume excess/fluid overload. Priority is reducing fluid volume with diuretics and fluid restriction. Position in high Fowler's to ease breathing. Monitor I&O, daily weights, lung sounds. Watch for potassium loss from diuretics.
</details>
