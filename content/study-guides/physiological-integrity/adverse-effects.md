# Adverse Effects & Drug Reactions

## Overview
Recognizing and managing adverse drug reactions is a critical nursing responsibility and heavily tested on the NCLEX. This guide covers adverse effects, allergic reactions, anaphylaxis, and medication interactions.

---

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Types of Drug Reactions

### Side Effects vs. Adverse Effects vs. Allergic Reactions

| Type | Definition | Severity | Example |
|------|------------|----------|---------|
| **Side Effect** | Expected, predictable response | Mild to moderate | Drowsiness from antihistamine, dry mouth from anticholinergics |
| **Adverse Effect** | Unintended, harmful response | Moderate to severe | Respiratory depression from opioids, bleeding from anticoagulants |
| **Allergic Reaction** | Immune system response | Mild to life-threatening | Rash, hives, anaphylaxis |
| **Toxic Effect** | Drug accumulation to dangerous levels | Severe | Digoxin toxicity, acetaminophen overdose |

## üö® Anaphylaxis - Life-Threatening Emergency

### Recognition: Signs & Symptoms

**Onset**: Usually within **minutes** of exposure (can occur up to 1 hour)

| System | Signs/Symptoms |
|--------|----------------|
| **Respiratory** | Dyspnea, wheezing, stridor, tightness in throat/chest, hoarseness |
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypotension, tachycardia, weak pulse, dizziness, syncope |
| **Integumentary** | Urticaria (hives), pruritus, flushing, angioedema (swelling of face/lips/tongue) |
| **Gastrointestinal** | Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramping |
| **Neurological** | Anxiety, sense of impending doom, confusion, loss of consciousness |

### Immediate Nursing Actions - Follow ABC

**Mnemonic: STOP-CALL-EPI**

1. **STOP** medication/infusion immediately
2. **CALL** for help - activate Rapid Response/Code Blue
3. **EPI**nephrine administration

**Detailed Intervention Steps**:

1. **STOP** the medication/transfusion
2. **Maintain airway** - place patient supine, elevate legs (modified Trendelenburg)
3. **Call for help** - Rapid Response Team
4. **Administer Epinephrine**:
   - **0.3-0.5 mg IM** (1:1000 solution)
   - **Anterolateral thigh** (vastus lateralis)
   - **Repeat every 5-15 minutes** if needed
5. **Establish IV access** if not present
6. **Administer oxygen** - high-flow, prepare for intubation
7. **IV fluids** - normal saline bolus for hypotension
8. **Secondary medications** (after epinephrine):
   - Antihistamines: Diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV
   - H2 blocker: Ranitidine or famotidine
   - Corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone IV (prevent biphasic reaction)
   - Bronchodilators: Albuterol if bronchospasm
9. **Monitor vital signs** continuously
10. **Document** thoroughly

### Why Epinephrine First?

| Action | Benefit |
|--------|---------|
| **Alpha-agonist** | Vasoconstriction ‚Üí Increases BP |
| **Beta-1 agonist** | Increases cardiac output |
| **Beta-2 agonist** | Bronchodilation ‚Üí Improves breathing |
| **Stabilizes mast cells** | Prevents further histamine release |

**Critical Point**: **Epinephrine is the ONLY first-line treatment for anaphylaxis!** Antihistamines and steroids are secondary.

## üíâ Common Medications Causing Anaphylaxis

### High-Risk Medications

| Category | Examples | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Antibiotics** | Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Sulfonamides | Most common cause of drug anaphylaxis |
| **NSAIDs** | Aspirin, Ibuprofen, Ketorolac | Cross-reactivity common |
| **Contrast Dye** | Iodinated contrast media | Higher risk with shellfish allergy (controversial) |
| **Biologics** | Monoclonal antibodies | Infusion reactions common |
| **Chemotherapy** | Platinum agents, Taxanes | Premedication often required |
| **Blood Products** | Packed RBCs, Platelets | IgA deficiency risk |
| **Neuromuscular Blockers** | Rocuronium, Succinylcholine | During anesthesia |
| **Latex** | Gloves, catheters | Cross-reactivity with banana, avocado, kiwi |

### Cross-Allergies to Know

**Penicillin ‚Üí Cephalosporins**:
- 10% cross-reactivity
- Use cephalosporins cautiously, start with later generations (less cross-reactivity)

**Sulfonamide antibiotics ‚Üí Other sulfonamides**:
- Different sulfonamide groups have minimal cross-reactivity
- Furosemide and thiazides are generally safe

## üî¥ Drug-Specific Adverse Effects

### Cardiovascular Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Digoxin** | Toxicity (N/V, vision changes, arrhythmias) | Check level (therapeutic: 0.5-2.0), monitor K+ | Hold if HR <60, give digoxin immune fab if severe |
| **Beta Blockers** | Bradycardia, hypotension, bronchospasm | HR, BP, lung sounds | Hold if HR <60 or SBP <90 |
| **ACE Inhibitors** | Persistent dry cough, angioedema, hyperkalemia | Cough, facial swelling, K+ levels | Switch to ARB if cough, stop immediately if angioedema |
| **Warfarin** | Bleeding, hemorrhage | PT/INR, bleeding signs | Vitamin K (antidote), hold dose |
| **Heparin** | Bleeding, HIT (thrombocytopenia) | aPTT, platelet count | Protamine sulfate (antidote) |

### Respiratory Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Albuterol** | Tachycardia, tremors, hypokalemia | HR, K+ level | Monitor HR, typically resolves |
| **Corticosteroids (inhaled)** | Oral thrush, hoarseness | Oral cavity exam | Rinse mouth after use |
| **Corticosteroids (systemic)** | Hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, infection risk | Glucose, bone density, infection signs | Monitor glucose, calcium/vitamin D, avoid sick contacts |

### Gastrointestinal Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **PPIs** | C. diff risk, decreased calcium/B12 absorption | Diarrhea, bone health | Use lowest effective dose |
| **Metoclopramide** | Tardive dyskinesia, extrapyramidal symptoms | Involuntary movements | Discontinue immediately |
| **Ondansetron** | QT prolongation, constipation | ECG, bowel sounds | Monitor ECG, encourage fluids |

### Pain Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Opioids** | Respiratory depression, constipation, sedation | Respiratory rate, bowel function, LOC | Naloxone if RR <12, stool softeners |
| **NSAIDs** | GI bleeding, renal impairment | Stool for blood, BUN/Cr | Take with food, monitor kidney function |
| **Acetaminophen** | Hepatotoxicity | Liver enzymes, daily dose <4g | Acetylcysteine (antidote) |

### Neurological/Psychiatric Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Phenytoin** | Gingival hyperplasia, Stevens-Johnson syndrome | Gum health, rash | Good oral hygiene, STOP if rash develops |
| **Lithium** | Toxicity (tremor, confusion, seizures) | Lithium level (therapeutic: 0.6-1.2) | Maintain hydration, monitor level |
| **SSRIs** | Serotonin syndrome, suicidal ideation | Mental status, vital signs | STOP drug, supportive care |
| **MAOIs** | Hypertensive crisis with tyramine | BP, dietary compliance | Avoid tyramine foods, phentolamine for crisis |

### Endocrine Medications

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Insulin** | Hypoglycemia, lipodystrophy | Blood glucose, injection sites | Give glucose/glucagon, rotate sites |
| **Levothyroxine** | Thyroid storm if excessive | HR, BP, temperature, tremor | Reduce dose |
| **Corticosteroids** | Adrenal insufficiency with abrupt stop | Hypotension, weakness, N/V | **NEVER stop abruptly** - taper |

### Antibiotics

| Drug | Adverse Effect | Assessment | Intervention |
|------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| **Aminoglycosides** | Ototoxicity, nephrotoxicity | Hearing, tinnitus, BUN/Cr | Monitor levels (peak/trough) |
| **Vancomycin** | Red man syndrome, nephrotoxicity | Flushing, hypotension, BUN/Cr | Slow infusion rate |
| **Fluoroquinolones** | Tendon rupture, QT prolongation | Joint pain, ECG | Stop if tendon pain |
| **Tetracyclines** | Photosensitivity, tooth discoloration | Sun exposure, dental health | Sunscreen, avoid in children <8 years |

## üß¨ Serious Adverse Reactions

### Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)

**Life-threatening skin reactions**

**Triggers**: Sulfonamides, anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine), allopurinol, NSAIDs

**Signs**:
- Prodrome: Fever, malaise, sore throat
- Skin: Painful rash ‚Üí blisters ‚Üí sloughing (looks like severe burns)
- Mucous membranes: Oral, ocular, genital involvement
- Positive **Nikolsky sign** (skin sloughs with light pressure)

**Nursing Actions**:
1. **STOP causative drug immediately**
2. Transfer to burn unit
3. Supportive care (fluids, pain management, wound care)
4. Monitor for infection
5. Ophthalmology consult (prevent blindness)

### Serotonin Syndrome

**Excessive serotonin activity** - potentially fatal

**Causes**: SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, tramadol, linezolid, St. John's Wort (especially in combination)

**Signs - Triad of**:
1. **Mental status changes**: Agitation, confusion, hallucinations
2. **Autonomic instability**: Hyperthermia, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis
3. **Neuromuscular hyperactivity**: Tremor, rigidity, myoclonus, hyperreflexia, clonus

**Nursing Actions**:
1. **STOP serotonergic drugs**
2. Supportive care (cooling, IV fluids)
3. Benzodiazepines for agitation
4. Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist)

### Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

**Rare but life-threatening reaction to antipsychotics**

**Signs**:
- **Severe muscle rigidity** ("lead pipe")
- **Hyperthermia** (>104¬∞F)
- **Altered mental status**
- **Autonomic instability**
- Elevated CK (creatine kinase)

**Nursing Actions**:
1. **STOP antipsychotic immediately**
2. Aggressive cooling measures
3. IV fluids
4. Dantrolene (muscle relaxant)
5. ICU transfer

### Agranulocytosis

**Severe decrease in WBCs** - high infection risk

**Causes**: Clozapine (most common), methimazole, carbamazepine

**Signs**:
- Fever, sore throat, mouth ulcers
- WBC <500/mm¬≥

**Nursing Actions**:
1. Stop causative medication
2. Reverse isolation
3. Broad-spectrum antibiotics
4. Colony-stimulating factors

## üîÑ Drug Interactions

### Pharmacokinetic Interactions

**Absorption**:
- **Tetracycline + Dairy**: Decreased absorption (chelation)
- **Levothyroxine + Iron**: Give 4 hours apart

**Metabolism** (CYP450 enzymes):
- **Grapefruit juice**: Inhibits metabolism ‚Üí increased drug levels (statins, CCBs)
- **St. John's Wort**: Induces metabolism ‚Üí decreased drug levels (birth control, warfarin)

**Excretion**:
- **NSAIDs**: Decrease lithium excretion ‚Üí toxicity risk

### Pharmacodynamic Interactions

**Additive Effects** (same action):
- **CNS depressants**: Alcohol + opioids + benzodiazepines ‚Üí severe sedation/respiratory depression
- **Anticoagulants**: Warfarin + aspirin + NSAIDs ‚Üí increased bleeding risk

**Antagonistic Effects** (opposing actions):
- **Beta blocker + Albuterol**: Reduced bronchodilation
- **Dopamine agonist + Antipsychotic**: Reduced effectiveness

### High-Risk Interactions Table

| Drug 1 | Drug 2 | Result | Management |
|--------|--------|--------|------------|
| **Warfarin** | NSAIDs, Aspirin | Increased bleeding | Avoid if possible, monitor INR closely |
| **Digoxin** | Loop diuretics | Hypokalemia ‚Üí digoxin toxicity | Monitor K+, supplement if needed |
| **ACE inhibitor** | K+-sparing diuretic | Hyperkalemia | Monitor K+ closely |
| **MAOIs** | SSRIs | Serotonin syndrome | Allow 2-week washout period |
| **MAOIs** | Tyramine foods | Hypertensive crisis | Avoid aged cheese, wine, smoked meats |
| **Statins** | Gemfibrozil | Rhabdomyolysis | Avoid combination |
| **Metformin** | Contrast dye | Lactic acidosis | Hold metformin 48 hrs before/after |
| **Aminoglycosides** | Loop diuretics | Increased ototoxicity | Monitor hearing |

## üç∑ Drug-Food Interactions

| Drug | Food/Nutrient | Interaction | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Warfarin** | Vitamin K (leafy greens) | Decreased anticoagulation | Maintain **consistent** intake |
| **MAOIs** | Tyramine (aged cheese, wine) | Hypertensive crisis | **Avoid** completely |
| **Tetracycline** | Dairy products | Decreased absorption | Take 1 hr before or 2 hrs after meals |
| **Levothyroxine** | Food, coffee, soy | Decreased absorption | Take on **empty stomach** |
| **Grapefruit juice** | Statins, CCBs, many drugs | Increased drug levels | **Avoid** grapefruit |
| **Potassium-sparing diuretics** | High-K+ foods (bananas) | Hyperkalemia | Limit K+ intake |
| **Iron supplements** | Coffee, tea, calcium | Decreased absorption | Take with vitamin C, separate from calcium |

## üìä Monitoring for Adverse Effects

### Baseline Assessment Before Administration

**Always assess**:
1. **Allergies** - drug, food, environmental
2. **Vital signs** - baseline for comparison
3. **Renal function** - many drugs cleared by kidneys
4. **Hepatic function** - many drugs metabolized by liver
5. **Current medications** - check for interactions
6. **Pregnancy/breastfeeding** - contraindications
7. **Age** - pediatric/geriatric dosing differences

### Drug-Specific Monitoring

| Drug Class | Before Giving | During Therapy | Labs to Monitor |
|------------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Antihypertensives** | BP, HR | BP, orthostatic changes | BUN/Cr, electrolytes |
| **Anticoagulants** | Bleeding risk | Signs of bleeding | PT/INR (warfarin), aPTT (heparin), platelets |
| **Opioids** | Pain level, RR | Sedation level, RR, pain | None routinely |
| **Aminoglycosides** | Hearing, renal function | Hearing, urine output | Peak/trough levels, BUN/Cr |
| **Digoxin** | HR, K+ level | HR, signs of toxicity | Digoxin level, K+ |
| **Insulin** | Blood glucose | Blood glucose, hypoglycemia signs | Blood glucose, HbA1c |
| **Lithium** | Thyroid, renal function | Tremor, confusion, thirst | Lithium level, TSH, BUN/Cr |

## ü©∫ NCLEX Tips

### High-Yield Testing Points

1. **Anaphylaxis = Epinephrine IM first**, then antihistamines/steroids
2. **Stop the drug immediately** if serious adverse effect suspected
3. **Notify provider** of adverse effects - document thoroughly
4. **Know antidotes**:
   - Warfarin ‚Üí Vitamin K
   - Heparin ‚Üí Protamine sulfate
   - Opioids ‚Üí Naloxone
   - Benzodiazepines ‚Üí Flumazenil
   - Acetaminophen ‚Üí Acetylcysteine
   - Digoxin ‚Üí Digoxin immune fab
5. **Patient has "right to refuse"** - don't force medication
6. **Hold beta blocker if HR <60**, hold antihypertensive if BP too low
7. **Check for allergies** before EVERY medication administration
8. **Therapeutic vs. toxic levels** - know the difference

### Priority Questions

**What to do FIRST when adverse reaction occurs?**
1. **Life-threatening** (anaphylaxis, respiratory depression) ‚Üí Intervene immediately
2. **Not immediately life-threatening** ‚Üí Assess first, then intervene

**Patient safety always comes first!**

## üìù Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A patient develops hives, wheezing, and hypotension 5 minutes after starting an IV antibiotic. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Stop the infusion immediately and administer epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM.

**Rationale**: This is anaphylaxis - a life-threatening emergency. Stop the causative agent immediately. Epinephrine is the FIRST-LINE treatment (not antihistamines). Give IM in the anterolateral thigh. Call for help, maintain airway, give oxygen.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A patient taking warfarin has an INR of 5.2 and reports bleeding gums. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold the warfarin dose and notify the provider immediately.

**Rationale**: Therapeutic INR is 2.0-3.0. An INR of 5.2 with bleeding indicates excessive anticoagulation. Hold the dose. Provider may order vitamin K (antidote). Assess for other bleeding signs. INR should be checked before each warfarin dose.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A patient on an SSRI develops agitation, tremor, diaphoresis, and hyperthermia. What does the nurse suspect?</summary>

**Answer**: Serotonin syndrome

**Rationale**: Classic triad: mental status changes (agitation), autonomic instability (diaphoresis, hyperthermia), and neuromuscular changes (tremor). This is a medical emergency. Stop the SSRI immediately and notify provider. May occur with SSRI alone or in combination with other serotonergic drugs.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Before administering digoxin, the nurse notes HR = 56 bpm. What action should be taken?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold the digoxin and notify the provider.

**Rationale**: Hold digoxin if HR <60 bpm. Digoxin slows heart rate; giving it when HR is already low can cause severe bradycardia or heart block. Always check apical pulse for 1 full minute before giving digoxin.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A patient taking an aminoglycoside reports "ringing in my ears." What is the priority nursing action?</summary>

**Answer**: Hold the medication and notify the provider immediately.

**Rationale**: Tinnitus (ringing in ears) is an early sign of ototoxicity from aminoglycosides. This can be irreversible if the drug is not stopped. Hold the dose and notify provider. Monitor peak and trough levels.
</details>
