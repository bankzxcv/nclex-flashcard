### GTPAL System

Obstetric history documentation:

**G** = **Gravida** (total number of pregnancies)
**T** = **Term** births (â‰¥37 weeks)
**P** = **Preterm** births (20-36 weeks)
**A** = **Abortions** (spontaneous or induced, <20 weeks)
**L** = **Living** children

**Example**: G4 T2 P1 A1 L3
- 4 total pregnancies
- 2 term births
- 1 preterm birth
- 1 abortion/miscarriage
- 3 living children

**NCLEX Tip**: Twins count as ONE pregnancy but TWO in T or P category

---

### Calculating Due Date

**Naegele's Rule**:
1. Take first day of last menstrual period (LMP)
2. Subtract 3 months
3. Add 7 days
4. Add 1 year

**Example**:
- LMP: April 10, 2025
- Subtract 3 months: January 10, 2025
- Add 7 days: January 17, 2025
- Add 1 year: January 17, 2026

**Alternative**: LMP + 280 days (40 weeks)

## ğŸ§¬ Prenatal Testing

### First Trimester Screening (11-14 weeks)

**Combined Test**:
- Ultrasound: Nuchal translucency (NT) measurement
- Blood work: hCG, PAPP-A (pregnancy-associated plasma protein A)

**Screens for**: Down syndrome (Trisomy 21), Trisomy 18

**Increased Risk**:
- Increased NT thickness
- Elevated hCG
- Decreased PAPP-A

---

### Second Trimester Screening (15-20 weeks)

**Quad Screen** (Maternal Serum Alpha-Fetoprotein screening):
- AFP (Alpha-fetoprotein)
- hCG
- Estriol
- Inhibin A

**Screens for**: Neural tube defects, Down syndrome, Trisomy 18

**Results**:
- **Elevated AFP**: Neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly), abdominal wall defects, multiples
- **Low AFP**: Down syndrome

---

### Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing (NIPT)

**Cell-Free DNA Testing** (10+ weeks):
- Analyzes fetal DNA in maternal blood
- Highly accurate for Down syndrome, Trisomy 18, Trisomy 13
- Can determine fetal sex, Rh status

**Advantages**: Non-invasive, early, accurate
**Disadvantages**: Cost, not diagnostic (screening only)

---

### Diagnostic Testing

#### Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)

**Timing**: 10-13 weeks

**Method**: Transcervical or transabdominal sample of placental tissue

**Advantages**: Early diagnosis

**Risks**: Miscarriage (1%), Rh sensitization (give RhoGAM if Rh negative)

**NCLEX Tip**: Monitor for cramping, bleeding, infection after procedure

---

#### Amniocentesis

**Timing**: 15-20 weeks (genetic testing), 36+ weeks (lung maturity)

**Method**: Transabdominal needle aspiration of amniotic fluid

**Purposes**:
- Genetic testing (karyotype)
- Neural tube defect screening (AFP)
- Fetal lung maturity (L/S ratio)
- Intrauterine infection

**Fetal Lung Maturity**:
- **L/S ratio â‰¥2:1** = Mature lungs
- **Phosphatidylglycerol (PG) present** = Mature lungs

**Risks**: Miscarriage (0.5-1%), infection, Rh sensitization

### Fetal Monitoring

#### Non-Stress Test (NST)

**Purpose**: Assess fetal well-being

**Method**: Monitor FHR and fetal movement

**Timing**: Usually starts 28-32 weeks if high-risk

**Reactive (Normal)**:
- 2+ accelerations in 20 minutes
- Each acceleration: 15 bpm above baseline for 15 seconds
- **"15 Ã— 15 Ã— 2 in 20"**

**Non-Reactive**: Does not meet reactive criteria
- May indicate fetal compromise OR sleep
- Try to wake fetus (vibroacoustic stimulation)
- May need further testing (BPP, contraction stress test)

**NCLEX Tip**: Reactive NST = reassuring, no intervention needed

---

#### Biophysical Profile (BPP)

**Components** (score 0-2 each, max 10):
1. **NST** (reactive = 2)
2. **Fetal breathing movements** (30 seconds in 30 minutes)
3. **Fetal movement** (3+ movements in 30 minutes)
4. **Fetal tone** (extension/flexion)
5. **Amniotic fluid volume** (AFI >5 cm)

**Scoring**:
- **8-10**: Normal
- **6**: Equivocal, repeat in 24 hours
- **â‰¤4**: Abnormal, consider delivery

## ğŸ’Š Common Pregnancy Discomforts & Management

### First Trimester

#### Nausea/Vomiting (Morning Sickness)

**Non-Pharmacological**:
- Eat small, frequent meals
- Avoid empty stomach (crackers before rising)
- Avoid triggers (strong odors)
- Ginger, vitamin B6
- Adequate hydration

**Pharmacological**:
- Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)
- Doxylamine (Unisom)
- Ondansetron (Zofran) if severe

**Hyperemesis Gravidarum**: Severe, persistent vomiting
- Weight loss, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance
- May require IV fluids, hospitalization

---

### Second/Third Trimester

#### Heartburn/GERD

- Small, frequent meals
- Avoid spicy, fatty, acidic foods
- Remain upright after eating
- Sleep with head elevated
- Antacids (calcium carbonate - Tums) - safe

---

#### Constipation

- â†‘ Fiber, fluids
- Regular exercise
- Stool softeners (docusate) - safe
- Avoid mineral oil (â†“ fat-soluble vitamin absorption)

---

#### Back Pain

- Proper posture
- Pelvic tilt exercises
- Supportive shoes (avoid heels)
- Prenatal massage
- Warm (not hot) baths
- Maternity support belt

---

#### Leg Cramps

- Dorsiflexion of foot
- Adequate calcium, magnesium
- Hydration
- Avoid pointing toes

---

#### Varicose Veins & Hemorrhoids

- Elevate legs
- Support stockings
- Avoid prolonged standing
- Avoid crossing legs
- Sitz baths for hemorrhoids
- Topical creams for hemorrhoids

---

#### Urinary Frequency

- Normal due to uterine pressure on bladder
- Empty bladder regularly
- Kegel exercises (pelvic floor strengthening)
- Avoid caffeine
- **Report** pain, burning (UTI symptoms)

### Stage 1: First Stage of Labor

#### Latent Phase (0-3 cm)

**Characteristics**:
- Dilation: 0-3 cm
- Contractions: Irregular, 5-30 minutes apart, 15-30 seconds
- Duration: Primipara ~8 hours, Multipara ~5 hours

**Maternal Behavior**: Excited, talkative, anxious

**Nursing Care**:
- Assess maternal/fetal status
- Encourage ambulation
- Comfort measures
- Conserve energy (may be long)

---

#### Active Phase (4-7 cm)

**Characteristics**:
- Dilation: 4-7 cm
- Contractions: Regular, 3-5 minutes apart, 30-60 seconds
- Duration: Primipara ~5 hours, Multipara ~2 hours

**Maternal Behavior**: Serious, focused, less talkative

**Nursing Care**:
- Continuous monitoring
- Pain management
- Encourage position changes
- Support, encouragement

---

#### Transition Phase (8-10 cm)

**Characteristics**:
- Dilation: 8-10 cm
- Contractions: 2-3 minutes apart, 60-90 seconds, INTENSE
- Duration: 30 minutes to 2 hours

**Maternal Behavior**:
- Irritable, restless
- May say "I can't do this"
- Nausea, vomiting
- Rectal pressure, urge to push
- Trembling legs

**Nursing Care**:
- Stay with patient continuously
- Reassure this is almost over
- Encourage breathing techniques
- Do NOT push until complete dilation (cervical edema)
- Prepare for delivery

**NCLEX Tip**: Transition = shortest but most intense phase

---

### Stage 2: Delivery of Baby

**Begins**: Complete cervical dilation (10 cm)
**Ends**: Birth of baby

**Duration**:
- Primipara: Up to 2 hours
- Multipara: Up to 1 hour

**Maternal Behavior**:
- Renewed energy, focus
- Strong urge to bear down

**Nursing Care**:
- Coach pushing with contractions
- Monitor FHR continuously
- Prepare for delivery
- Support perineum
- Suction newborn after head delivered

**Pushing Technique**:
- Push with contractions
- Take deep breath, hold, push down
- Open glottis pushing preferred over prolonged Valsalva

---

### Stage 3: Delivery of Placenta

**Duration**: 5-30 minutes

**Signs of Placental Separation**:
- Gush of blood
- Lengthening of umbilical cord
- Uterus becomes globular, firm
- Uterus rises in abdomen

**Nursing Care**:
- DO NOT pull on cord
- Allow placenta to deliver spontaneously
- Examine placenta for completeness (prevent hemorrhage)
- Administer oxytocin (Pitocin) to prevent hemorrhage
- Check perineum for lacerations

**Placental Inspection**:
- Maternal side: Rough, dark red, divided into cotyledons
- Fetal side: Smooth, shiny, covered by amnion

## ğŸ” Fetal Heart Rate Monitoring

### Baseline FHR

**Normal**: 110-160 bpm

**Tachycardia**: >160 bpm
- Causes: Maternal fever, fetal infection, prematurity, medications

**Bradycardia**: <110 bpm
- Causes: Fetal hypoxia, heart block, post-term

---

### Variability

**Moderate (Normal)**: 6-25 bpm fluctuation
- Reassuring
- Indicates intact CNS

**Minimal**: <5 bpm
- May indicate hypoxia, sleep, medications (narcotics, magnesium sulfate)
- Non-reassuring

**Absent**: No fluctuation
- Ominous sign
- Immediate intervention

**Marked**: >25 bpm
- May indicate hypoxia

---

### Accelerations

**Definition**: Increase of 15 bpm for 15 seconds

**Significance**: Reassuring, indicates fetal well-being

---

### Decelerations

#### Early Decelerations

**Cause**: Head compression during contraction

**Pattern**: Mirror image of contraction (begins and ends with contraction)

**Significance**: **BENIGN** - no intervention needed

**NCLEX Tip**: "Early = Head = OK"

---

#### Variable Decelerations

**Cause**: Umbilical cord compression

**Pattern**: Abrupt drop, variable timing relative to contraction, V-shaped

**Significance**: Most common, usually benign if brief and FHR recovers

**Interventions**:
- Reposition patient (left side, knee-chest)
- Discontinue oxytocin if running
- Administer oxygen
- Vaginal exam (check for cord prolapse)

**NCLEX Tip**: "Variable = Cord = Change position"

---

#### Late Decelerations

**Cause**: Uteroplacental insufficiency, fetal hypoxia

### Postpartum Assessment

**Frequency**:
- First hour: Every 15 minutes
- Second hour: Every 30 minutes
- First 24 hours: Every 4-8 hours
- After 24 hours: Every 8-12 hours

**Use BUBBLE-LE** (see Stage 4 above)

---

### Uterine Assessment

**Normal Fundus**:
- Firm, midline
- At umbilicus immediately postpartum
- Descends ~1 cm (fingerbreadth) per day
- Below symphysis by day 10-14

**Fundal Height**:
- Immediately after delivery: At umbilicus
- Day 1: 1 cm below umbilicus
- Day 2: 2 cm below umbilicus (pattern continues)

**Boggy Uterus** (Uterine Atony):
- Soft, not contracted
- **Leading cause of postpartum hemorrhage**

**Intervention for Boggy Uterus**:
1. Massage fundus until firm
2. Empty bladder (full bladder prevents contraction)
3. Administer uterotonics (oxytocin, methergine, hemabate)
4. If continues â†’ Notify provider (may need manual exploration, surgery)

---

### Lochia

**Normal Progression**:

| Type | Color | Days | Amount |
|------|-------|------|--------|
| **Rubra** | Dark red | 1-3 | Moderate |
| **Serosa** | Pink-brown | 4-10 | Decreasing |
| **Alba** | Yellow-white | 10-14+ | Scant |

**Abnormal Lochia**:
- Bright red after rubra phase (hemorrhage)
- Large clots (>golf ball)
- Foul odor (infection)
- Excessive amount (>1 pad/hour)

**NCLEX Tip**: Lochia should DECREASE over time and progress rubra â†’ serosa â†’ alba

---

### Perineal Care

**Assessment**:
- **REEDA**: Redness, Edema, Ecchymosis, Discharge, Approximation

**Comfort Measures**:
- Ice pack first 24 hours
- Sitz bath after 24 hours (warm or cool)
- Peri-bottle after toileting
- Witch hazel pads (Tucks)
- Topical anesthetics (dermoplast spray)

**Teaching**:
- Front to back wiping
- Change pads frequently
- Report increased pain (hematoma)

---

### Breastfeeding Support

**Benefits**:
- Ideal nutrition for infant
- Antibodies, immunity
- Bonding
- Uterine involution (oxytocin release)
- Decreased postpartum bleeding
- Decreased maternal cancer risk

**Initial Breastfeeding**:
- Within first hour of birth (skin-to-skin)
- Teach proper latch
- Feed on demand (8-12 times/24 hours)

**Proper Latch**:
- Infant's mouth wide open
- Lips flanged outward
- Areola in mouth (not just nipple)
- Audible swallowing
- No pain (pain = poor latch)

**Engorgement**:
- Occurs days 2-4 (milk "comes in")
- Breasts hard, painful, warm
- Management: Frequent feeding, cold compresses after feeding, warm compress before feeding, massage

**Mastitis**:
- Breast infection (usually one breast)
- Red, warm, painful wedge-shaped area
- Flu-like symptoms, fever
- Treatment: Antibiotics, continue breastfeeding, heat, rest

---

### Emotional Changes

**"Baby Blues"** (50-80% of women):
- Onset: 2-3 days postpartum
- Duration: 2 weeks
- Symptoms: Mood swings, crying, anxiety, insomnia
- **Self-limiting**, resolves without treatment

**Postpartum Depression** (10-15%):
- Onset: Within first year, often weeks 4-6
- Duration: Persistent (>2 weeks)
- Symptoms: Depressed mood, anhedonia, guilt, thoughts of harm
- **Requires treatment** (therapy, medications)

**Postpartum Psychosis** (0.1-0.2%):
- Onset: First 2 weeks
- Symptoms: Hallucinations, delusions, thoughts of harming self/baby
- **PSYCHIATRIC EMERGENCY**

**Screening**: Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale

### Immediate Newborn Care

### Physical Assessment

#### Head

**Molding**: Overlapping skull bones (resolves in days)

**Caput Succedaneum**:
- Edema of scalp
- Crosses suture lines
- Resolves in days

**Cephalohematoma**:
- Bleeding between skull bone and periosteum
- Does NOT cross suture lines
- Resolves in weeks
- Risk: Hyperbilirubinemia (RBC breakdown)

**Fontanelles**:
- **Anterior**: Diamond-shaped, closes 12-18 months
- **Posterior**: Triangular, closes 2-3 months
- Should be soft, flat
- Bulging = increased ICP
- Sunken = dehydration

---

#### Eyes

- Slate gray or brown (final color by 6-12 months)
- Subconjunctival hemorrhage common (resolves)
- Vision: 8-12 inches (sees best at breast)
- Eye prophylaxis: Erythromycin ointment (prevent gonococcal conjunctivitis)

---

#### Skin

**Vernix Caseosa**: White, cheesy protective coating

**Lanugo**: Fine body hair (preterm infants)

**Milia**: Tiny white bumps on nose (blocked sebaceous glands, resolve)

**Erythema Toxicum**: Pink rash with white/yellow center (benign, resolves)

**Mongolian Spots**: Blue-gray pigmentation on sacrum, back (common in darker-skinned infants, fade over years)

**Acrocyanosis**: Blue hands/feet (normal first 24-48 hours due to immature circulation)

**NCLEX Tip**: Acrocyanosis of extremities is normal; central cyanosis is NOT

### Newborn Reflexes

| Reflex | How to Elicit | Normal Response | Disappears |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|------------|
| **Moro (Startle)** | Sudden loud noise or drop head back | Arms extend, then come together | 4-6 months |
| **Rooting** | Stroke cheek | Turns toward stimulus, opens mouth | 3-4 months |
| **Sucking** | Place object in mouth | Sucks | 2-4 months |
| **Grasp (Palmar)** | Place finger in palm | Grasps finger | 3-4 months |
| **Babinski** | Stroke lateral sole of foot | Toes fan out | 12 months |
| **Tonic Neck (Fencing)** | Turn head to side | Extends arm/leg on that side | 4-6 months |
| **Stepping** | Hold upright, feet touch surface | Stepping movements | 2 months |

**NCLEX Tip**: Absent reflexes may indicate neurological problem

---

### Thermoregulation

**Newborns at Risk for Heat Loss**:
- Large surface area relative to weight
- Thin skin, little subcutaneous fat
- Inability to shiver

**Mechanisms of Heat Loss**:
- **Evaporation**: Wet skin (dry immediately after birth!)
- **Conduction**: Contact with cold objects (warm blankets, skin-to-skin)
- **Convection**: Cold air currents (avoid drafts)
- **Radiation**: Heat transfer to cooler objects (keep away from cold windows/walls)

**Prevention**:
- Dry immediately
- Warm blankets
- Skin-to-skin contact
- Hat (head = large surface area)
- Delay first bath until temp stable (24 hours)

**Hypoglycemia Risk**: Cold stress increases glucose consumption

---

### Newborn Nutrition

**Breastfeeding**:
- Recommended exclusively for 6 months
- On demand, 8-12 feedings/24 hours
- 10-15 minutes per breast

**Formula Feeding**:
- Iron-fortified formula
- 2-3 oz every 3-4 hours initially
- Increase as grows

**Hunger Cues**:
- Rooting, sucking on hands
- Restlessness
- Crying (late cue)

**Adequate Intake Indicators**:
- 6-8 wet diapers/day after day 4
- 3-4 stools/day initially (varies after)
- Weight gain (regains birth weight by 2 weeks)

---

### Newborn Elimination

**Urine**:
- First void within 24 hours (document!)
- 6-8 wet diapers/day by day 4

**Stool**:
- **Meconium**: First 1-3 days, thick, black, tarry, sticky
- **Transitional**: Days 3-5, green-brown, looser
- **Breastfed**: Yellow, seedy, loose, frequent
- **Formula-fed**: Pale yellow-tan, formed, less frequent

---

### Newborn Medications

#### Vitamin K (Phytonadione)

**Purpose**: Prevent hemorrhagic disease of newborn

**Route**: IM, vastus lateralis

**Timing**: Within 1 hour of birth

**Rationale**: Newborns have sterile gut (vitamin K produced by gut bacteria), clotting factors low

---

#### Erythromycin Eye Ointment

**Purpose**: Prevent ophthalmia neonatorum (gonococcal/chlamydial conjunctivitis)

**Route**: Both eyes

**Timing**: Within 1 hour of birth (can delay to allow bonding)

**NCLEX Tip**: Required by law in most states

---

#### Hepatitis B Vaccine

**Timing**: Before hospital discharge (within 24 hours if mother HBsAg positive)

**Route**: IM, vastus lateralis

**Series**: 3 doses (birth, 1-2 months, 6-18 months)

---

### Newborn Screening

**Heel Stick Screening**:
- After 24 hours of life (needs protein intake)
- Screens for metabolic disorders:
  - Phenylketonuria (PKU)
  - Hypothyroidism
  - Galactosemia
  - Sickle cell disease
  - Many others (varies by state)

**Hearing Screen**: Before discharge (universal screening)

**Critical Congenital Heart Disease**: Pulse oximetry screening

---

### Circumcision Care

**Procedure**: Removal of foreskin

**Contraindications**: Hypospadias, epispadias (foreskin needed for repair)

**Post-Circumcision Care**:
- Monitor for bleeding first few hours
- Petroleum jelly gauze (Plastibell method: no gauze)
- Keep clean
- Yellow exudate normal (healing, not infection)
- First void after procedure (document)

**Teaching**:
- No tub baths until healed
- Report: Excessive bleeding, redness, swelling, no void in 6-8 hours

---

### Jaundice (Hyperbilirubinemia)

**Physiologic Jaundice**:
- Onset: After 24 hours (usually day 2-3)
- Peak: Day 3-5
- Resolves: 1-2 weeks
- Cause: Immature liver, increased RBC breakdown

**Pathologic Jaundice**:
- Onset: BEFORE 24 hours
- Causes: ABO/Rh incompatibility, infection, hemolysis

**Treatment**:
- **Phototherapy**: Converts bilirubin to water-soluble form
  - Eyes covered
  - Maximize skin exposure
  - Monitor temperature
  - Increase fluids
- **Exchange transfusion**: Severe cases

**NCLEX Key**: Jaundice in first 24 hours is ALWAYS pathological - requires evaluation

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A pregnant woman asks when she will feel the baby move. What is the best response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Most women feel fetal movement, called quickening, around 16-20 weeks, earlier if this isn't your first pregnancy."

**Rationale**: Quickening (first felt fetal movement) typically occurs 16-20 weeks for primigravidas, 14-16 weeks for multigravidas (experienced mothers recognize sooner). This is a presumptive sign of pregnancy. Movement should be felt regularly after 20 weeks. Decreased movement after feeling regular movement should be reported.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A laboring patient has contractions every 2 minutes lasting 90 seconds. She states "I can't do this anymore" and is trembling. What phase of labor is she likely in?</summary>

**Answer**: Transition phase (8-10 cm dilation).

**Rationale**: Transition characteristics: Most intense, frequent contractions (2-3 min apart, 60-90 sec), maternal irritability, statements of inability to cope, trembling, nausea, rectal pressure. This is the shortest but most intense phase. Nurse should stay with patient, provide support and reassurance that delivery is near. Check dilation before allowing pushing.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Which FHR pattern requires immediate intervention?</summary>

**Answer**: Late decelerations with minimal variability.

**Rationale**: Late decelerations indicate uteroplacental insufficiency and fetal hypoxia. Combined with minimal variability (indicates depressed CNS), this is ominous. Immediate interventions: Position left side, oxygen, discontinue oxytocin, IV fluids, notify provider, prepare for possible emergency delivery. Early decelerations are benign. Variable decelerations usually benign if FHR recovers.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Postpartum assessment reveals boggy uterus 2 cm above umbilicus, displaced to right. What is the priority nursing action?</summary>

**Answer**: Have patient void or catheterize to empty bladder.

**Rationale**: Full bladder prevents uterine contraction (causing boggy uterus) and displaces uterus from midline (usually to right). Priority is empty bladder, then reassess fundus. After bladder empty, massage fundus to firm. Boggy uterus = risk for postpartum hemorrhage. If remains boggy after bladder emptied and massage, administer uterotonics (oxytocin) per protocol.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: A mother is concerned about "soft spots" on her newborn's head. What is the best response?</summary>

**Answer**: "These are fontanelles, normal openings between skull bones that allow the brain to grow. The larger one closes around 12-18 months, the smaller one around 2-3 months."

**Rationale**: Fontanelles are normal, allow for brain growth and molding during birth. Anterior (diamond) closes 12-18 months, posterior (triangle) closes 2-3 months. Should be soft and flat. Bulging = increased ICP (meningitis, hydrocephalus). Sunken = dehydration. Teach parents these are normal and protective.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: A newborn's APGAR score is 8 at 1 minute (loses points for acrocyanosis and slow irregular respirations). What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Provide routine care, stimulation, dry infant, and reassess at 5 minutes.

**Rationale**: Score 7-10 is normal. Acrocyanosis (blue hands/feet) is normal in first 24-48 hours. Initial respirations may be irregular. Stimulation (drying, rubbing back) encourages respirations. Reassess at 5 minutes. Most newborns score 8-9 initially (lose points for acrocyanosis). Perfect score of 10 is rare. Score 4-6 needs oxygen/stimulation, 0-3 needs resuscitation.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: A newborn develops jaundice at 36 hours of life. What is the most likely cause?</summary>

**Answer**: Physiologic jaundice.

**Rationale**: Physiologic jaundice appears AFTER 24 hours (usually day 2-3), peaks day 3-5, resolves 1-2 weeks. Caused by immature liver and increased RBC breakdown. Jaundice BEFORE 24 hours is always pathological (ABO/Rh incompatibility, infection). Treatment: Phototherapy if bilirubin elevated, encourage frequent feeding (helps excrete bilirubin in stool).
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 8: Which maternal behavior indicates successful bonding with newborn?</summary>

**Answer**: Mother holds baby close, makes eye contact, talks to baby, and calls baby by name.

**Rationale**: Bonding behaviors (en face position): Eye contact, talking/cooing to baby, touching/stroking, calling by name, expressing positive feelings, responding to cues. Concerning behaviors: Lack of interest, negative comments, not touching, turning away, not responding to cries. Immediate postpartum period is critical for bonding. Skin-to-skin contact promotes bonding and breastfeeding.
</details>
