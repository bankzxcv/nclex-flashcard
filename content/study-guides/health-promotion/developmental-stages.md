# Developmental Stages Across the Lifespan

## Overview

Understanding human development from infancy through older adulthood is essential for NCLEX success. This guide covers major developmental theories (Erikson, Piaget, Freud, Kohlberg) and age-specific characteristics, helping you provide developmentally appropriate care and anticipatory guidance.

### Stage 1: Trust vs Mistrust (Infancy: 0-1 year)

**Developmental Task**: Develop trust in caregivers and environment

**Positive Resolution (TRUST)**:
- Consistent, responsive caregiving
- Basic needs met promptly
- Secure attachment
- Foundation for healthy relationships

**Negative Resolution (MISTRUST)**:
- Inconsistent or neglectful care
- Needs not met
- Insecurity, anxiety
- Difficulty trusting others

**Nursing Implications**:
```
âœ“ Encourage parent-infant bonding
âœ“ Support breastfeeding
âœ“ Teach responsive parenting
âœ“ Consistent caregivers in hospital
âœ“ Minimize separation from parents
âœ“ Assess attachment behaviors
âœ“ Screen for postpartum depression
```

**NCLEX Tip**: For hospitalized infants, assign same nurses consistently and encourage rooming-in with parents.

---

### Stage 2: Autonomy vs Shame and Doubt (Toddler: 1-3 years)

**Developmental Task**: Develop independence and self-control

**Positive Resolution (AUTONOMY)**:
- Sense of independence
- Ability to make choices
- Self-control
- Confidence

**Negative Resolution (SHAME/DOUBT)**:
- Over-control by caregivers
- Lack of confidence
- Self-doubt
- Excessive dependence

**Key Characteristics**:
- "No!" and "Me do it!" are normal
- Toilet training major milestone
- Exploring independence within safe limits
- Temper tantrums common

**Nursing Implications**:
```
âœ“ Offer limited choices ("Red cup or blue cup?")
âœ“ Allow self-care within abilities
âœ“ Praise attempts at independence
âœ“ Provide consistent routines
âœ“ Use positive reinforcement
âœ“ Teach parents about toilet training readiness
âœ— Avoid shaming for accidents
âœ— Don't force toilet training
```

**Toilet Training Readiness**:
- Stays dry for 2 hours
- Indicates need to go
- Can pull pants up/down
- Shows interest
- Age 18-24 months typically

### Stage 3: Initiative vs Guilt (Preschool: 3-6 years)

**Developmental Task**: Develop sense of purpose and ability to initiate activities

**Positive Resolution (INITIATIVE)**:
- Confidence in abilities
- Imaginative play
- Goal-directed behavior
- Leadership skills

**Negative Resolution (GUILT)**:
- Fear of punishment
- Excessive self-restraint
- Lack of initiative
- Follower rather than leader

**Key Characteristics**:
- Magical thinking
- Asks endless "Why?" questions
- Imaginative play
- May have imaginary friends
- Developing conscience

**Nursing Implications**:
```
âœ“ Encourage imaginative play
âœ“ Provide simple explanations
âœ“ Allow participation in care
âœ“ Use play therapy for procedures
âœ“ Praise initiative
âœ“ Set clear, consistent limits
âœ— Avoid making child feel guilty for curiosity
```

**Hospitalization Considerations**:
- Fear of bodily harm (use bandages)
- Separation anxiety decreases
- May regress under stress
- Use therapeutic play

### Stage 4: Industry vs Inferiority (School Age: 6-12 years)

**Developmental Task**: Develop competence and skills

**Positive Resolution (INDUSTRY)**:
- Sense of competence
- Pride in accomplishments
- Mastery of new skills
- Academic success

**Negative Resolution (INFERIORITY)**:
- Feelings of inadequacy
- Low self-esteem
- Lack of confidence
- Fear of failure

**Key Characteristics**:
- Learns rules and logic
- Peer relationships important
- Enjoys collections and hobbies
- Develops reading, math skills
- Begins team sports

**Nursing Implications**:

**NCLEX Key**: School-age children can understand cause-and-effect and follow complex instructions.

### Stage 5: Identity vs Role Confusion (Adolescence: 12-18 years)

**Developmental Task**: Develop sense of self and personal identity

**Positive Resolution (IDENTITY)**:
- Strong sense of self
- Clear values and beliefs
- Career direction
- Sexual identity

**Negative Resolution (ROLE CONFUSION)**:
- Unclear sense of self
- Difficulty with decisions
- Lack of direction
- Identity crisis

**Key Characteristics**:
- Abstract thinking develops
- Peer group extremely important
- Body image concerns
- Testing boundaries
- Independence vs dependence conflict

**Nursing Implications**:

### Stage 6: Intimacy vs Isolation (Young Adult: 18-40 years)

**Developmental Task**: Form intimate, loving relationships

**Positive Resolution (INTIMACY)**:
- Deep relationships
- Commitment
- Love and companionship
- Emotional connection

**Negative Resolution (ISOLATION)**:
- Loneliness
- Superficial relationships
- Fear of commitment
- Social isolation

**Key Characteristics**:
- Establishing career
- Marriage/partnerships
- Starting family
- Peak physical health

**Nursing Implications**:
- Assess relationship health
- Family planning education
- Career/life balance
- Preventive health focus

---

### Stage 7: Generativity vs Stagnation (Middle Adult: 40-65 years)

**Developmental Task**: Contribute to society and help next generation

**Positive Resolution (GENERATIVITY)**:
- Productivity
- Mentoring others
- Community involvement
- Legacy building

**Negative Resolution (STAGNATION)**:
- Self-absorption
- Lack of growth
- Disconnection
- Feeling unproductive

**Key Characteristics**:
- Peak earning years
- "Sandwich generation" (caring for children and aging parents)
- Midlife evaluation
- Physical changes begin

**Nursing Implications**:
- Health screening emphasis
- Chronic disease prevention
- Stress management
- Work-life balance

---

### Stage 8: Integrity vs Despair (Older Adult: 65+ years)

**Developmental Task**: Reflect on life and find meaning

**Positive Resolution (INTEGRITY)**:
- Life satisfaction
- Acceptance of death
- Wisdom
- Peace with life choices

**Negative Resolution (DESPAIR)**:
- Regret
- Fear of death
- Bitterness
- Feeling life was wasted

**Nursing Implications**:
- Encourage life review
- Support reminiscence
- Address end-of-life concerns
- Spiritual support

#### Concrete Operations Stage (7-11 years)

**Logical Thinking** (but only about concrete, tangible things):
- Can classify objects
- Understands cause and effect
- Can't yet think abstractly

**Conservation**:
- Understands amount stays same regardless of shape
- Can reverse operations mentally

**Nursing Implications**:
- Use concrete examples and demonstrations
- "Show and tell" approach
- Can understand body diagrams
- Explain procedures step-by-step

---

#### Formal Operations Stage (11+ years to adult)

**Abstract Thinking**:
- Can think hypothetically
- Understands abstract concepts
- Problem-solving abilities
- Can consider multiple perspectives

- **Imaginary audience**: Believes everyone is watching them
- **Personal fable**: Believes they're unique, invincible ("It won't happen to me")

**Nursing Implications**:
- Discuss long-term consequences
- Involve in complex care decisions
- Explain pathophysiology
- Respect privacy (imaginary audience)

## âš–ï¸ Kohlberg's Moral Development Theory

### 3 Levels of Moral Reasoning

Lawrence Kohlberg described how moral reasoning develops.

#### Level 1: Preconventional (Birth - 9 years)

**Stage 1: Punishment-Obedience**
- Right = Avoiding punishment
- "I'll be good so I don't get in trouble"

**Stage 2: Instrumental-Relativist**
- Right = Personal benefit
- "I'll share so you'll share with me"

**NCLEX Application**: Young children need external controls (rules, consequences)

---

#### Level 2: Conventional (9-20 years)

**Stage 3: Good Boy-Nice Girl**
- Right = Pleasing others
- "I want others to think I'm good"

**Stage 4: Law and Order**
- Right = Following rules and laws
- "Rules are rules, must be followed"

**NCLEX Application**: School-age and adolescents understand rules and social expectations

---

#### Level 3: Postconventional (20+ years, if at all)

**Stage 5: Social Contract**
- Right = Agreed-upon rights
- "Laws can be changed if unjust"

**Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principles**
- Right = Internal ethical principles
- "I follow my conscience"

**NCLEX Application**: Not everyone reaches this level; adults may function at conventional level

## ğŸ Nutrition Across Development

### Infant Nutrition

**0-6 months**:
- Breast milk or formula ONLY
- No water, juice, or solid foods
- Feed on demand (8-12 times/day newborn)

**6-12 months**:
- Continue breast milk/formula
- Introduce solid foods at 6 months
- Start with iron-fortified cereal
- One new food at a time (3-5 day intervals)
- No honey until 1 year (botulism risk)
- No cow's milk until 1 year

**Introduction Order**:
1. Iron-fortified cereal
2. Vegetables
3. Fruits
4. Meats

---

### Toddler Nutrition

**Characteristics**:
- Decreased appetite (slower growth)
- Food jags (wants same food repeatedly)
- Picky eating
- Ritualistic about food

**Recommendations**:
- Whole milk until age 2
- Limit milk to 16-24 oz/day (prevent iron-deficiency anemia)
- Finger foods, self-feeding
- Small portions
- No forced feeding

**Choking Hazards to AVOID**:
- Hot dogs, grapes (unless cut)
- Popcorn
- Nuts
- Hard candy
- Raw carrots

---

### Preschool/School Age Nutrition

**Preschool**:
- Appetite improves
- May still be picky
- Likes simple, familiar foods
- Help with food preparation

**School Age**:
- Steady growth requires balanced diet
- May skip breakfast
- Influence of peers, media
- Teach healthy choices

**Adolescent**:
- Rapid growth = increased caloric needs
- At risk for poor nutrition (fast food, fad diets)
- Females need iron (menstruation)
- Screen for eating disorders

## ğŸ“ Clinical Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Separation Anxiety

**Patient**: 10-month-old admitted for dehydration, crying when parents leave

**Assessment**: Normal separation anxiety (peaks 10-18 months)

**Interventions**:
- Encourage parent to room-in
- Consistent nursing staff
- Comfort items from home
- Maintain home routines when possible
- Allow transitional object (blanket, stuffed animal)

**NCLEX Key**: This is NORMAL, not pathological. Support parent-infant bond.

### Scenario 2: Toddler Refusing to Cooperate

**Patient**: 2-year-old refuses to take medicine, says "No!"

**Assessment**: Normal autonomy-seeking behavior

**Interventions**:
- Offer limited choices: "Medicine in red cup or blue cup?"
- Praise cooperation
- Use matter-of-fact approach
- Avoid power struggles
- Allow some control (can hold the cup)

**Don't**: Force, threaten, or shame

### Scenario 3: Preschooler Fears

**Patient**: 4-year-old says "I got sick because I didn't listen to mommy"

**Assessment**: Magical thinking - believes thoughts/actions caused illness

**Intervention**:
- Correct misconception immediately
- "You got sick from germs, not from being bad"
- Use concrete, simple language
- Provide reassurance
- Therapeutic play

**Patient**: 8-year-old having surgery, asks many questions

**Assessment**: Concrete operational thinking, industry stage

**Interventions**:
- Provide honest, concrete explanations
- Use diagrams, models
- Allow questions
- Involve in care

## âœ… Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: Which toy is most appropriate for a 6-month-old infant?</summary>

**Answer**: Soft, colorful toy that makes noise when shaken (rattle).

**Rationale**: 6-month-olds are in sensorimotor stage. They learn through senses - sight, sound, touch. Can grasp and bring objects to mouth. Toy should be safe to mouth, no small parts (choking), bright colors, interesting sounds. Can also transfer hand-to-hand at this age.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A 2-year-old is hospitalized. Which nursing action best supports development?</summary>

**Answer**: Offer choices such as "Do you want to wear the red shirt or blue shirt?"

**Rationale**: Toddlers are in autonomy vs shame/doubt stage. They need to assert independence and make choices. Hospitalization threatens autonomy. Offering LIMITED choices (2 options) gives sense of control without being overwhelming. Promotes autonomy and cooperation.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A 4-year-old is scheduled for surgery. Which explanation is best?</summary>

**Answer**: "The doctor will fix your tummy so it doesn't hurt anymore. You will go to sleep for a little while, then wake up and your parents will be there."

**Rationale**: Preschoolers are in preoperational stage - need simple, concrete, literal language. Avoid medical jargon and abstract concepts. Address magical thinking and fears (bodily injury, separation from parents). Reassure parents will be there. Keep explanations short and simple.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: An 8-year-old tells the nurse "I'm stupid because I can't do math like the other kids." This statement reflects which Erikson stage?</summary>

**Answer**: Industry vs Inferiority

**Rationale**: School-age children (6-12 years) are in industry vs inferiority stage. They develop competence through mastering skills. Failure or perceived failure leads to feelings of inferiority. Nurse should praise child's strengths, encourage effort, avoid comparisons to peers.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: Which statement by a 16-year-old indicates understanding of contraception teaching?</summary>

**Answer**: "I understand that condoms protect against STIs and pregnancy when used correctly."

**Rationale**: Adolescents are in formal operations stage - can understand abstract concepts and future consequences. Can comprehend complex health information. This response shows understanding of dual protection. Despite personal fable ("it won't happen to me"), education can be effective.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: A 9-month-old cries when the nurse approaches. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Ask the parent to hold the child during the assessment.

**Rationale**: 9-month-old experiencing normal stranger anxiety (develops 8-12 months). Child will be most comfortable with familiar caregiver. Having parent hold child during assessment reduces anxiety, supports trust, and facilitates exam. Trying to force interaction will increase distress.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: Which behavior is expected in a 3-year-old?</summary>

**Answer**: Plays alongside other children with similar toys (parallel play).

**Rationale**: Toddlers and young preschoolers engage in parallel play - play alongside but not interactively with peers. Cooperative play develops in later preschool years (4-5 years). This is normal developmental progression, not delayed social skills.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 8: A parent asks when to start toilet training. The child is 18 months old. What is the best response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Wait until your child shows signs of readiness such as staying dry for 2 hours and indicating the need to go."

**Rationale**: Toilet training requires physical and psychological readiness. Readiness signs: stays dry 2 hours, indicates need to go, can pull pants up/down, shows interest. Average readiness 18-24 months, but varies. Forcing before readiness causes shame and delays success. Autonomy stage - child must feel in control.
</details>

## ğŸ”‘ Quick Reference Summary

### Erikson's Stages (Most Important!)

| Age | Stage | Positive Outcome | Nursing Support |
|-----|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 0-1 | Trust vs Mistrust | Trust | Consistent care, meet needs |
| 1-3 | Autonomy vs Shame | Independence | Offer choices, praise attempts |
| 3-6 | Initiative vs Guilt | Purpose | Encourage play, answer questions |
| 6-12 | Industry vs Inferiority | Competence | Praise accomplishments, no comparisons |
| 12-18 | Identity vs Role Confusion | Sense of self | Respect privacy, allow independence |

### Piaget's Stages

- **Sensorimotor (0-2)**: Object permanence, sensory learning
- **Preoperational (2-7)**: Magical thinking, egocentric
- **Concrete (7-11)**: Logical about concrete things
- **Formal (11+)**: Abstract thinking

### Key Milestones

- **Sits**: 6 months
- **Stands**: 9 months
- **Walks**: 12 months
- **First word**: 12 months
- **2-word phrases**: 24 months
- **Toilet training readiness**: 18-24 months
