# Aging Process & Gerontological Nursing

## Overview

Understanding the normal aging process and age-related changes is essential for providing quality care to older adults. This topic is heavily tested on the NCLEX, requiring nurses to differentiate normal aging from pathological conditions and implement appropriate interventions for the geriatric population.

**Critical for NCLEX**: Know the differences!

| **Reversibility** | **REVERSIBLE** | Usually irreversible | **REVERSIBLE** |
| **Sleep-wake** | Disrupted | Fragmented | Early morning awakening |
| **Hallucinations** | Visual common | Less common | Rare |
| **Memory** | Immediate/recent impaired | Recent â†’ remote impaired | Selective, "I don't know" |

### Components

1. **Functional Assessment**
   - **ADLs** (Activities of Daily Living): Bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, feeding
   - **IADLs** (Instrumental ADLs): Shopping, cooking, housework, managing money, medications, transportation

   - Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE)
   - Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA)
   - Clock drawing test

   - Geriatric Depression Scale
   - Screen for depression, anxiety

   - Head-to-toe examination
   - Focus on sensory deficits
   - Gait and balance assessment

5. **Nutritional Assessment**
   - Weight history
   - Mini Nutritional Assessment
   - Dietary recall

6. **Social Assessment**
   - Living situation
   - Support systems
   - Safety concerns
   - Elder abuse screening

## ğŸ§  Cognitive Health

### Normal vs Abnormal Memory Changes

| Normal Aging | Alzheimer's Disease |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Forgets part of experience | Forgets entire experience |
| Finds misplaced items eventually | Unable to retrace steps |
| Sometimes forgets words | Frequent word-finding difficulty |
| Learns to use new devices with practice | Unable to learn new things |
| Makes occasional errors | Impaired judgment, poor decisions |
| Independent in ADLs | Requires assistance |

### Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI)

- Memory problems beyond normal aging
- Does NOT interfere significantly with daily function
- NOT dementia
- May progress to dementia (~15% per year)

## ğŸ  Elder Abuse

### Types of Abuse

1. **Physical**: Hitting, slapping, restraining
2. **Emotional/Psychological**: Threats, humiliation, isolation
3. **Sexual**: Non-consensual contact
4. **Financial**: Theft, misuse of money/property
5. **Neglect**: Failure to provide basic needs
6. **Abandonment**: Desertion by caregiver

### Warning Signs

**Physical Indicators**:
- Unexplained bruises, burns, fractures
- Poor hygiene
- Malnutrition, dehydration
- Overmedication or undermedication
- Pressure ulcers

**Behavioral Indicators**:
- Fearfulness, anxiety
- Withdrawal, depression
- Reluctance to speak in front of caregiver
- Conflicting accounts of injuries

## âœ… Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: An 80-year-old patient is suddenly confused and agitated. What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Assess for underlying causes (vital signs, oxygen saturation, recent medication changes, signs of infection).

**Rationale**: Acute confusion in elderly is delirium until proven otherwise. Delirium is a medical emergency with underlying physiological cause. Assess ABCs, vital signs, oxygenation first. Common causes include UTI, pneumonia, medication changes, electrolyte imbalances. NEVER assume confusion is "normal aging."
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A nurse is caring for a 75-year-old taking 9 medications. Which action is priority?</summary>

**Answer**: Perform complete medication reconciliation and assess for drug interactions and duplicate therapies.

**Rationale**: Polypharmacy (â‰¥5 medications) increases risk of adverse drug reactions and interactions. Elderly have decreased kidney/liver function, making them more susceptible to toxicity. Medication reconciliation can identify unnecessary medications, duplicates, and potential interactions. "Start low, go slow" principle applies.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Which statement by an elderly patient indicates understanding of fall prevention?</summary>

**Answer**: "I will turn on lights before getting up at night and sit on the edge of the bed before standing."

**Rationale**: Falls often occur at night due to orthostatic hypotension, poor lighting, and nocturia. Turning on lights prevents environmental hazards. Sitting before standing allows blood pressure to adjust, preventing orthostatic hypotension. Demonstrates understanding of intrinsic and extrinsic fall risk factors.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: A family member says "My mother has been very sad since my father died 6 months ago." What is the best response?</summary>

**Answer**: "Tell me more about her symptoms. Depression is not a normal part of aging and can be treated."

**Rationale**: While grief is normal, prolonged depression is NOT normal aging. Depression in elderly is often under-diagnosed and under-treated. Screen for depression, suicidal ideation, and functional impairment. Depression is treatable with therapy and/or medications. Never dismiss mood changes as "just old age."
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: An 82-year-old has bruises in various stages of healing on arms and torso. Adult daughter says "She bruises easily." What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Conduct thorough assessment and report suspected abuse to Adult Protective Services.

**Rationale**: Bruises in various stages of healing are red flag for physical abuse. Caregiver making excuses is suspicious. Nurses are MANDATORY reporters of suspected elder abuse - do not need proof, only suspicion. Report to APS immediately. Document objectively (measurements, locations, colors, patterns). Ensure patient safety.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 6: Which finding in an 85-year-old patient requires further evaluation?</summary>

**Answer**: Inability to recall recent conversation or events (short-term memory loss).

**Rationale**: Significant memory loss affecting daily function is NOT normal aging. May indicate dementia, delirium, or other pathology. Requires cognitive assessment (MMSE, MoCA). Normal aging may include slower processing or occasional word-finding difficulty, but NOT significant memory impairment affecting ADLs.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 7: A patient taking warfarin has an INR of 4.5. What should the nurse assess for?</summary>

**Answer**: Signs of bleeding (bruising, hematuria, bleeding gums, tarry stools).

**Rationale**: Therapeutic INR for warfarin is 2-3 (or 2.5-3.5 for mechanical heart valves). INR 4.5 indicates over-anticoagulation and bleeding risk. Elderly have increased sensitivity to warfarin due to decreased liver metabolism. Assess for signs of bleeding. Hold dose and notify provider. May need vitamin K reversal.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 8: Which intervention best prevents pneumonia in immobile elderly patient?</summary>

**Answer**: Turn, cough, deep breathe every 2 hours and encourage use of incentive spirometer.

**Rationale**: Immobility leads to hypostatic pneumonia due to secretion pooling. Elderly have decreased cough reflex and ciliary action. Turning prevents secretion accumulation. Deep breathing and incentive spirometry promote lung expansion. Also: elevate HOB, encourage mobility when possible, adequate hydration to thin secretions.
</details>
