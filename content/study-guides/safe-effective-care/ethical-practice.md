# Ethical Practice in Nursing

## Overview

Ethical practice is the foundation of professional nursing. Understanding ethical principles, theories, and frameworks is essential for navigating complex clinical situations and is heavily tested on the NCLEX.

## ğŸ¥ Common Ethical Dilemmas in Nursing

### Dilemma 1: End-of-Life Care

**Scenario**: Family wants "everything done" but patient has terminal cancer and DNR

**Ethical Conflict**:
- Autonomy (patient's wishes) vs. Family's wishes
- Non-maleficence (avoid suffering) vs. Beneficence (family thinks aggressive care is beneficial)

**Resolution Framework**:
1. Patient's advance directive takes priority (Autonomy)
2. Educate family about patient's wishes
3. Explain DNR doesn't mean "do nothing"
4. Offer palliative care and comfort measures
5. Provide emotional support to family
6. Ethics committee consultation if needed

---

### Dilemma 2: Substance Abuse in Pregnancy

**Scenario**: Pregnant patient admits to using heroin

**Ethical Conflict**:
- Patient's autonomy vs. Fetal well-being
- Non-maleficence (harm to fetus) vs. Confidentiality
- Justice (resources for mother) vs. Fetal rights

**Resolution Framework**:
1. Non-judgmental care for mother (patient is the patient)
2. Education about risks to fetus
3. Connect with addiction services, prenatal care
4. Mandatory reporting if state law requires
5. Focus on harm reduction
6. Support, not punishment

---

### Dilemma 3: Resource Allocation

**Scenario**: Pandemic - limited ventilators, must decide who gets one

**Ethical Conflict**:
- Justice (fair allocation) vs. Individual needs
- Beneficence (maximize lives saved) vs. Individual patient care
- Various allocation criteria in conflict

**Allocation Criteria** (vary by institution):
- Medical prognosis/likelihood of survival
- Organ system function
- Sequential (first come, first served)
- Random (lottery)
- Social worth (controversial - generally NOT used)

**Resolution**: Follow institutional protocol and triage criteria

---

### Dilemma 4: Impaired Colleague

**Scenario**: Nurse suspects coworker is diverting narcotics and working while impaired

**Ethical Conflict**:
- Loyalty to colleague vs. Patient safety
- Fidelity (to nursing profession) vs. Beneficence (help colleague)

**Resolution**:
1. **Patient safety is priority** - never ignore
2. Document specific observations (slurred speech, patients reporting ineffective pain medication)
3. Report to supervisor immediately
4. Follow institutional policy
5. May report to Board of Nursing
6. Helping colleague = getting them help, not covering up

**Remember**: Reporting impaired colleague IS advocacy - for patients and the colleague

## ğŸš¨ Ethical Issues in Special Situations

### Organ Donation & Transplantation

**Ethical Principles**:
- Justice: Fair allocation of scarce organs
- Autonomy: Donor consent
- Non-maleficence: Verify death before organ recovery
- Transparency: Clear allocation criteria

**UNOS Allocation Criteria**:
1. Medical urgency
2. Tissue match
3. Time on waiting list
4. Geographic location

**Nursing Role**:
- Support family during decision
- Separate organ donation discussion from death notification (different people)
- Never pressure family
- Maintain care of donor with respect

---

### Confidentiality vs. Duty to Warn

**Scenario**: Patient tells nurse he plans to harm specific person

**Tarasoff Principle**: Duty to warn identified victim may override confidentiality

**When Duty to Warn Applies**:
- Specific, credible threat
- Identifiable victim
- Imminent danger

**Action**:
1. Notify intended victim
2. Notify police
3. Document
4. Follow institutional protocol

**Balance**: Maintain confidentiality unless clear danger exists

---

### Genetic Testing & Privacy

**Ethical Issues**:
- Right NOT to know genetic information
- Disclosure to family members (can affect their health decisions)
- Insurance/employment discrimination
- Privacy of genetic information

**GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act)**:
- Protects against health insurance and employment discrimination
- Does NOT cover life insurance, disability insurance, long-term care

**Nursing Implications**:
- Genetic counseling before testing
- Ensure informed consent
- Protect genetic information
- Support patient's decision to test or not

---

### Cultural Practices vs. Medical Recommendations

**Scenario**: Family's cultural practices conflict with medical treatment

**Examples**:
- Hmong family wants to take placenta home (biohazard)
- Muslim family requests same-gender caregivers
- Jehovah's Witness refuses blood transfusion
- Traditional healers in hospital room

**Approach**:
1. **Cultural humility** - learn about patient's culture
2. **Explore accommodations** - can we honor both?
3. **Negotiate** - find middle ground when possible
4. **Respect** - when safe, honor cultural practices
5. **Ethics committee** - if significant conflict

**Balance**: Respect culture while maintaining safety

## ğŸ¯ Ethics Committee

### When to Consult Ethics Committee

- Conflict about treatment decisions
- Disagreement between patient/family/team
- End-of-life decision making
- Resource allocation issues
- Questions about decision-making capacity
- Cultural or religious conflicts
- Research ethics questions

### What Ethics Committee Does

**NOT a decision-making body** - provides:
- Ethical analysis of situation
- Education about ethical principles
- Mediation between parties
- Recommendations (not binding)
- Support for healthcare team

**Outcomes**:
- Clarify ethical issues
- Facilitate communication
- Identify options
- Support difficult decisions

## ğŸ’¡ NCLEX Success Tips

### Common Test Scenarios

**1. Patient Rights Questions**
- ALWAYS respect competent patient's decisions
- Patient autonomy is almost always the priority
- Don't be paternalistic ("we know what's best")

**2. Confidentiality Questions**
- Protect privacy unless legal exception (abuse, duty to warn)
- Don't share information with family without permission
- Report HIPAA violations

**3. Advocacy Questions**
- Speak up for patient's wishes
- Question inappropriate orders
- Use chain of command when needed

**4. Ethical Dilemma Questions**:
- Identify the ethical principles in conflict
- Patient safety/welfare always priority
- Consider all stakeholders
- Choose answer that respects autonomy while ensuring safety

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: A conscious patient refuses life-saving surgery. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Support the patient's decision and ensure they understand the consequences.

**Rationale**: Competent adults have the right to refuse any treatment, even life-saving treatment (Autonomy). Nurse should verify understanding, but must respect the decision. Continue to provide care and support.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: Nurse observes colleague taking narcotics from medication cart. What is the priority action?</summary>

**Answer**: Report immediately to supervisor.

**Rationale**: Patient safety is the priority (Non-maleficence). Impaired nurse endangers patients. Reporting is advocacy for both patients and the impaired colleague. Never ignore or cover up.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Family asks nurse not to tell patient their cancer diagnosis. What should nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Explain that the patient has the right to know their diagnosis and involve the physician in family discussion.

**Rationale**: Patient's right to information and autonomy. Withholding diagnosis violates patient rights. Nurse should advocate for patient and help family understand importance of truth-telling.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Which action best demonstrates the principle of justice?</summary>

**Answer**: Providing the same quality of care to all patients regardless of ability to pay.

**Rationale**: Justice = fairness and equality. All patients deserve quality care regardless of socioeconomic status, insurance, race, religion, lifestyle, or any other factor.
</details>
