# Informed Consent

## Overview

Informed consent is a legal and ethical requirement ensuring patients understand and voluntarily agree to treatments and procedures. Understanding informed consent is critical for NCLEX success and safe nursing practice.

---

## ğŸ“‹ Definition of Informed Consent

**Informed Consent**: Voluntary agreement to treatment or procedure after receiving and understanding complete information about:
- Nature of the procedure/treatment
- Risks and benefits
- Alternatives (including doing nothing)
- Expected outcomes
- Who will perform it

## ğŸ©º What Requires Informed Consent?

### Procedures Requiring Written Informed Consent

- Surgical procedures
- Anesthesia
- Invasive diagnostic tests (biopsy, endoscopy, cardiac catheterization)
- Blood transfusions
- Experimental treatments or research participation
- High-risk procedures
- Procedures with significant risks

### Nursing Procedures (May Require Consent)

**Usually IMPLIED consent**:
- Vital signs
- Oral medications
- Routine care

**May require EXPLICIT consent**:
- IV insertion
- Catheterization
- Injections (patient can refuse)
- Physical examination

**Good Practice**: Explain ALL procedures and obtain verbal permission

## ğŸš« When Consent is NOT Required

### Emergency Exception

**Criteria**:
1. Life-threatening emergency
2. Patient unable to consent
3. No authorized decision-maker available
4. Delay would cause serious harm/death

**Rationale**: Presumed consent - reasonable person would want life-saving treatment

**Example**: Unconscious patient brought to ED after car accident â†’ Emergency surgery proceeds without consent

---

### Implied Consent

**Definition**: Patient's actions imply consent

**Examples**:
- Patient rolls up sleeve for blood pressure
- Patient opens mouth for oral medication
- Patient extends arm for blood draw

**Note**: Patient can still refuse at any time

## ğŸ‘¤ Who Can Give Informed Consent?

### Adults (18+ years)

**Competent Adults**: Can consent for themselves

**Requirements**:
- 18 years or older (in most states)
- Mentally competent
- Not under influence of medications/substances affecting judgment

**General Rule**: Parent or legal guardian consents

### Special Situations

#### Married Person
- Can consent for self (even if under 18)
- Cannot consent for spouse

#### Unconscious/Incapacitated Patient
- Healthcare proxy/power of attorney
- Legal guardian
- Next of kin (order varies by state)
- Court order if no one available

#### Mentally Ill Patient
- Can consent if competent

#### Intoxicated Patient
- Cannot give valid consent if impaired
- Wait until sober (if not emergency)
- Emergency exception if life-threatening

### If Patient Has Questions or Doesn't Understand

**DO:**
1. âœ“ **STOP the process**
2. âœ“ Tell patient "I'll get the doctor to answer your questions"
3. âœ“ **Notify physician immediately**
4. âœ“ **Do NOT send patient to procedure**

- âœ— Answer medical questions about the procedure

### Witnessing Signature

**As a Witness, You Verify**:
- Patient's identity (check ID band)
- Patient signed voluntarily
- Patient was conscious and oriented
- Patient appeared to understand

**You DO NOT verify**:
- That patient understands all risks
- That all information was provided
- Medical accuracy of information

**Documentation**: Sign as witness with credentials and time

## ğŸ§  Decision-Making Capacity Assessment

### When to Assess Capacity

**Assess if patient**:
- Is confused or disoriented
- Has received sedating medications
- Has mental illness or dementia
- Is intoxicated
- Seems to have impaired judgment
- Is in severe pain or distress

---

### Four Components of Capacity

```
PATIENT DEMONSTRATES CAPACITY BY:

1. UNDERSTANDING
   Can patient explain what they were told?
   "Can you tell me what the doctor said about this procedure?"

2. APPRECIATION
   Does patient understand how it applies to them?
   "How do you think this will affect you?"

3. REASONING
   Can patient weigh risks vs benefits?
   "What makes you want to have/not have this done?"

4. EXPRESSION
   Can patient clearly communicate a choice?
   "What have you decided to do?"
```

**If Lacks Capacity**: Cannot provide valid consent; need authorized decision-maker

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: Patient scheduled for surgery says "What exactly are they going to do?" What should the nurse do FIRST?</summary>

**Answer**: Notify the surgeon that patient has questions about the procedure.

**Rationale**: Patient's question indicates lack of understanding. Physician is responsible for explaining the procedure. Nurse should STOP process and notify surgeon. Do NOT send patient to surgery until questions are answered.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: A 16-year-old pregnant teen needs a cesarean section. Who should sign the consent?</summary>

**Answer**: The pregnant teen can sign her own consent.

**Rationale**: Pregnant minors are considered emancipated in most states and can consent for their own care and their baby's care. Do NOT need parental consent.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: Nurse is about to witness consent when patient says "My family wants me to have this surgery." What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Speak with patient privately to ensure decision is voluntary.

**Rationale**: Consent must be voluntary. Patient's statement suggests possible coercion. Assess patient's own wishes privately. Patient may be signing to please family rather than own choice.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Patient received pre-op sedation. Surgeon brings consent form to sign. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Inform surgeon that consent should have been obtained before sedation.

**Rationale**: Sedating medication impairs decision-making capacity. Cannot obtain valid consent after sedation. Consent must be obtained BEFORE pre-op medications. Procedure may need to be delayed.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 5: Unconscious patient needs emergency surgery to stop internal bleeding. Family cannot be reached. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Surgery can proceed based on emergency exception to consent.

**Rationale**: Life-threatening emergency + unable to obtain consent + delay would cause serious harm = emergency exception applies. Presumed consent - reasonable person would want life-saving treatment.
</details>
