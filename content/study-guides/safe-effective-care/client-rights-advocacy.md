# Client Rights & Advocacy

## Overview

Understanding patient rights and the nurse's role as an advocate is fundamental to ethical nursing practice and essential for NCLEX success. Nurses are legally and ethically obligated to protect patient rights and serve as patient advocates.

## ğŸ›¡ï¸ HIPAA Privacy Rule

### Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

**Purpose**: Protect patient's private health information (PHI)

### What is Protected Health Information (PHI)?

**Any information that can identify a patient:**
- Name, address, phone number
- Social Security number, medical record number
- Dates (birth, admission, discharge, death)
- Photos, fingerprints, voice recordings
- Medical diagnoses, treatments, test results
- Insurance information

### Permitted Disclosures (No Authorization Needed)

| Situation | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Treatment** | Sharing info with healthcare team caring for patient |
| **Payment** | Billing information to insurance company |
| **Healthcare Operations** | Quality improvement, training, auditing |
| **Required by Law** | Court order, subpoena, mandatory reporting |
| **Public Health** | Reporting communicable diseases to health department |
| **Legal/Law Enforcement** | Reporting gunshot wounds, abuse, suspicious deaths |

### When Authorization IS Required

- Marketing purposes
- Releasing records to patient's employer
- Sharing with family/friends (unless emergency)
- Non-treatment purposes
- Patient requests copy of records

### When to Advocate

**Scenario Examples:**

| Situation | Advocacy Action |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Physician speaks only to family, ignoring competent patient | Redirect conversation to include patient; ask patient their wishes |
| Patient seems confused about procedure they're signing for | Stop consent process; notify physician patient needs more explanation |
| Family pressuring patient to agree to treatment patient doesn't want | Speak privately with patient; support patient's autonomous choice |
| Patient's cultural/religious needs being ignored | Educate team about patient's needs; ensure accommodations made |
| Patient afraid to "bother" doctor with questions | Encourage patient to ask questions; facilitate communication with physician |
| Unsafe staffing ratios putting patients at risk | Use chain of command; file incident report; document concerns |

## âš–ï¸ Right to Refuse Treatment

### Key Principles

**Competent adult patients have the ABSOLUTE right to refuse any treatment**, including:
- Medications
- Procedures
- Surgery
- Blood transfusions
- Life-saving treatment

### Requirements for Valid Refusal

```
PATIENT MUST:
  âœ“ Be competent/have decision-making capacity
  âœ“ Be informed of consequences of refusal
  âœ“ Make voluntary decision (no coercion)
```

### Nursing Actions When Patient Refuses

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Assess understanding**
   - "Can you tell me why you're refusing?"
   - "What do you understand about this treatment?"

2. **Ensure informed decision**
   - Verify patient understands consequences
   - Notify physician if patient needs more info
   - Don't assume refusal means lack of understanding

3. **Support the decision**
   - Respect patient's autonomy
   - Don't pressure, coerce, or guilt-trip
   - Continue to provide care

4. **Document thoroughly**
   - Patient's statement of refusal
   - Patient's understanding of consequences
   - Education provided
   - Physician notification
   - Patient's signature on refusal form

5. **Continue to offer**
   - Patient can change mind anytime
   - Offer treatment at later time
   - Maintain therapeutic relationship

---

### Special Situations

#### Jehovah's Witnesses & Blood Transfusions

**Beliefs**: Refuse blood transfusions based on religious beliefs

**Nursing Actions:**
- âœ“ Respect religious beliefs
- âœ“ Explore alternatives (volume expanders, erythropoietin, iron)
- âœ“ Use blood conservation strategies
- âœ“ Document refusal with witness
- âœ“ Never give blood against patient's wishes

**If Patient is Minor:**
- Parents can refuse for themselves
- Courts may intervene if child's life at risk
- Emergency: court order may authorize transfusion
- Ethics committee consultation

#### Against Medical Advice (AMA)

**Definition**: Patient leaves healthcare facility before medically recommended

**Nursing Actions:**
1. Notify physician immediately
2. Explain risks of leaving (document)
3. Try to persuade patient to stay (but don't coerce)
4. If patient still wants to leave:
   - Have patient sign AMA form
   - If refuses to sign, document with witness
   - Provide discharge instructions anyway
   - Arrange follow-up care if possible
   - Document entire situation

**Never**: Restrain patient, block exit, threaten patient

## ğŸ‘¤ Rights of Special Populations

### Mentally Ill Patients

**Rights Include:**
- Least restrictive environment
- Refuse psychotropic medications (if voluntary)
- Confidentiality
- Freedom from restraints/seclusion except emergency
- Participate in treatment planning
- Communicate with attorney

**Involuntary Commitment:**
- Must be danger to self or others, or gravely disabled
- Requires court order (except emergency hold)
- Patient retains most rights
- Can challenge commitment in court

---

### Incarcerated Patients

**Rights Include:**
- Receive necessary medical care
- Refuse treatment (same as any patient)
- Informed consent
- Confidentiality (with some exceptions)

**Limitations:**
- Security measures may be present
- Information may be shared with corrections officers if safety concern
- Some restrictions on visitors

**Nursing Considerations:**
- Treat with same respect as any patient
- Don't make assumptions or judgments
- Security personnel may need to be present
- Follow facility security protocols

---

### Pediatric Patients

**Rights:**
- Parents/guardians usually make decisions
- Child should be involved appropriately for age
- Assent (child's agreement) should be obtained
- Confidentiality (with limits for minors)

**Emancipated Minors** (can consent for themselves):
- Married
- In military
- Financially independent and living separately
- Pregnant or parent
- Court-declared emancipated

**Mature Minor Doctrine**: Some states allow mature adolescents to consent for certain care (STI treatment, contraception, substance abuse treatment, prenatal care)

---

### Non-English Speaking Patients

**Rights:**
- Receive information in language they understand
- Access to qualified medical interpreter
- All consent forms translated
- Family members should NOT interpret medical information

**Best Practices:**
- Use professional medical interpreters
- Telephone interpretation services
- Translated written materials
- Speak directly to patient, not interpreter
- Use teach-back method to verify understanding

**Reporting Process:**

2. Report to designated agency (CPS, APS, police, health dept)
3. Document factual observations (not opinions)
4. Do NOT need proof - report suspicions
5. Reporting in good faith protects from liability
6. Failure to report = legal/license consequences

---

### Child Abuse - Indicators

**Physical Abuse:**
- Bruises in unusual locations or patterns
- Burns (cigarette, rope, immersion patterns)
- Fractures inconsistent with history
- Injuries in various stages of healing

**Emotional Abuse:**
- Extreme behavioral changes
- Fear of caregiver
- Inappropriately adult or infantile behavior

**Sexual Abuse:**
- Genital trauma
- STIs in children
- Sexualized behavior inappropriate for age
- Fear of specific person or places

**Neglect:**
- Poor hygiene, malnutrition
- Untreated medical conditions
- Inadequate supervision
- Failure to thrive

---

### Elder Abuse - Indicators

**Physical Abuse:**
- Unexplained bruises, welts, burns
- Overmedication or undermedication
- Poor hygiene despite ability to pay for care

**Financial Abuse:**
- Sudden changes in bank accounts
- Unusual financial activity
- Missing belongings

**Emotional Abuse:**
- Withdrawal, depression
- Fearful around caregiver
- Helplessness, hopelessness

**Neglect:**
- Dehydration, malnutrition
- Pressure ulcers, poor hygiene
- Unsafe living conditions
- Untreated medical conditions

## ğŸ“ Practice Questions

<details>
<summary>Question 1: Nurse overhears nursing assistant discussing patient's HIV status in cafeteria. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Immediately stop the conversation and remind assistant of HIPAA confidentiality requirements. Report incident to supervisor.

**Rationale**: This is a HIPAA violation. PHI must not be discussed in public areas. Nurse has responsibility to intervene immediately and report. Nursing assistant may face disciplinary action.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 2: Patient refuses morning medications. What is the nurse's FIRST action?</summary>

**Answer**: Ask patient why they are refusing the medications.

**Rationale**: First assess the reason for refusal. Patient may have concerns, side effects, or misunderstanding. Ensure decision is informed. Patient has right to refuse, but nurse should assess understanding first.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 3: A competent adult patient wants to leave AMA. Security asks if they should lock the doors. What should the nurse say?</summary>

**Answer**: "No, the patient has the right to leave. I'll get the AMA form."

**Rationale**: Competent adults have the right to leave AMA. Cannot restrain or prevent them from leaving. Have patient sign AMA form, provide discharge instructions, and document.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Question 4: Nurse suspects a 4-year-old patient is being physically abused. What should the nurse do?</summary>

**Answer**: Report suspicions to Child Protective Services and document objective findings.

**Rationale**: Nurses are mandatory reporters. Must report ANY suspicion of child abuse - don't need proof. Document factual observations (not opinions). Reporting in good faith protects from liability.
</details>
